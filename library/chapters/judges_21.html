The first two chapters in the book of Judges review the situation at the close of Joshua's era and preview the coming cycles of success and failure in Israel's efforts to be God's exemplary nation. Details follow about several of the judges (Heb., shophetim, "leaders, defenders, judges"). Most of them were raised up by the Lord to supplement the priestly leaders in governing Israel, but some who were not raised up by the Lord are also covered. One of them was Jephthah, a somewhat tragic man whom the people themselves summoned to lead them. A different tragic case is Samson, who was called of God even before his birth but seems to have failed his divine calling. The book concludes with a few examples of the extremes of evil that developed during periods of Israel's deep apostasy when "every man did that which was right in his own eyes" (Judg. 17:6; 21:25). (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament, p.210)

The book of Ruth serves as an appendix to the book of Judges. It tells of some nobler events that "came to pass in the days when the judges ruled" (Ruth 1:1). It is reassuring to read about Israelites of that time who were good, even exemplary-showing faith, love, loyalty, generosity, and devotion to duty. Perhaps they represent many other Israelites of the time whose family history we do not have. (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament, 224.) RUTH The Book of Ruth offers a striking contrast to the Book of Judges, but its story is associated with the same period. In Judges, national sin and corruption portray a dark picture. The story of Ruth the Moabitess and her loyalty and devotion to Naomi, her Hebrew mother-in-law, presents the reader with a picture of the nobler side of Hebrew life in the days of the judges.

Naomi and her husband, Elimelech, and their two sons had gone into the enemy country of Moab because there was a great famine in Israel, their home. In due time their sons married Moabite wives, named Orpah and Ruth. Then in a ten-year period, the father and the two sons died. Naomi had heard that the famine had lifted in Judah, and she wanted to return to her people. She counsels her daughters-in-law to return to their mothers' homes. Naomi called them her daughters and kissed them, and they wept with their love for her. (Now isn't that quite remarkable? I fail to understand how this clear and well-known record hasn't had more influence on all the unfunny mother-in-law jokes that

abound in the world.) But eventually Orpah decides to stay in Moab, and again Naomi says to Ruth, "Behold, thy sister in law has gone back to her people, and unto her gods: return thou after thy sister in law." (Ruth 1:15.) At this point, in majestic Hebraic poetry, Ruth announces her decision and confirms her conversion. "Intreat me not to leave thee, or to return from following after thee: for whither thou goest, I will go; and where thou lodgest, I will lodge: thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God." (Ruth 1:16.) When Naomi, herself realistic and wise, saw Ruth's steadfastness, she "left off speaking to her" (see Ruth 1:18), which does not mean she stopped talking with her, but that she quit trying to convince her of the difficulties she would face in Israel. Ruth, the Moabitess, would face bigotry, poverty, and much insecurity, but she was converted, and she had decided. She and Naomi became a great team, facing together not only the problems before them, but the opportunities that would come also. In time, Ruth married Boaz, and a child was born to them. "And the women said to Naomi, Blessed be the Lord … for thy daughter in law, which loveth thee, which is better to thee than seven sons, hath born [a child]. "And Naomi took the child … and became nurse unto it. "And the women her neighbours gave it a name, saying, There is a son born to Naomi; and they called his name Obed: he is the father of Jesse, the father of David." (Ruth 4:14-17.) Now that was a kind of a prophecy which is very important to us. In a culture hostile to the leadership of women, these women-Naomi and Ruth-lived to bring about an end the scripture's writer carefully emphasizes: Obed, the father of Jesse, the father of David, through whose lines, which are carefully detailed for us in the first chapter of Matthew, came Jesus who is called Christ. Would you ever have expected the small book of Ruth to foretell such a great event? Ruth confidently met hardships not uncommon in our time-the death of a loved one, loneliness in a new place, and the need to work hard for her bread. Her small efforts, linked significantly to a later great event, tell me that each of us can take seriously the importance of our daily lives and decisions as we choose to follow God. (Aileen H. Clyde, "Confidence through Conversion," Ensign, Nov. 1992, 89)

The story of Ruth occurred during the period of the judges (about 1150 B.C.) but was not written in its present form until centuries later (about 500-400 B.C.). According to early Jewish tradition as recorded in the Talmud, the story was originally written by Samuel. In its present form, it is a compact, complete literary work. It has a plot, identifiable characters, suspense, and a moral. The plot shifts through six scenes: 1. Moab (1:1-18): Naomi, a native of Bethlehem, loses her husband and two sons before returning home with one of her daughters-in-law, Ruth. 2. Bethlehem (1:19-22): Naomi expresses deep sorrow for the emptiness of her life and the calamities which God has sent her. 3. The harvest field of Boaz (2:1-23): Ruth steps forward as the physical provider for the two women by following the Israelite custom of welfare for the poor. (See Lev. 19:9-10; Deut. 24:19-22.) Boaz recognizes her industry and grants her full harvest privileges. 4. The threshing floor of Boaz (3:1-18): Naomi instructs Ruth on how to test Boaz's intentions about marriage. Boaz is flattered and treats Ruth honorably. He accepts responsibility to clear the marriage from possible legal complications resulting from the patriarchal levirate law. (See Deut. 25:5-10; BD "Levirate Marriage.") 5. The city gates of Bethlehem (4:1-12): One other kinsman has first claim to the estate lands of Naomi and the attached moral responsibility to marry Ruth and provide her a family, but he rejects his claims. Boaz publicly claims the estate and proclaims his marriage intentions to Ruth. 6. Conclusion (4:13-17) and postscript (4:17-22): Ruth and Boaz have a son who brings joy to Naomi and who becomes an ancestor to King David (and Jesus). Throughout the story, Naomi and Ruth faced danger in the form of drought, death, and hunger. Finally, they rested secure in the home of Boaz. More importantly, their emotional and social welfare were rewarded through their posterity. Spiritual values were also gained when God's hand in the life of this

one family was made manifest. An Israelite and a Moabite had their faith rewarded and they became models for a righteous community and links in the noble heritage of David, Solomon, and Jesus. (See BD "Ruth.") (Victor L. Ludlow, Unlocking the Old Testament, p.75)

The book of Ruth, which Goethe described as "the loveliest complete work on a small scale," appears in the "Writings," the third division of the Hebrew Bible (which is divided into sections designated as "Law," "Prophets," and "Writings"). It is one of the five Megillot ("scrolls"), each of which is read during a Jewish feast or fast. Ruth is recited during Shavuot, or the Feast of Weeks. In the King James Version (as also in the Septuagint and Vulgate versions) the book appears immediately following the book of Judges, doubtless because the events which are outlined in the book occurred during the reign of the Judges. No indication of authorship is given in the book of Ruth itself. According to the Talmud (Baba Bathra 14b), the prophet Samuel wrote the book of Ruth (as well as the book of Judges and 1 Samuel). However, the genealogy at 4:18-22 is probably a later addition and postdates Samuel, whose life must have been roughly contemporary with the events described in Ruth; in addition, the description of the removal of the sandal in 4:7 as a custom from the remote past suggests a date substantially after the occurrences outlined in the book. Genealogy cited from Pharez (the son of Judah and an ancestor of Boaz) through Obed to King David Social Customs in the Book of Ruth The book of Ruth is rife with allusions to social customs and legal procedures observed at the time. An understanding of these customs will enhance an appreciation of the book. Among the most important of these are the references to gleaning in the fields (2:2-17), the redemption of property (4:3-7), and the remarriage of a childless widow. Gleaning. According to what must have been an ancient custom, codified in Leviticus and Deuteronomy, landowners were expected to leave the "corners" of the fields for the less fortunate members of the community to glean from. Thus in Lev. 23:22 we read: "And when ye reap the harvest of your land, thou shalt not make clean riddance of the corners of thy field when thou reapest, neither shalt thou gather any gleaning of thy harvest: thou shalt leave them unto the poor, and to the stranger" (cf. Lev. 19:9-10). Similarly, in Deut. 24:19 the Israelites were commanded: "When thou cuttest down thine harvest in thy field, and hast forgot a sheaf in the field, thou shalt not go again to fetch it: it shall be for the stranger, for the fatherless, and for the widow." Ruth, as a widow and a resident alien (Hebrew ger, rendered "stranger" in the KJV of Lev. 23:22 and Deut. 24:19), had a right to glean in the fields. It was a sign of Boaz' generosity (as well, perhaps, as a response to Ruth's request in 2:7: "I pray you, let me glean and gather after the reapers among the sheaves") that he told his workers to allow Ruth to glean throughout the field. That Ruth gathered an ephah (i.e., 29-50 pounds) of barley in one day attests to her success in gleaning. Redemption of Property and the Remarriage of a Childless Widow. At a pivotal point in Ruth, Boaz hailed someone called "Such a One" (Hebrew almoni peloni, something like "Mr. So and So") at the city gate and brought him before the elders of the city, who happened to be sitting there, and advised him of his opportunity to redeem the property of Naomi. At first "Such a One" seemed interested and expressed a willingness to purchase the property. At this juncture, however, Boaz informed him, "On the day that you purchase the field from Naomi, I will be acquiring Ruth the Moabitess, the wife of the deceased, in order to raise up a name for the deceased upon his inheritance" (4:5-my translation). This vital passage can best be understood in the light of the Israelite custom of redemption of property and the remarriage of a childless widow, practices which were designed to preserve property within the family and to perpetuate the memory of the deceased. The kinsman's right of redemption of property is outlined in codified form in Lev. 25:25-34. According to the custom in Israel, a kinsman had the right to redeem property that had been sold on account of poverty. The order of those having the right to redeem property may have been the same as the order for inheritance: brother, paternal uncle, and the "kinsman that is next to him of his family" (Num. 27:11). However, the situation in Ruth is somewhat different from the Leviticus legislation. In Lev. 25:25, because the property had already been sold, the stress was on the right of the kinsman to redeem it. In Ruth, the property of Naomi had not yet been sold, but the relative was given the right of first refusal. No obligation to do so is stated in Leviticus, nor is it implied in Ruth. Still, we must assume that this was the standard practice during the time the events recounted in Ruth took place, since, as D. R. G. Beattie pointed out, "a story-teller . . . will not create a legal situation which his audience will know to be impossible." The remarriage of a childless widow (codified in one particular form in the Pentateuch in legislation referred to as the "levirate law") is a practice mentioned in the twenty-fifth chapter of Deuteronomy:

If brethren dwell together, and one of them die, and have no child, the wife of the dead shall not marry without unto a stranger; her husband's brother shall go in unto her, and take her to him to wife, and perform the duty of an husband's brother unto her. And it shall be, that the firstborn which she beareth shall succeed in the name of his brother which is dead, that his name shall not be put out of Israel (Deut. 25:5-6). The situation in Ruth differs from the "levirate law" codified in Deuteronomy in two important respects: (1) Boaz was clearly not a brother-in-law (Hebrew yabam, Latin levir), and (2) there seems to have been no obligation for either Boaz or "Such a One" to marry Ruth, or it would certainly have come to their attention soon after the arrival of Naomi and Ruth from Moab. However, in one way-which is important for our appreciation of "Such a One's" reaction to Boaz' declaration of his intention to marry Ruth-the remarriage of a childless widow had the same effect as the "levirate": any offspring born to that union would have the right to inherit the property redeemed from Naomi. "Such a One's" initial willingness to redeem the land probably stemmed from his expectation that it would eventually pay for itself. However, the prospect of creating an heir to that property through Ruth, which heir might also be eligible to inherit the property that "Such a One" already owned and would otherwise have fallen to the sons he already had, made the purchase far less appealing. As a consequence, he encouraged Boaz to "redeem thou my right to thyself; for I cannot redeem it" (4:6b). Part of the storyteller's art lies in the suspense in which he kept his listeners for "Such a One's" reaction to Boaz' expression of intention to marry Ruth after "Such a One" had already said that he would redeem the property. Theology and Literary Characteristics The dramatis personae of the book of Ruth are unusual, if not unique, among the narrative writings of the Old Testament: in the entire story there is no king, no royalty, no priests, Levites, Judges, or prophets. All the characters in the story were ordinary people who lived in a town far removed from the centers of power, intrigue, and violence. A similarly striking feature of Ruth is its absence of traditional manifestations of Israelite religion: there is no evidence of the priesthood, nor is there a trace of oracles, visions, dreams, or any of the other usual means of determining God's will. Still, God's "all-causality" was felt continually as he moved in the background of events in the lives of everyday people who possessed the virtues of patience, loyalty, generosity, and trust. It brings the story to its successful and happy resolution. Related to the theme of God's "all-causality" is God's love, which extends to the lives and homes of the simplest of his people, as well as across national borders to traditional enemies of Israel. Ruth is repeatedly referred to as "the Moabitess" in the course of the story (1:22; 2:2, 6, 21; 4:5, 10). Yet, despite the fact that she was a foreigner, and despite the traditional antagonism between Israelites and Moabites (cf. Deut. 23:3-6), through God's love she was permitted to become a mother in Israel and to play an even larger role in God's design as the ancestress of David (4:22) and his greater Son Jesus (Matt. 1:1). Naomi left Bethlehem-Judah with a husband and two sons. When she returned to her home, she was widowed, bereft of both her sons; she brought only a Moabite daughter-in-law with her. Naomi lamented upon her return, "I went out full, and the Lord hath brought me home again empty" (Ruth 1:21). It seemed to Naomi that "the Almighty [had] dealt very bitterly" with her. Nevertheless God, in his never-flagging love, directed the subsequent events which led to the marriage of Ruth and Boaz and the birth of Obed, whom Naomi lovingly held in her bosom and for whom she acted as a nurse at the end of the story. Ruth is a masterpiece of the storytellers' art. With an absolute economy of words, without the use of a "villain" or any real antagonist, without the entrance of kings or priests onto the stage of the story, without the searing emotions or wrenching conflicts of which many stories are made, the teller of Ruth maintains the attention and interest of the listener from beginning to happy conclusion. As long as ordinary people who demonstrate the simple virtues of loyalty, love, and generosity are valued, the book of Ruth will remain a cherished part of our biblical heritage. (Stephen D. Ricks in Kent P. Jackson and Robert L. Millet, eds., Studies in Scripture, Vol. 3: Genesis to 2 Samuel, p.249-257)

The Book Of Ruth Introduction For me there are generally at least 3 levels of meaning in scripture. 1. Conveys a record of an event as it occurred with specific meanings for those specific people. 2. Contains more universal practical applications from the lessons taught in a particular book. 3. Prophetic. Many stories and events are types or foreshadows of future events. The book of Ruth is all 3 for me. I believe that Ruth, Boaz and Naomi as well as all the other characters mentioned are literal people who actually lived these experiences. I believe that there are universal moral and ethical implications in the book that are very deep and profound. I also believe that Boaz is a type of Christ (our kinsman redeemer), Naomi is a type of Israel (displaced from her land and inheritance but ultimately restored by Boaz). Ruth is a type of the church (She is a Gentile woman specifically excluded from the Abrahamic Covenant who is restored by becoming the bride of the kinsman redeemer). Historical Notes Up until about AD 450 the "Book of Ruth" was part of the "Book of Judges" in the OT when it was separated as a book. The period of the Judges is generally considered to be the time from Othniel until Samson. There is some argument on the exact dates the period is believed to be about 410 years and have ranged from about 1600 BC to about 1200 BC. This book was part of the time of the judges and most probably in the time of Gideon (see at Ruth 1:1). The fact that Ruth is a Moabite is essential to understanding the story so who exactly were the Moabites? The origin of both the Moabites and Ammonites is detailed in Genesis 19: 30-38 where Lot gets drunk and engages in sex with his all too willing daughters who both become pregnant. The animosity between the Israelites and the Moabites is throughout the OT. King David finally destroys that kingdom and takes great spoil from their country, which is ironic since through Ruth he is part Moabite. Deuteronomy 23: 3 has an interesting prophecy "23:3 An Ammonite or Moabite shall not enter into the congregation of the LORD; even to their tenth generation shall they not enter into the congregation of the LORD for ever:". Just how many generations existed between the sin of Lot's daughters and the marriage of Ruth and Boaz? Where a Moabite once again entered the congregation of the LORD through marriage? According to Genesis 11:27-31 Lot is Abraham's nephew so he is one generation removed from Abraham. If we turn to Matthew 1:1-5 we find 11 generations between Abraham and Boaz which makes 10 generations between Lot and Boaz when a Moabite woman was restored. Rahab the harlot is no doubt the same person as the Rachab mentioned in the genealogy of Jesus Christ, who married Salmon the tribe prince of Judah, to whom she bore Boaz, an ancestor of David (Matt 1:5). The genealogical proof of the descent of David from Perez through Boaz and the Moabitess Ruth (Ruth 4:18-22) forms not only the end, but also the starting-point, of the history contained in the book. Rahab was adopted into the congregation of Israel (Josh 6:25), and according to ancient tradition was married to Salmon (Matt 1:5), so the Moabitess Ruth was taken by Boaz as his wife, and incorporated in the family of Judah, from which Christ was to spring according to the flesh (see Matt 1:3,5) The story of Rahab is detailed in the book of Joshua Nothing more is known of Rahab. The accounts of the later Rabbis, such as that she was married to Joshua, or that she was the mother of eight prophets, and others of the same kind, are fables without the slightest historical foundation (see Lightfoot, hor. hebr. et talm. in Matt 1:5) When an Israelite is obliged to sell his inheritance on account of poverty, and actually did sell it, it was the duty of the nearest relation to redeem it as “goël”. But if it should not be redeemed, it came back, in the next year of jubilee, to its original owner or his heirs without compensation. Consequently no actual sale took place in our sense of the word, but simply a sale of the yearly produce till the year of jubilee (see Leviticus 25:10,13-16,24-28). When such a sale is made there is a sealed scroll written on the inside and out and sealed. Inside is the description of the title being transferred. On the outside is written the terms of the redemption that are to be performed by the one near of kin redeeming the property. We see

this acted out in Jeremiah chapter 32. Despite the fact that the Israelites are about to be led into captivity, Jeremiah redeems a piece of land and seals the scroll. They are identified as being from Bethlehem Ephrithah (see 1Chronicles 4:4 for more info on Ephrithah) as there were two locations known as Bethlehem. Ephrithah is specifically identified as the location of these people and the connection to the messianic prophecy in Micah 5:2 is hard not to notice. A famine exists in the land of Judah and Emilech takes his family into Moab. The sons of Emilech both take Moabites as wives but soon die and their father also leaving Naomi alone in the strange land. Naomi releases her daughters-in-law from any responsibility to her and decides to return home. One daughter, Ruth rejects that offer and insists on returning to Judah with Naomi. The Kinsmen Redeemer When Naomi and Ruth return they are greeted by the town that appear happy to see Naomi but she feels that she is under a judgment from God since her experience has been tragic. Ruth identifies Boaz as a near of kin of Naomi and gleans wheat in his field in the hope that she will find grace in his eyes (Ruth 2:1-2). Boaz uses his position to ensure the protection of Ruth (Ruth 2:8-18). As the barley harvest was ending and the threshing was to begin Ruth goes to lie at the feet of her benefactor Boaz. By asking him to place his skirt over her she is asking him to perform the duties of the kinsmen redeemer (goël). After one closer in kin steps aside he marries Ruth and redeems the land for Naomi. 2nd Interpretation: Practical Application A People Lost We are given the practical advice that when we are lost or in trouble it is our family that we should turn to for support. When the best-laid plans don’t work out we should be able to depend upon our family for help. The Kinsmen Redeemer If we find ourselves in the position of Boaz and able to help those in need we are to do it with grace and humility. If we find ourselves in the position of Ruth or Naomi we should not fear asking for the help we need. Denial of the request by someone near of kin should not cause us to resent that person. The request does not place a burden to perform upon the person being asked. It allows him to act as the kinsmen redeemer if he is able and willing to perform the task. Our opportunities to redeem one another are not limited to blood. Ruth was only kin to Boaz by law and all of mankind is kin to one another through Adam. 3rd Interpretation: Prophetic A People Lost Humanity as a people is lost in sin. We cannot save ourselves just as Naomi and Ruth were powerless to save themselves from their own situation. We are sojourning in a strange land because our inheritance is in heaven. Israel is also a nation lost. Through circumstances they were disbursed from their land and while in foreign lands she (Israel) suffered greatly leaving many dead sons in those countries. Even while Israel is dispersed among the nations God is raising up a bride for her redeemer (the church). Israel has started to return to the land but they have yet to have their former possessions restored. The church has been very influential in the return of the remnants of Israel to their land but it is Jesus who will make the restoration complete. The Gentile church is referred to in scripture as the branch that is grafted on. (Romans 11:17-24). Ruth is the beneficiary of a provision made by God for the widows and orphans in Jewish society even though she isn't Jewish. The church is the beneficiary of the extension of a provision made for Israel through grace just as Ruth was in this story. The entire passage is one of grace and provision made through the love of the redeemer. Ruth is

reminded several times not to glean in any other field. This is again similar to God's requirement that we only come to him for our provision. Love underpins everything Boaz does as a prophetic example of God’s love underpinning everything he does for us. The Kinsmen Redeemer Ruth approaches Boaz as the kinsmen redeemer (goël) of Naomi and asks him to redeem her also through marriage. The Abrahamic Covenant was made between God and the children of Abraham through Isaac. We as Gentiles had no covenantal right to redemption. Just as Ruth was solely dependant upon the grace of Boaz or her redemption we are solely dependant upon the grace of our LORD for our redemption. Our part in that redemption is simply to avail ourselves of that grace. The threshing floor typology here is more subtle then many others in the book and one needs to understand what "threshing" is and what it is associated with prophetically. Now threshing is performed generally by spreading the sheaves on the threshing-floor and causing oxen and cattle to tread repeatedly over them (Deuteronomy 25:4; Isaiah 28:28). On occasions flails or sticks were used for this purpose (Ruth 2:17; Isaiah 28:27). There was also a “threshing instrument” (Isaiah 41:15; Amos 1:3), which was drawn over the corn. It was called by the Hebrews a (moreg) threshing roller or sledge (2 Samuel 24:22; 1 Chronicles 21:23; Isaiah 3:15). It was somewhat like the Roman tribulum, or threshing instrument. When the grain was threshed, it was winnowed by being thrown up against the wind (Jeremiah 4:11), and afterwards tossed with wooden scoops (Isaiah 30:24). The shovel and the fan for winnowing are mentioned in Psalm 35:5, Job 21:18, Isaiah 17:13. The refuse of straw and chaff was burned (Isaiah 5:24). Freed from impurities, the grain was then laid up in granaries till used (Deuteronomy 28:8; Proverbs 3:10; Matthew 13:30; Luke 12:18). Now prophetically the threshing floor and winnowing of the wheat is connected to the coming judgment of the LORD. (See Dan 2:35, Matt 3:12) We see it explained in Matthew 13:24-30. "Another parable put he forth unto them, saying, The kingdom of heaven is likened unto a man which sowed good seed in his field. But while men slept, his enemy came and sowed tares among the wheat, and went his way. But when the blade was sprung up, and brought forth fruit, then appeared the tares also. So the servants of the householder came and said unto him, Sir, didst not thou sow good seed in thy field? From whence then hath it tares? He said unto them, An enemy hath done this. The servants said unto him, Wilt thou then that we go and gather them up? But he said, Nay; lest while ye gather up the tares, ye root up also the wheat with them. Let both grow together until the harvest: and in the time of harvest I will say to the reapers, Gather ye together first the tares, and bind them in bundles to burn them: but gather the wheat into my barn." If Ruth is a type of the Church then it appears from this passage that the Church will be safely at the feet of it's Lord during the threshing. The threshing floor also has an amazing prophetic destiny. When the chaff is separated from the wheat as described in the historical notes above it is done at an elevated location with a prevailing wind. The saddle of a hill or mountain would make a perfect threshing floor. Jebus was the name of the Canaanitish city, which stood on Mount Zion (Joshua 15:8; 18:16, 28). It is identified with Jerusalem (Judges 19:10). It is also identified with the castle or city of David (1 Chronicles 11:4,5). It was a place of great natural strength, and its capture was one of David’s most brilliant achievements (2 Samuel 5:8). Jebusites is the name of the original inhabitants of Jebus, mentioned frequently among the seven nations doomed to destruction (Genesis 10:16; 15:21; Exodus 3:8, 17; 13:5, etc.). At the time of the arrival of the Israelites in Palestine the Jebusites were ruled by Adonizedek (Joshua 10:1, 23). Joshua defeated them, and their king was slain; but they were not entirely driven out of Jebus till the time of David, who made it the capital of his kingdom instead of Hebron. The site on which the temple was afterwards built belonged to Araunah, a Jebusite, from whom it was purchased by David, who refused to accept it as a free gift (2 Samuel 24:16-25; 1 Chronicles 21:24, 25). The temple was built at Jerusalem, on Mount Moriah, in the threshing-floor of Arnon (also spelled Araunah,) the Jebusite; where Abraham had offered up Isaac. This is where David met the angel of the Lord when he came with his sword drawn in his hand to cut off the people of Jerusalem for the sin,

which David committed in his disorderly numbering of the people. Genesis 22:3, 4, 5; 1 Chronicles 21:15; 1 Chronicles 22:1; 2 Chronicles 3:1. Here we see that God went out of his way to purchase a specific threshing floor for his temple. It does not appear to me that the threshing floor of Boaz is at the same location as the threshing floor of Araunah because it is at least implied that Naomi returned to the area of Bethlehem. I do see it as a prophetic type of both the threshing floor used for the temple and the coming threshing floor of God that will be applied to the whole world. One part of the book that is normally misunderstood is what Ruth was doing at the feet of Boaz. “3:9 And he said, Who art thou? And she answered, I am Ruth thine handmaid: spread therefore thy skirt over thine handmaid; for thou art a near kinsman.” Many people interpret this as Ruth propositioning Boaz. That demonstrates a lack of understanding of Jewish culture. On the hem of the garment were the symbols of a tribe and any symbol of authority. Ruth is acknowledging his position and seeking his favor in redeeming her. She is asking him to play the role of kinsmen redeemer. Naomi however is not placed safely at the feet of her kinsman redeemer during this threshing period but once Boaz has confirmed his right to take Ruth as a wife he restores Naomi to her land. Paul discusses this restoration in Romans chapter 11. The 70th week of Daniel 9:27 represents the time of this threshing and we as the church will be safely situated at the feet of our redeemer while Israel will still be awaiting the conclusion of that week for our redeemer to restore them to their land. In the book of Ruth the title deed being redeemed is a piece of property formerly owned by Naomi’s husband. In Revelation 5:1 we find another scroll written on the inside and outside and sealed. It is the title deed of the Earth, which Jesus as one near of kin has redeemed. He is the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the root of David and he has prevailed to open the scroll (Rev 5:5) In four short chapters the Book of Ruth lays out prophetically the facts that the Gentiles may also share in the redemption of the LORD, we will obtain this redemption through grace and that Israel will be restored to its land. It is here where we get to see how the law of the kinsmen redeemer works, which is a law that allows us to be redeemed at all. (Bill Donahue, Bible Study)
