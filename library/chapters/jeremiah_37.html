This threatened invasion by the Egyptians, possibly at the request of Zedekiah (see verse 7), caused a temporary departure of the Chaldean army, but Jeremiah prophesied that they would return and destroy the city. Donaldson●●●●●

A careful examination of Jeremiah 37:3-39:7 gives the distinct impression that Jeremiah was cast into prison during the lull in the siege of Jerusalem-that is to say, while the Chaldean army was away for the purpose of facing an Egyptian force which was approaching. (See especially 37:5-15.) The siege lasted eighteen months, and the city must have fallen in 590 B.C.-that is, during the eleventh year of Zedekiah's reign (Jeremiah 30:1,2). It is not unreasonable to surmise, therefore, that Jeremiah had been cast into prison during the year 591 B.C., or during the early months of 590 (Book of Mormon chronology). Then we could say that Ishmael and his family must have joined Father Lehi's party at that time. Unfortunately, this logical method of dating Ishmael's arrival in Lehi's camp does not comport with other data in the Book of Mormon. In the first place, when Nephi was chiding his brothers during the return from Jerusalem, he seems not to have been aware that the city had been under siege (7:13). And in the second place, the Nephite party was in the wilderness a total of but eight years (17:4). In fact, they arrived in the Land Bountiful at the seashore about 593 B.C., which was some time before the siege of Jerusalem began. Consequently, we are forced to the conclusion either that something is wrong with the account of Jeremiah as it stands, or that Nephi was referring to an earlier imprisonment of Jeremiah of

which we know nothing-that is, one prior to 593 B.C. This fact shows us how, on occasion, the Nephite record may be used to check biblical data. It is obvious that further research on Jeremiah's imprisonment may prove of considerable interest and historical value. (Sidney B. Sperry, Book of Mormon Compendium, p.111)

Donaldson●○○○○

Throughout her whole history, Israel's prophets were tortured, mocked, scourged, imprisoned, stoned, sawn asunder, and slain. (Heb. 11:35-38.) For instance: Jeremiah was beaten and imprisoned (Jer. 37:15-21; 38:6); Zechariah the son of Jehoiada was stoned (2 Chron. 24:18-22); Urijah the son of Shemaiah was slain (Jer. 26:20-24), as were other prophets (Neh. 9:26); and according to tradition, Isaiah was sawn asunder. (Bruce R. Mc Conkie, Doctrinal New Testament Commentary, 3 vols., 1:, p.594)

Nowhere in the Bible are the children of Zedekiah enumerated, let alone named, although we are told that he had daughters as well as sons (Jer. 43:6; 52:10). He was twenty-one on his accession to the throne. Being a noble, he already had the economic resources to have possessed a wife and child(ren) at that time. After his accession, he took multiple wives in the manner of the kings of Judah before him (Jeremiah, in 38:22-23, refers to Zedekiah's "wives") so that when he was captured at age thirty-two, he might have had a considerable progeny. Robert F. Smith has mustered evidence that a son of Zedekiah with a name recalling Mulek may actually be referred to in the Bible. Jer. 38:6 in the King James translation speaks of Jeremiah's being cast into "the dungeon [literally, "pit"] of Malchiah the son of Hammelech." The last five words should be rendered, "Malkiyahu, the son of the king." This personal name could have been abbreviated to something like Mulek. Thus Jeremiah might have been put into "the [very] dungeon of[Mulek[?], the son of the king [Zedekiah]" referred to in the Hebrew text of Jer. 38:6. If Mulek was Zedekiah's eldest son, he could have been as old as fifteen at the time Jerusalem fell and as a prince may have had his own house, wherein there could have been a dungeon (Jer. 37:15-16 mentions one in a private house). (The "Mulekites" by John L. Sorenson Fn, BYU Studies, vol. 30 (1990), Number 3 - Summer 1990, p.7)
