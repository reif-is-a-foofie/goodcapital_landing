1-6. The Pashur in this passage is not to be confused with the Pashur of 21:1 or 38:1--the two men are of different lineages. The Lord set Jeremiah as prophet over the nations of the earth; and Pashur was appointed as overseer of the temple by an earthly tribunal. Now the earthly overseer asserted his power over the heavenly overseer. The Lord undoubtedly allowed Pashur to do this so he would bring judgments upon himself. One evidence of this is the Lord's personal prophecy against Pashur after he beat Jeremiah and placed him in the stocks overnight. Also, the Lord's changing of Pashur's name symbolizes the fate which he was to suffer. Pashur's new name, Magor-missabib, means "Fear round about" (King James Version of the Bible marginal note) or "Terror all around" (Anchor Bible): The judgments which the Lord pronounced upon Pashur through Jeremiah would bring fear or terror not only to him, but to all his family and friends. (Monte S. Nyman, The Words of Jeremiah, p.64)

14-18. This scripture is about one of the lowest points of Jeremiah's life. His depression might be better understood by reflecting upon a parallel situation related in the Book of Mormon: Mormon's mission among the destruction-bound Nephites. Writing of his circumstances, Mormon recorded: "a continual scene of wickedness and abominations has been before mine eyes ever since I have been sufficient to behold the ways of man" (Mormon 2:18; see also 5:8-9). Jeremiah's surroundings unquestionably paralleled those of Mormon's, for the conditions are always such in societies ripe for destruction. (Monte S. Nyman, The Words of Jeremiah, p.64)
