The Long Journey of Paul Lesson 38: Acts 21-28 Lee Donaldson, Editorial Consultant _ New Testament for Latter-day Saint Families_ Twice in the book of Acts the apostle Paul claims his rights or privileges as a Roman citizen. On both occasions his preaching ignited strong reactions against him and required the authorities to become involved. First, early in his missionary journeys in Philippi (which happened to be a Roman colony) he told the authorities that he was a Roman citizen (Acts 16:35-38) and again many years later during his final visit in Jerusalem (Acts 22:24-29). Paul's final claim on his Roman citizenship occurred after his third journey and while he attended the temple in Jerusalem. Some Jews recognized him and reported him to the temple leaders. The crowd erupted into a riot cursing and beating Paul. A Roman guard saved his life from the mob. The Roman captain Lysias arrested Paul and chained him. As they took him away, the Jews grew violent again. The Roman soldiers lifted Paul off his feet and carried him away. The Romans thought they had arrested an Egyptian who had led a riot earlier. Then the apostle pleaded, "I am a man which am a Jew of Tarsus, a city in Cilicia, a citizen of no mean city: and I beseech thee, suffer me to speak unto the people." (Acts 21:39) The Romans allowed Paul to speak. The apostle testified in Hebrew, but still, his Jewish audience called for his death. The Romans quickly took Paul away and Lysias ordered that Paul be flogged, but the apostle protested, "Is it lawful for you to scourge a man that is a Roman, and uncondemned?" (Acts 22:25) When the soldiers heard this, they rushed to Lysias and told him that Paul was a Roman citizen. The captain, fearful that he had illegally bound a Roman citizen, ordered the soldiers to take Paul away. Lysias convened the Sanhedrin to know the charges. However, during the trial the angered Jews again threatened Paul's life and Lysias quickly ordered his Roman troops to surround Paul and take him back to his cell. This is the last recorded apostolic witness in Jerusalem until nearly eighteen hundred years later when Orson Hyde dedicated Jerusalem. Paul had been warned by other disciples that going to Jerusalem was dangerous (Acts 21:4). Yet, Paul knew that he needed to return, so he responded, "for I am ready not to be bound only, but also to die at Jerusalem for the name of the Lord Jesus" (Acts 21:13). That night, with Paul's life hanging in the balance, the Lord appeared to Paul and said, "Be of good cheer, Paul: for as thou hast testified of me in Jerusalem, so must thou bear witness also at Rome" (Acts

23:11). Orson Hyde, who also risked his life to go to Jerusalem, testified "True it is, that in the most trying hour, the servants of God may then be permitted to see their Father, and elder brother. "But," says one, "I wish to see the Father, and the Saviour, and an angel now." Before you can see the Father, the Saviour, or an angel, you have to be brought into close places in order to enjoy this manifestation. The fact is, your very life must be suspended on a thread, as it were. If you want to see your Saviour, be willing to come to that point where no mortal arm can rescue, no earthly power save! When all other things fail, when everything else proves futile and fruitless, then perhaps your Saviour and your Redeemer may appear; his arm is not shortened that he cannot save, nor his ear heavy that he cannot hear; and when help on all sides appears to fail, my arm shall save, my power shall rescue, and you shall hear my voice, saith the Lord. (Journal of Discourses, Vol.1, p.125, October 6, 1853) Paul, the Roman citizen and Jewish Pharisee, had given his adult life to Christ.
