1THEN went Boaz up to the gate, and sat him down there: and, behold, the kinsman of whom Boaz spake came by; unto whom he said, Ho, such a one! turn aside, sit down here. And he turned aside, and sat down.
Donaldson Commentary

You will notice a reference to the gate here. At the gate of the city of Bethlehem, as was true of the

gates of those ancient cities, there was an enclosure without a roof. The elders of the city met within this enclosure to decide governmental matters that were related to the city or to adjudicate any individual problems that arose between the citizens within the city. Because Boaz wanted to get the matter settled as to whether or not he could be the husband of Ruth, he went up to this place that is referred to here as the gate. Then he watched for the one who was the nearer kinsman to Ruth than was he. He asked that one to come and sit down and discuss the matter. You will notice how he addressed the man: "Ho, such a one." That would be the same as our saying, "Hey, there," or "Hey, fellow; Hey,buddy." The point is no one knew what this nearer kinsman's name was. Perhaps it would be more accurate for me to say that the Bible does not record what his name was. It would likely be that Naomi would have known what his name was, perhaps even Boaz, but at any rate he does not call him by name. (Dr. Joe Temple, Ruth Study, livingbiblestudies.org)

Donaldson Commentary

The ritual of removing the shoes is called halitza (loosening). In the Bible, yibum (levirate marriage) requires that when a man who is married dies childless, his brother must marry his widow in order to "build his brother's house." If the brother-in-law of the widow willfully refuses this marriage, he formalizes that refusal through the ceremony of halitza. In some variations, when the shoe is removed and thrown to the ground, the widow spits on the shoe. The ceremony can be interpreted as a ritual of degradation since the brother-in-law has refused to perform his duty -- to have a child in his brother's name to preserve his brother's line. In effect, the ceremony releases the widow so she may marry whom she pleases since she was heretofore obligated to marry the brother. (Leslie Cook, Dawn R. Schuman Institute for Jewish Learning)

The first book of Samuel continues the narrative of the book of Judges (review the concluding paragraphs in the article on the book of Judges in this book). Although Judges demonstrates the growing wickedness among the Israelites, the short story of Ruth indicates that there were still noble, honorable people in the land. The book of Samuel introduces new models of righteousness (Hannah, Samuel, Jonathan, David, Nathan, etc.), but it also warns us of human weaknesses (Eli, Saul, David, Ammon, Absalom, etc.). Thus, it continues the themes of both Judges and Ruth. In fact, Samuel was probably the primary editor behind all three books. Samuel was the youthful servant of Eli, the high priest, and later became the prophet, seer, and judge of Israel. He bridged a crucial time period as Israel changed from a loose confederation of tribes under the leadership of elders and judges to a monarchy under the rule of Saul and then David. The lives of these three men, Samuel, Saul, and David, dominate the books of Samuel, which record the following events: 1 Samuel I. Life and administration of Samuel (1-12). A. Inspired promise, youth, prophetic call (1-3). B. Ark of the covenant and the Philistines (4-6). C. Samuel leads and teaches Israel (7). D. Desires for and dangers of a king (8). E. Saul to be a "prince over Israel" (9-10:16). F. Saul's inhibitions (10:17-27). G. Saul exhibits leadership (11). H. Samuel's last message and warning (12). II. Saul as king (13-31). A. Saul vacillates between strength and weakness (13-15). B. David's anointing, victory over Goliath, interactions with Saul's family (16-20). C. Saul seeks David's life (21-24). D. David's friends and refuge among the Philistines (25-27). E. Saul's discouragement, defeat, and death (28-31). 2 Samuel

I. The kingship of David (1-24). A. David consolidates rule and conquers Jerusalem (1-6). B. Promises to David and his family (7). C. David's strong rule (8-10). D. David and Bathsheba (11-12). E. David loses spirit of leadership (13-21, 24). 1. Family problems (13-18). 2. Political problems: Disobedience by generals and civil war (18-20). 3. Natural catastrophes: Famine and pestilence (21, 24). F. David's last words-a psalm and a prayer (22-23). A number of important lessons can be learned from these stories: the Lord answers prayers (of Hannah, David), the importance of religious leaders and their families in setting a good example (Eli and his sons, Samuel's sons), the calling of a young prophet (Samuel), having trust in the Lord (ark of the covenant and the Philistines), the dangers of centralized rule (Israel desires a king), the value of personal integrity and discipline (Saul's problems in his relationship with God), a young man grows in favor with the Lord (David), the evils of jealousy (Saul), trust in the Lord during periods of persecution (David), power and promises to the righteous (David), danger of evil thoughts and acts (David and Bathsheba), the frustrations of life without the Spirit of God (David), and the need to account for stewardships before death (Samuel and David). In other words, there is a wealth of stories, moral lessons, gospel themes, historical facts, and personality insights in these chapters. For the first time since the life of Moses, the Old Testament record has slowed down a little and given us more material covering just a short time period with enough facts to study characters, events, and feelings in greater depth. The books of Samuel are a record deserving careful study. The footnotes and topical guide references in the LDS edition of the Bible help clarify this material so that its richness can be appreciated. I and II SAMUEL These books were named after Samuel, not only because he is the principal figure in the first part, but also because he anointed the two other principal characters, Saul and David. Originally a single book which was divided when translated into Greek, the books of Samuel cover a period of time in Israel’s history from the birth of Samuel to the close of the reign of David. First Samuel presents the transition from Israel’s judges to the monarchy. Second Samuel deals almost exclusively with the history of David and presents a vivid picture of the theocratic monarchy in which the king represents God’s rule over the people.

Samuel: Prophet, Priest, Judge, and Anointer of Kings (1 Samuel) Prophet, Priest, and Judge The miraculous circumstances surrounding the birth and the call of Samuel make it clear that he had a divine calling before he entered mortality, and that he was chosen to play an important role in the history of Israel. The agonizing years of the barrenness of his mother Hannah, as well as the insensitive rivalry with Peninnah, are motifs that are reminiscent of other great women in the Bible who were, in time, to become mothers of great men: Sarah, Rebekah, the wife of Manoah, and Elizabeth, who were to become the mothers of Isaac, Jacob, Samson, and John the Baptist. These elements of the story serve as harbingers of Samuel's greatness even before he was born. Hannah took the grief of her childlessness to the Temple and there promised that if she were to be blessed with a son, she would dedicate him, all the days of his life, to the Lord (1 Sam. 1:9-11). Of course, every firstborn male belonged to the Lord, and in the case of animals they were to be given to him by sacrifice on the eighth day, while the firstborn of men were to be redeemed by an appropriate

offering (Ex. 13:2; 22:29-30; Deut. 15:19). Hannah vowed that Samuel would not be redeemed and that he would be "lent to the Lord," as long as he lived (1 Sam. 1:28). The language of Hannah's vow that "there shall no razor come upon his head" (1 Sam. 1:11) is very similar to the language of the Nazirite vow specified in Numbers 6 and taken by Samson in Judg. 13:5-7. It is almost certain (especially considering the Qumran and LXX texts of this passage discussed in the previous chapter) that she had in mind for Samuel a lifelong Nazirite vow. The Lord heard the prayer of Hannah and blessed her with a son whom she called Samuel, a name which means "Name of 'El (God)" or "his name is 'El." When she went to the temple at Shiloh to deliver her son to the Lord, by entrusting him to the ministry of the temple under the priest Eli, she prayed a prayer in the form of a song (or poem) of joyous thanksgiving and praise to the Lord (1 Sam. 2:1-10). This poem seems to be the model for the Magnificat-Mary's song of thanksgiving which she uttered after the conception of the Savior (Luke 1:46-55). This poem echoes themes of the omnipotence of God, his love for his children, and the justice that he metes out to them. It ends with the phrase "he shall give strength unto his king, and exalt the horn of his anointed" (1 Sam. 2:10), which is a clear allusion to the establishment of kingship in Israel in which Samuel was to play a part. Although the Hebrew word nabi' (prophet) is used previous to Samuel in reference to Abraham, Moses, Miriam, and Deborah, Jewish tradition, in a certain sense of the word, regards Samuel as the "first of the prophets." This tradition is expressed in New Testament times by Peter in his speech in the temple where he said that, "all the prophets from Samuel and those that follow after, as many as have spoken, have likewise foretold of these days" (Acts 3:24). This same statement was made by Jesus to the Nephites in 3 Ne. 20:24. Paul also alluded to Samuel as the first of the prophets, as well as the last of the judges, when he said that God gave the people of Israel judges "until Samuel the prophet" (Acts 13:20). It is not that the men and women of God before Samuel did not function as the mouthpieces of God, but that the term "prophet" was used after Samuel, and especially after the rise of the monarchy, to designate an office with a much more defined and specific function than before. It was Samuel who first functioned in this office and thus Samuel becomes the 'prophet par excellence.' An editorial note introduces the account of the call of Samuel: "And the word of the Lord was precious [or rare] in those days; there was no open vision" (1 Sam. 3:1). What was about to happen to Samuel was extraordinary. The narrative of Samuel's call is full of light and dark, sight and sound. As the lamp of God was flickering in the temple, Eli the aged priest, who was dim of sight, and young Samuel, who knew not the voice of the Lord, were lying down in their beds. Three times the silence was broken by the voice of the Lord as it called to Samuel. Eli could not hear it and Samuel, who could, did not recognize it as the voice of the Lord. Three times Samuel responded to the call by presenting himself to the priest, the Lord's administrator, thinking it was Eli who had called. The fourth time, following Eli's instructions, he recognized and answered the voice of the Lord, responding, "Speak; for thy servant heareth" (1 Sam. 3:10). Light came into the darkness and the Lord stood before Samuel and spoke to him. The Lord immediately explained the symbolism of Samuel's three fruitless visits to Eli. The chain of authority had been circumvented and changed. Because of the iniquity of Eli's sons, his house was destined for destruction. And just as the house of Eli was to decrease, Samuel was to increase. A new prophet had been called, and Samuel became the direct recipient and dispenser of the word of the Lord to Israel. In the morning the darkness came to light for Eli as Samuel recounted the judgment of the Lord against his house. Sorrowfully but with humility the priest accepted his fate as the "will of the Lord" (1 Sam. 3:18). The Lord continued with Samuel as he matured "and did let none of his words fall to the ground" (1 Sam. 3:19). Samuel enjoyed the presence of the Lord at Shiloh and all of Israel recognized him as the prophet of God (1 Sam. 3:20-21). The account of the call of Samuel is followed by a crisis in Israel and a partial fulfillment of the prophecy against the house of Eli. Chapters 4-6 record the great battle with the Philistines, the death of Eli's sons, and the capture of the Ark of the Covenant on the field of battle. Eli the priest of Shiloh died as a result of a fall caused by the news-not when he heard about the deaths of his own sons, but at the news of the loss of the Ark (1 Sam. 4:22). But the Lord preserved the Ark, and, after causing havoc in Philistia, it was returned to Israel. Samuel then stepped forward and revealed to Israel what they should have already known: the cause of

their oppression at the hands of the Philistines was their own wickedness. He called them to repentance and they responded, turning away from their false gods, and turning their hearts back to the true God. Then he invited the people to gather at Mizpeh, where he promised them that he would pray for them. In the narrative of the events at Mizpeh, Samuel is portrayed as functioning as prophet, judge, intercessor, and priest (1 Sam. 7:5-8). Just as Samuel is known as the "first prophet," he is also the last of the great judges in the Bible. The office of judge is well known from the book of Judges as a special leader called of God and imbued with the Spirit to call the people to repentance and to deliver them, usually in battle, from their enemies. In addition to his (or her) military leadership, these judges also apparently had judicial responsibilities-presumably judging cases and dispensing the appropriate justice or mercy; Samuel is said to have done this at Mizpeh, Bethel, and Gilgal, all of which he visited on an annual circuit (1 Sam. 7:16). Mizpeh, originally the place where Jacob made a covenant with Laban (Gen. 31:43-55), was also the place where Saul would be formally chosen as king (1 Sam. 10:17-25). It was a well-known gathering place for Israel in the times of the Judges, when all of Israel came together to fight against the Ammonites (Judg. 10:17-18) and to punish the Benjaminites for the rape and murder of the Levite's concubine (Judg. 20-21). Moreover, the circumstances of Israel at the time Samuel called them together were also very reminiscent of the cycle of behavior described in the period of the Judges (as well as from the Book of Mormon): apostasy, oppression, a cry for deliverance, the rise of a judge, repentance and deliverance, and then after a time a return to apostasy. The gathering at Mizpeh began with a libation of water, which seems to be a rite of purification of some sort, especially since it is done in conjunction with Israel's fasting and a confession of their sins. 1 Sam. 7:6 records that Samuel then "judged the children of Israel." This statement probably simply indicates that Samuel was functioning at Mizpeh in the same tradition known from the book of the Judges. It may, however, indicate a specific function of this office in the penitence of Israel, with Samuel dispensing justice for the transgressions of Israel and/or his intervention with God for mercy. The role of judge is also closely related to intercession; the people cried out to Samuel to take up their cause with the Lord that he might be merciful to them in the conflict with the approaching Philistines. Functioning as a priest, as he must have learned from his years of service in the temple, Samuel sacrificed to the Lord and cried out on behalf of his people-and the Lord heard his voice. As the smoke of the burnt offering rose to the heavens, the Philistines drew near to do battle with the Israelites. The Lord responded and intervened to preserve his penitent people with a divinely dispatched storm: "The Lord thundered with a great thunder on that day upon the Philistines, and discomfited them; and they were smitten before Israel" (1 Sam. 7:10). The scriptures further record that "the hand of the Lord was against the Philistines all the days of Samuel" (1 Sam. 7:13). The great power of Samuel as an intercessor is recalled in the Psalms, where he is regarded along with Moses and Aaron as one who was able to cry unto the Lord and be heard (Psalms 99:6), and by Jeremiah, who pointedly indicted Israel with the declaration of the Lord that "though Moses and Samuel stood before me, yet my mind could not be toward this people" (Jer. 15:1). Samuel and Saul Samuel, in spite of his righteousness and dedication to the Lord, suffered a tragedy shared by many great men since the beginning of the world-unrighteous offspring. This tragedy was responsible in part for the second sorrow of Samuel's life as well. After his corrupt sons had proven unworthy of their positions of judgeship in Israel, the elders came to Samuel and asked him to appoint over Israel, instead of judges, a king just like all of the other nations of the world. The Lord relented to the will of the people and the only words of consolation that he could offer Samuel were "they have not rejected thee, but they have rejected me" (1 Sam. 8:7). The establishment of the monarchy led to one of Samuel's most commonly known tasks as prophet and priest in Israel. It was the lot of Samuel, in spite of his opposition to the kingship and his stern warnings to the children of Israel, to seek out and anoint the first two kings of Israel-Saul and David. Saul, the Benjaminite and son of Kish, was sent by his father with a servant to locate their lost asses. When after considerable travel and searching the asses were not found, it was suggested that they seek

out a local "man of God" (none other than Samuel), of whom it was said "all that he saith cometh surely to pass" (1 Sam. 9:6). A well-known passage in 1 Sam. 9:9 gives another one of Samuel's titles as "seer," explaining that "seer" was simply the older term synonymous with "prophet." Of course, LDS readers are familiar with the Book of Mormon specification of this term as one who has the power to "know of things which are past, and also of things which are to come, and by them . . . shall secret things be made manifest, and hidden things shall come to light" (Mosiah 8:17), and also of its association with one who makes use of the Urim and Thummim (Mosiah 8:13-18; 28:10-16). It is possible, although there is no evidence, that Samuel had the Urim and Thummim that was used by the kings and priests of Israel. Nevertheless, his reputation as one who was able "to see" the things of the future in the incident of the lost asses is certainly in harmony with the definition of a seer found in Mosiah 8:17. In the meantime the Lord had advised Samuel of the coming of Saul, and had informed him that he was to anoint Saul as the "captain" (or prince) of Israel who was to have the function of the judges of old to deliver the people from the Philistines (1 Sam. 9:16). After the predicted meeting and the anointing had taken place, Samuel, as a sign and token of the divine origin and validity of Saul's call, told him where the lost asses could be found and gave a detailed description of the people that Saul was to meet on the road that day. Among these people was a group of men called a "company of prophets," who would be coming down from a high place playing musical instruments (1 Sam. 10:5). The source of the ecstatic behavior of the prophets was the Spirit of God, which came upon the group and caused them to prophesy. And this Spirit was 'contagious.' When Saul met the prophets and the Spirit of God fell upon them, it fell upon him as well. He began to prophesy with them, and as Samuel had predicted, he was "turned into another man" (1 Sam. 10:5-6, 10). This gave rise to the proverb in Israel "Is Saul also among the prophets?" (1 Sam. 10:11-12, 24). Saul later came in contact with this company of prophets once again: his messengers, sent to seize David, were detained three times because they ran into the prophets and, falling under the influence of the Spirit of God, began to prophesy with them. This passage also associates Samuel with this group and says that he was "appointed over them" (1 Sam. 19:19-20). When Saul went himself to find Samuel and David, he too fell under the influence of the Spirit of God, and the scriptures say that "he stripped off his clothes also, and prophesied before Samuel in like manner, and lay down naked all that day and all that night" (1 Sam. 19:24)-"another man" indeed! Ecstatic prophecy, although not necessarily characteristic of the northern prophetic traditions of Elijah, Elisha, and Micaiah or of the Classical prophets (from eighth-century Amos to fifth-century Malachi), appears to have been at one time in ancient Israel a normal manifestation of the Spirit of the Lord, and various elements of it survived throughout the prophetic history of Israel. In fact, the spiritual gift of tongues manifested in New Testament as well as modern times could be considered "ecstatic behavior." In addition to anointing the king and conducting the ceremony of his public acclamation, Samuel soon learned that his office of prophet included the necessity of monitoring and correcting the errant behavior of kings as well as of the children of Israel. One incident from the reign of Saul vividly illustrates the precedent that Samuel set for the future prophets of Israel to be the outspoken conscience of the public as well as the private acts of kings. Samuel knew that in a covenant society the unrighteous acts of a king brought the consequences of the judgment of God to the whole nation. Following the instructions of the Lord, Samuel commanded Saul to attack the Amalekites, who had incurred the wrath of God by harassing Israel in the years following the Exodus from Egypt. Samuel then laid down the strict rules of "holy war"-that all of the spoils should be "devoted" to the Lord by destruction (1 Sam. 15:1-4). The sole motivation for divinely ordained military operations must be obedience to the Lord, and must not become tainted with greed and self-interest, which inevitably lead to self-serving imperialism. After defeating the Amalekites, Saul blatantly ignored the strict regulations to kill the king and to destroy the spoils, giving in, as he said, to the cries of the people instead of the commandments of God (1 Sam. 15:24). Samuel, being informed of the transgression by the Lord, confronted Saul, "rebuked him sharply," and then rectified the situation by hacking Agag, the Amalekite king, to death with a sword before the Lord (1 Sam. 15:33). The episode ends with God's rejection of Saul and his house from the kingship. The Lord then sent Samuel to seek out another-David, son of

Jesse. Samuel diligently sought out and anointed young David, and although he would die before David actually became king, from that time forth "the Spirit of the Lord departed from Saul" and "came upon David" (1 Sam. 16:13, 14). Kings and Prophets In spite of the fact that the term "company of prophets" does not appear outside of the book of Samuel, this same tradition was probably carried on in the history of Israel by the groups or guilds of prophets known by various terms, such as "sons of the prophets" (1 Kgs. 20:35-41; 2 Kgs. 2:3-15; 4:1, 38-41; and 9:1-4), later in the Bible. Sometimes these groups worked in conjunction with, and at other times in opposition to, the great individual prophets of Israel. Just as in the case of Samuel, these groups of prophets played a significant role in the monarchy. The relationship of the prophet to the king demonstrated by Samuel and Saul continued throughout the history of Israel and is further complicated by the existence of competing prophets and groups of prophets. An illustration of this more complex situation is found in the days of Elijah, where Jezebel is said to have "cut off the prophets of the Lord." A certain Obadiah had preserved a hundred of them from death by secreting them in caves (1 Kgs. 18:3-4, 13). That these "prophets of the Lord" had once enjoyed an official consulting role under the monarchy is suggested by the fact that Jezebel had replaced them with the "prophets of Baal." The ecstatic excesses of the priests of Baal were shown by Elijah to be only a false imitation of the "Spirit of God." In contrast to the futile mechanistic attempts of the priests of Baal to rouse their god to action, Elijah called fire from the heavens with a simple prayer. One of the great lessons that Elijah learned from the vision on Mount Horeb was that the Lord was not in the strong wind, the earthquake or the fire, but in "a still small voice" (1 Kgs. 19:11). From this time on it seems that the prophets of the Lord, for the most part, tended to avoid ecstatic behavior, possibly because it too closely resembled the cults of the false prophets of Baal and Asherah. Kingship provides particularly good opportunities for false prophecy-misrepresenting the word of the Lord to gain favor with the king. And that the office of prophet was subject to abuse, both by individuals as well as groups of prophets, is well attested in the history of Israel. The story of Micaiah (1 Kgs. 22:1-28) beautifully illustrates the abuse of prophecy under the monarchy, where the king of Israel surrounded himself with 400 of his own so-called "prophets," who were no more than syncophants, essentially functioning as "yes-men" to his whims. They of course predicted glorious success for the joint battle plan of Israel and Judah to go to war against Ramoth-gilead of Syria. The perceptive, but vain, kings of Judah and Israel could tell that Micaiah (the true prophet of the Lord) was not being honest with them when he pretended to agree with the optimistic assessment of the "royal" prophets. Although the kings were sensible enough to finally get the true word of the Lord from Micaiah-spelling doom for the expedition-they were not wise enough to heed it. The history of Israel under the monarchy is full of similar phenomena-true and false prophets, and kings who often would rather listen to the desires of their own hearts, skillfully articulated by their own prophets, than to follow the true-although sometimes unpleasant-words of the Lord transmitted by his prophets. The salvation of Israel depended on the king and the people listening to and giving heed to the words of the prophets. Samuel faithfully fulfilled his role as prophet in the monarchy, but the seeds of the problems that eventually led to the destruction of Israel were already present in the reign of Saul. Even after his death Samuel played a part in the tragic story of Saul. While Samuel was alive the Lord had delivered Israel from the Philistines, but upon his death they once again posed a serious threat. As preparations were being made for a great battle, Saul "was afraid, and his heart greatly trembled" (1 Sam. 28:5). But when he sought the Lord, the heavens were silent. The voice of the Lord was not to be heard "neither by dreams, nor by Urim, nor by prophets" (1 Sam. 28:6). In desperation he went to a medium (the so-called Witch of Endor), which he himself had prohibited in Israel. He then asked for Samuel, the great intercessor, to find out what it was that he should do. As depicted in the biblical narrative, Samuel, just as to Eli, could only offer words of doom to Saul. He reminded him that because of his disobedience in the matter of the Amalekites, the Lord had rejected him and chosen David in his stead. Then he made his last prophecy-that Saul and his sons would be killed on the morrow, and that all

of Israel would feel the consequences of the wrath of God-to be delivered into the hands of the Philistines (1 Sam. 28:16-19). (David R. Seely in Kent P. Jackson and Robert L. Millet, eds., Studies in Scripture, Vol. 3: Genesis to 2 Samuel, p.271-280)
