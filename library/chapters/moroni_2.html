“What Moroni first recorded in the book carrying his own name were vignettes—a brief catalog, if you will—of things he felt needed to be recorded before he died and the Book of Mormon saga ended. These included the words of Christ to his twelve disciples when they were commissioned to bestow the Holy Ghost by the laying on of hands, the prayer by which priests and teachers were ordained, the sacramental prayers, and instructions as to how those who were baptized were to be received into the ‘church of Christ’ and numbered among the ‘people of Christ.’” (Jeffrey R. Holland, Christ And The New Covenant, p. 332-333)

1-3. God is the same yesterday today, and forever. Whenever there has been priesthood, keys, and servants of the Lord authorized to proclaim the everlasting gospel and administer in its ordinances, there also has been the Church- the earthly kingdom of God. Perhaps Moroni, in writing to his latter-day readers, is showing that the foundational principles upon which the Nephite Church was built are the same today and in all generations. These verses also testify that the Nephite Church organized by the Savior himself operated under the power and authority of the Melchizedek Priesthood. It is by this higher priesthood that the gift of the Holy Ghost can be conferred upon people who have entered the Church through proper baptism. In discussing the practices and principles that governed the Nephite Church, there is probably good reason why Moroni first records the disciples being empowered to confer the gift of the Holy Ghost. The Holy Ghost and its accompanying gifts are imperative to the operation of the priesthood and the work of the Church. "We believe in the gift of the Holy Ghost being enjoyed now, as much as it was in the Apostles' days," the Prophet Joseph declared. "We believe that it [the gift of the Holy Ghost] is necessary to make and to organize the Priesthood, that no man can be called to fill any office in the ministry without it; we also believe in prophecy, in tongues, in visions, and in revelations, in gifts, and in hearings; and that these things cannot be enjoyed without the gift of the Holy Ghost." (Teachings, p. 243.) (Joseph Fielding Mc Conkie and Robert L. Millet, Doctrinal Commentary on the Book of Mormon, 4 vols., 4:, p.323)

Holy Ghost In Greek holy is hagios most holy. It also is the same term that refers to members of the church (see Romans 1:7; 12:13). Ghost in Greek is pneuma which is the same word as the spirit that is inside of each of us (see Matthew 27:50; 1 Cor. 6:20). The Hebrew equivalent is Holy Spirit (see Psalms 51:11; Isaiah 63:11). Holy is qodesh apartness, holiness, sacredness, separateness; from the root vdq qadash meaning to consecrate, sanctify. Spirit in Hebrew is ruwach meaning the same as the Greek term for ghost.

lay (Heb. camak) to lean, lay, rest, support, put, uphold

hands (Heb. yad) hands; strength, power (fig.); sign, monument

apostles (Grk. apostolos) a delegate, messenger, one sent forth with orders. From the root apostello to order (one) to go to a place appointed. It comes from two words apw apo of separation and stello to set, place, set in order, arrange.

“While in every instance the Nephite twelve are spoken of as disciples, the fact remains that they had been endowed with divine authority to be special witnesses for Christ among their own people. Therefore, they were virtually apostles to the Nephite race, although their jurisdiction was, as revealed to Nephi, eventually to be subject to the authority and jurisdiction of Peter and the twelve chosen in Palestine (see Mormon 3:18-19). According to the definition prevailing in the world an apostle is a witness for Christ, or one who evangelizes a certain nation or people. ‘A zealous advocate of a doctrine or cause.’ Therefore the Nephite twelve became apostles, as special witnesses, just as did Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery in the Dispensation of the Fulness of Times.” (Joseph Fielding Smith, Answer to Gospel Questions, 1:122)
