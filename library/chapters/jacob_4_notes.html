Antenantiosis in the Book of Mormon Gail Call, one of our enthusiastic F.A.R.M.S. readers, has pointed out the presence in the Book of Mormon of an interesting figure of speech called antenantiosis. In this figure of speech, an expression is stated in terms of its negated opposite. The result is to express the positive in a very high degree, or as the biblical scholar E.W. Bullinger puts it, "We thus emphasize that which we seem to lessen" (Figures of Speech Used in the Bible [Baker Book House: Grand Rapids, 19891, pp. 159-64). For instance, when Jacob counsels to "despise not the revelations of God" (Jacob 4:8), he is not merely saying not to despise the revelations; he is actually urging the righteous to hold the revelations of God in the highest esteem.

Recently my missionary daughter requested help in understanding Jacob 5. Over the years I have had many students with the same request. In the early 1980s, the Foundation for Ancient Research and Mormon Studies (F.A.R.M.S.) called for research on interpreting and understanding this intricate allegory. A decade later they published their collected findings in _The Allegory of the Olive Tree_ . The number of pages in this book (622 pages) points out the difficulty of a single interpretation of Zenos's allegory. For example, Grant Underwood documents how most of the extant nineteenth century interpretations of Jacob 5 varied between historical understanding and homiletic application.

Both the historical and the personal interpretations of the allegory conform to the gospel plan. The spiritual history of Israel traces the decay and restoration of God's people. The spiritual history of the individual likens the allegory to the decays and restorations of our individual lives. The same scriptural story touches readers at different levels, but both understandings testify of the atoning power of reconciliation. Jacob 5, the longest chapter in the Book of Mormon, is one of the most challenging and intriguing chapters in the Nephite record. There is more commentary on this one chapter than any other chapter in the Book of Mormon. One could spend an enormous amount of time studying Jacob 5 and not exhaust its meaning.

Joseph Smith explained the way to understand parables and allegories: "I have a key by which I understand the scriptures. I enquire, what was the question which drew out the answer?" Jacob poses two key questions in his introduction to the allegory, which provide some clues to its meaning. First, Jacob asks: "Why not speak of the atonement of Christ, and attain to a perfect knowledge of him?" (Jacob 4:12). Jacob then points to the Jews' deliberate efforts to distance God and render him incomprehensible: they sought to create a God who could not be understood (Jacob 4:14). For their self-inflicted blindness God took away "his plainness from them . . . because they desired it" (Jacob 4:14).

Zenos Zenos is one of four Israelite prophets of Old Testament times cited in the book of Mormon whose writings appeared on the plates of brass but who are not mentioned in the Old Testament (see also Zenock; Neum; and Ezias). Zenos is quoted or mentioned by Nephi1 (1 Ne. 19:10-17), Jacob (Jacob 5:1-77; 6:1), Alma2 (Alma 33:3-11, 13, 15), Amulek (Alma 34:7), Nephi2 (Hel. 8:19-20), and Mormon (3 Ne. 10:14-17). Although specific dates and details of Zenos' life and ministry are not known, the Book of Mormon provides considerable information about him from his teachings and related facts. Evidently he lived sometime between 1600 and 600 B.C.

Thanksgiving Hymns of the Dead Sea Scrolls, noting certain striking similarities, suggests that the three may have been written by the same person. Further, the life situations of the author (or authors) are very similar (CWHN 7:276-83). Some LDS scholars anticipate that other evidences of Zenos' writings may appear as additional ancient manuscripts come to light. Book of Mormon prophets frequently quoted Zenos because of his plain and powerful testimony of the future life, mission, atonement, death, and resurrection of the Son of God.
