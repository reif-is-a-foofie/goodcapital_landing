Antenantiosis in the Book of Mormon Gail Call, one of our enthusiastic F.A.R.M.S. readers, has pointed out the presence in the Book of Mormon of an interesting figure of speech called antenantiosis. In this figure of speech, an expression is stated in terms of its negated opposite. The result is to express the positive in a very high degree, or as the biblical scholar E.W. Bullinger puts it, "We thus emphasize that which we seem to lessen" (Figures of Speech Used in the Bible [Baker Book House: Grand Rapids, 19891, pp. 159-64). For instance, when Jacob counsels to "despise not the revelations of God" (Jacob 4:8), he is not merely saying not to despise the revelations; he is actually urging the righteous to hold the revelations of God in the highest esteem. The unexpected negative increases the force of the idea that it apparently understates. It seems to make us notice and dwell on the expression, so that we can learn more from it. Thus in the promise, "if ye do this, and endure to the end, ye will in nowise be cast out" (Mormon 9:29), Moroni is actually promising that the faithful who endure will be blessed beyond measure. Recently my missionary daughter requested help in understanding Jacob 5. Over the years I have had many students with the same request. In the early 1980s, the Foundation for Ancient Research and Mormon Studies (F.A.R.M.S.) called for research on interpreting and understanding this intricate allegory. A decade later they published their collected findings in _The Allegory of the Olive Tree_ . The number of pages in this book (622 pages) points out the difficulty of a single interpretation of Zenos's allegory. For example, Grant Underwood documents how most of the extant nineteenth century interpretations of Jacob 5 varied between historical understanding and homiletic application. He concludes by writing, "As with any scripture, interpretation of Zenos's parable has ranged from the literal to the figurative" (_The Allegory of the Olive Tree_, 66). This conclusion that the text can be literal or figurative may be frustrating to many readers. I believe this frustration is born of a fundamental conflict between occidental (western) and oriental (eastern) thinking. This basic perceptual conflict may be illustrated by contrasting the English language (occidental) with the Hebrew language (oriental). The 1989 Oxford English Dictionary has entries for 615,100 words. There can be exactness in English that is not possible in any other language. On the other hand, the Old Testament is formed from approximately 1,000 basic Hebrew word stems. These Hebrew words can have multiple meanings and defy a single interpretation. For example, the word _qeren_ in Hebrew can be translated as horn, hill, or rays of light. It comes from the root word _qaran_ which means to send out rays or to display or grow horns. Michaelangelo, while reading this translation of Exodus 34, sculpted Moses with horns coming out of his head. Therefore, I believe that the allegory in Jacob 5 points out an important fundamental truth about scriptures - holy writ is not of single interpretation, but is written so we can be taught by the Spirit in many different ways. The scriptures are like a kaleidoscope which reveals different patterns and insights depending on our spiritual maturity and need. Each of these interpretations is valid as long as it brings us closer to Christ and helps us stay on the strait and narrow path outlined by His anointed servants.
