“The practice is believed to have started with George Hensley in the hills of Tennessee (Melton 1996, 636). By 1914 the practice had spread throughout the Church of God, however, the actual act of snake handling was only practiced by a small portion of the members (Melton 1996, 636).

16-31. What Do We Know Concerning Paul's Activities While He Was Imprisoned in Rome? Luke gives us little information respecting Paul's imprisonment in Rome. That Paul had been intending for some time to go there is certain (Acts 19:21 and Romans 1:9-11), though perhaps not as a political prisoner. That Paul was not inactive during the period of his confinement is more than a safe guess; it is a virtual certainty. Besides his native predisposition to hard work, Luke tells us that "Paul was suffered to dwell by himself with a soldier that kept him" (Acts 28:16) and that he "received all that came in unto him" and taught them of Jesus Christ, "with all confidence, no man forbidding him." (Acts 28:30-31.) It is generally recognized that Paul wrote at least four of his epistles during his first imprisonment, namely, Philippians, Colossians, Ephesians, and Philemon. That Paul entertained strong hopes of acquittal is evident from some of his letters. To the saints at Philippi he wrote: "I trust in the Lord that I also myself shall come shortly." (Philippians 2:24.) To Philemon he wrote, "But withal prepare me also a lodging: for I trust that through your prayers I shall be given unto you." (Philemon 22.) As for the trial itself, it would have to await the whim of Nero, most likely, so that much of Paul's time was taken up with waiting. (Life and Teachings of Jesus and His Apostles, p. 342)

1. Who wrote the Pauline epistles?

This may seem like a trivial question at first (like "who is buried in Grant's tomb?" which also isn't trivial), but it really is worth asking. You see, in the ancient world, there was no guaranteed way of ascertaining the authenticity of any written work. (For that matter, we can't always be certain today either.) For any given work that claimed to be the product of someone--let's say Paul--there were several possibilities:

The work was actually written by the famous Paul that everyone has heard of. The work was written by some other Paul, who has since been forgotten, and now everyone just assumes it was by the Paul they've heard of. The work was written by someone who used Paul's name to gain acceptance for his ideas. (Why would a person do this? Some reasons will be discussed below.) The work was written by someone who had no intention of passing off his work as Paul's, but since then someone else thought that the work looked like Paul's, so they attached Paul's name to it. The work was really written by Paul, but has since then been changed in some way--by the addition or subtraction of material, by rearrangement, or both. It is certain that letters falsely claiming to be by Paul (we call these "pseudonymous") did circulate. There is even a series of letters between Paul and the philosopher and Imperial advisor Seneca, the mere language of which is a giveaway that they were composed centuries after both lived. Thus, we must ask, of any given letter claiming Paul as an author, what the case really is: one of the above options, or some combination of them. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------2. 4. Why would someone write an "epistle of Paul"?

This is a good question--and one that is still hotly debated today. The first thing you might think is that this would be done in order to fill the church with heretical doctrines. Among the epistles that are currently in the New Testament, that does not seem to be the case--though some might have put teachings in Paul's mouth that he wouldn't have actually said (Paul had many differences of opinion with

other church leaders, but that doesn't necessarily that those differences of opinion are of any doctrinal importance).

So, if heresy is not the answer, what is? Many people find the idea that the writer of these epistles may be a forger to be very disconcerting, as if they shouldn't believe anything in them. However, as I said, no-one is saying the doctrine in them is not genuine. So what is possible to believe about the author of these letters that is compatible with their containing so much truth? I think there are three options.

Option 1: You can simply believe that the experts are wrong. Experts have certainly been wrong before. Option 2: You can believe that someone wrote the epistle under his own name, then someone came along later and said, "that doesn't sound like X, it sounds like Paul," and changed the name attached to it. This sort of thing is not at all difficult to imagine. Option 3: This option will take a little longer to explain. I'll give an example, below. Imagine that the Belmont Ward is a congregation in ancient Asia Minor. Right now the congregation is subject to severe persecution; perhaps because of the persecution, you have virtually no contact with any other church authorities. There are, of course, no phones, faxes, or e-mail, and letters arrive only very infrequently. In fact, let's say the last communication you received from any outside authority was the letter we just received from the First Presidency, telling us to focus more on spending time with our families (and also including a paragraph about reducing Sunday meetings). Say that it's been several years since then, and Bishop Bennett, after years of excellent leadership, has been fed to the lions as part of the persecution, which is still going on. The next thing you know, a group of members is advocating complete abolition of Sunday meetings, saying that "reduction of Sunday meetings" means to reduce them as far as possible, therefore they must be ended. You are sure that's not right, and argue in favor of continuing Sunday services. But soon, one of the members has produced a letter from the First Presidency that says that all Sunday meetings are to be discontinued forthwith. Now you're absolutely certain that letter can't be real. But what do you do?

If you said, "try to contact church leadership somehow," that's a great idea. But, practically speaking, that may be pretty hard, especially since they are in hiding because of the severe persecution. What do you do? Well, you might decide to forge a letter yourself in the name of the First Presidency, to try to put everyone back on track. I'm not sure that's the right choice, but it's certainly one that would cross the minds of many people.

By the way, this explanation seems almost certain to be analogous to the reason behind the composition of 2nd Thessalonians, if it is in fact pseudonymous. --------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5. What does a Pauline epistle look like?

I intend to be distributing outlines of the epistles as we go along, but, just as background, all the epistles tend to be built along a standard format, from which they vary to a greater or lesser degree. They go like this:

Opening matter. Salutation. Thanks for the congregation. Prayers. Discussion of the circumstances of the epistle. Answers to questions and advice. Closing matter. Prayers.

Farewell and good wishes to friends. You can see that the "meat" of the epistle is in the middle.

Just a few notes on variation: Prayers, in particular, can tend to be distributed widely throughout the poem; they often become a sort of thinly disguised advice ("I pray God you don't fall into the sin of X..."). Also, Paul tends to like to blend different parts of the letter and different themes in his message, so transition points are often not well marked. --------------------------------------------------------------------------------

6. Why is Paul so hard to read?

If you find Paul hard to read, it's not your fault. Paul was a practitioner of a rhetorical style known as the "Asiatic" style. Its primary characteristics were smoothness and floweriness. Thus, his transitions tend to be obscure where we would like them to be clear, and his primary points tend to be buried in a great mass of elaboration. This elaboration was a goal in itself, and need not serve the interests of the rhetorical thrust of the letter as a whole--thus, Paul can sometimes contradict himself. Those in his audience who were similarly schooled in rhetoric would have easily been able to understand Paul's message, while appreciating his rhetorical talents, but those who were not so schooled would have found him quite difficult, even in antiquity (note the assessment in 2 Pet. 3:15-16).

It is, of course, Paul's rhetorical skill that makes him still interesting to read today, but before we appreciate that, we must first get the main message. Let me give an illustration, which will actually be quite simple compared to some things Paul can do. Suppose I go to your house, and get a splinter while there. Later I write you a letter on the subject. Now, what is interesting about the splinter?

It is naturally part of the wood, not part of me. Now, since it is part of me, I am in pain. The splinter belongs inside the wood; now it is inside me. Before the splinter got inside me, it first was sticking out of the wood, where it didn't really belong anyway. So I write:

While I visited you, receiving your friendship, I received also something that tended very much against friendship, for a sliver, a natural part of a natural thing, was received by my body, a natural thing receiving another natural thing, by which receiving that natural thing became unnatural, for it is contrary to the nature of the body. What once was naturally inside a larger whole, a piece of wood, became first outside the whole, and then, unnaturally, inside my body. And while it was healthy first to be inside, when it became inside the second time, it tended greatly away from health, so that its removal, placing it again outside, was necessary for health to return. And thus inside, then outside, then inside and outside again, it became nothing more than a speck, to be discarded as wholly useless. See what I mean? If you pare away all the repetitions and oppositions I've introduced into that short paragraph, you see that it's still just about a splinter. I've made it more interesting, but not in a way that is really interpretively relevant. Paul very often did gain relevance by his elaborations, but not always, and, as I said, the first step is to discover the core of the message, before pursuing the many things that could be said about it. (Mike Tueller)

Who wrote this letter? Romans is generally considered to be a genuine letter of Paul. When was it written? Probably between 55 and 57 A.D. Paul was on his way to Jerusalem from Macedonia and Achaea (the capital of which is Corinth). We saw Paul's dealings with these two communities in 2nd Corinthians, so it is quite probable that this letter was written shortly after that one. Who were the Romans? Rome was the capital of the Roman empire, and the largest city in the known world. Please note that this epistle is written in Greek, not Latin, the language of Rome. This gives some

indication of the sort of people to whom Paul is writing. Note also that, of the people greeted in chapter 16, some have Latin names (e.g., Rufus, Junia, Julia), but most have Greek names. A Greek audience in Rome was almost certain to be an audience of either slaves or former slaves. More interesting is the question, "who converted these Christians?" While the traditional answer is that Peter made a successful missionary journey to Rome shortly before this letter, the best evidence is against this idea. The first missionary to Rome is unknown. However, Rome, in the typical way of cosmopolitan cities, had a constant influx of people from around the world, so it is not surprising that Christians would have made their way there. What is interesting about this letter? Notice that Paul is not answering any questions or trying to rectify any difficulties in this letter. This is because Paul is not in the same position with this letter as he is with any other letter (with the possible exception of Colossians): the Roman saints are not his congregation; he did not convert them. He is not writing this letter to follow up with them, but rather to get their help for a planned journey through Rome to Spain. Paul never made this journey. Instead, he was arrested in Jerusalem, spent several years in prison, and was finally transferred to Rome. This picture stands in contrast to Acts, where Rome was always his goal (Acts 19:21). Since Paul is not trying to rectify any doctrinal difficulties, why is this letter so full of doctrine? It seems that Paul was trying to recommend himself to the congregation by showing the sort of doctrine he preached. He hoped that by this means they would see what sort of a person he was, and would accept him and give him the help he needed. That makes this letter a great place to look for Paul's teaching as a whole: other letters give only the snippets that each congregation needed at the time, whereas this one lays out (at least the beginnings of) the whole gospel as Paul understood it. (Mike Tueller)

Purpose of the letter: "The epistle to the Romans is a letter, not a treatise on gospel subjects. It is not written to the world, but to the saints, to people who already know and understand the doctrines of salvation. Paul's comments on gospel subjects presuppose an extensive prior knowledge on the part of the readers. He does not here expound doctrines as such; he simply comments about them, leaving unsaid the volumes of gospel understanding already possessed by the saints. Romans, hence, is not a source of gospel knowledge for the spiritually untutored; it is not the initial place to turn to learn of Christ and his laws." (Bruce R. Mc Conkie, Doctrinal New Testament Commentary, Vol. II, Acts–Philippians, Bookcraft, 1970; p. 212.)

The church at Rome was composed of a mixture of Jewish and gentile converts. The Apostle Paul is aware that the different backgrounds of these two groups, prior to church membership, brought about conflicting views among them. His chief concern is with the claimed necessity and benefits of the law of Moses. Preoccupation with the centuries-old, legalistically annotated law had left a deep imprint in the lives of the Jewish converts to the church. The gentile converts were influenced by the Hellenistic philosophical system and concepts of learning. Their accompanying paganistic worship of idols was an additional deterrent to the proper understanding of gospel truths about which they were cautioned. (Rom. 1:20–25.)

Paul condemns the outward performances of the Jews (Rom. 2:23–24, 3:1) and reminds them that both Jew and gentile are sinful and in need of the gospel (Rom. 3:9, 23). He further stresses the individual's responsibility to do good and work righteousness as his covenant part of the principle of justification and also as the prerequisite to being justified by the Spirit. (Rom. 2:6–13.) The place of the atonement is next related to the individual's covenants as the means of making this status of justification possible. (Rom. 5, 6.)

Paul testifies that the law of Moses is a lesser law, inferior to the gospel of Jesus Christ (Rom. 7), and indicates that a justified individual is a person who has become righteous through obedience to the promptings of the Spirit of the Lord (Rom. 5:5, 8:5–6; see also Moses 6:60). These receive the promise of exaltation and of being joint heirs with Christ. (Rom. 8:14, 16–17.)

In addition, Paul comments about the doctrine of election, that is, that both Jew and gentile who had

accepted the gospel were called or were the foreordained of God. (Rom. 9:23–24.) Therefore, faithful individual response to the gospel message is the determining factor of one's status as an Israelite, not blood inheritance alone. (Rom. 9:6, 10:12.) He likewise warns the gentile members that faithfulness is necessary for them to maintain a worthy condition in the kingdom. (Rom. 11: 18–21.) "Brethren, I have written the more boldly unto you in some sort, as putting you in mind [i.e., to remind you]" (Rom. 15:15), writes the apostle, as he concludes his attempt to answer the conflict among convert parties within this branch of the early church.

Purposes of the atonement and the preeminence of the law of Christ in relation to the law of Moses are testified to. 2. Doctrine of Justification—Personal righteousness is emphasized as a prerequisite to justification and the other benefits of the atonement of Jesus Christ. 3. Doctrine of Joint Heirship with Christ—The covenant promise to all who achieve exaltation in the celestial kingdom is presented as a continuing challenge to church members striving to master gospel principles. 4. Doctrine of Election (Foreordination)—The epistle does not contain a comprehensive treatment of the doctrine, but rather points to the principle of the "election of grace" for those who are faithful in honoring gospel covenants. The place of the house of Israel and its relationship with the gentiles, particularly those within the covenant in terms of gospel blessings, are also discussed. The partial explanation of the allegory of the tame and wild olive trees is used to illustrate this doctrine. Special mention is made of the "grafting in" of the gentiles into the house of Israel. (Rom. 11:17.) (The entire allegory is found in the Book of Mormon, Jacob 5.)

In the first century after Christ, Rome was the center of the civilized world, with anywhere from one to four million inhabitants. Paul wrote to the Saints in Rome from Corinth, probably during the winter of A.D. 57-58. He wrote to prepare the members there for his visit and to correct false doctrines promulgated by Judaizers. Ten of the sixteen chapters address the relationship of the Mosaic law to the Christian gospel. The letter is heavily doctrinal, written to Saints who had already received the gift of the Holy Ghost and had been taught basic doctrines of the kingdom. The epistle to the Romans is the most comprehensive treatment of salvation in the New Testament, touching on the doctrines of justification, sanctification, reconciliation, election, making one's calling and election sure, foreordination, adoption, being joint-heirs with Christ, intercession, mediation, sealing by the Holy Spirit of Promise, and the Second Comforter (see Appendix 6, 404). The epistle to the Romans is, arguably, Paul's greatest theological treatise. Paul's writings, especially in this letter, are not always clear.
