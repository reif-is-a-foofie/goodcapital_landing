It may be helpful to review the story about the people of Ammon. They were Lamanites who had been taught the gospel by Ammon, Aaron, and others (see [CR, 75] Alma 23:1-4). When they accepted the gospel, these Lamanites were called the Anti-Nephi-Lehies and later called the people of Ammon (see Alma 23:16-17; 27:23-26). The sons of these people of Ammon were the army of Helaman, who helped fight the nonconverted Lamanites (see Alma 56:3-6). So the strength of the army of Helaman really began with their parents, who were the people of Ammon. They were the ones who first learned the gospel from the scriptures. They were the ones who learned about the power of prayer. And they were the ones who first made and kept covenants with the Lord. And just as it began with them, it begins with you. As first-generation members, you are the ones who begin the cycle of teaching and strengthening the next generation. (Vicki F. Matsumori, in Conference Report, Apr. 2007, 75; or Ensign, May 2007, 76)

These missionaries did not trifle with the Lamanites; they did not entertain them or seek by sophistry or by manipulation to bring people into the Church. They preached the gospel. The Anti-Nephi-Lehies" Generally, modern society has forgotten the ancient significance of names. However, Benjamin Blech Professor of Talmud at Yeshiva University, wrote recently, "It goes without saying that a name is the most distinctive thing about any human being. Our possessions leave us at death, but our names remain attached to us beyond the grave. It is, of course, possible that our unique 'calling card' is just a coincidence. Judaism, however, has decided otherwise. The Bible itself, in the Book of Samuel (1 Sam 25:25), makes it clear that. "As his name, so is he." A name describes an individual even more than it identifies a person. "How is that possible? Isn't a name given at birth before personality becomes manifest, before those who choose the child's name could possibly be aware of unique characteristics? Jewish tradition responds: The form of prophecy which stills exists to this day is the one granted to parents as they decide upon the name of their offspring. What seems like a decision based on whim, fancy, or personal preference is in fact a divinely sent message given expression through human medium. "The Midrash, a repository of Jewish wisdom, teaches that after death one of the questions every person will be asked is, "What is your name - and did you live up to its meaning?" Obviously, the reason God intervenes to this day in the naming process is to give each of us a sense of mission" (Jewish Book News, 5-6 August 5, 1999). This sense of mission or purpose in a name is underscored in the LDS Bible Dictionary. "The numerous passages of holy scripture in which reasons are given for bestowing a particular name on any person show that the Hebrews attached great importance to the meanings of their names" (The LDS Bible Dictionary , 736). It also states that "Biblical names especially are symbolic" (The LDS Bible Dictionary , 777). Even the Hebrew language connects names with the fundamental nature of a person's soul. The Hebrew word for name is shem and it forms the root of the Hebrew word for soul - the neshamah. Therefore, we can understand a great deal about the 'soul' or 'essence' of a scriptural character by his or her name.
