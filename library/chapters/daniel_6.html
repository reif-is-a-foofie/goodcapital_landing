In Daniel 6, we read of how jealous administrators attempted to frame and then to eliminate Daniel, who lived during and after the Babylonian exile of the Jews, by persuading the Persian King Darius (ca. 500 b.c.) to make it illegal for thirty days to pray to anyone other than the king himself. From this description it appears that a wad of clay (or perhaps wax) was pressed along a portion of the juncture of the stone cover and the mouth of the lions' den. This clay wad was then impressed with signets to certify the authority of the act and to indicate whether or not the stone cover remained intact. Although a document was not involved in this instance, the use of the signets of the king and his officials in this manner illustrates again how seals were used to indicate the authority of an action and render that act tamper-resistant. Da 7:1-28. VISION OF THE FOUR BEASTS.

Daniel 7 is another of the apocalyptic chapters. The sequence of episodes in the book of Daniel is not chronological, for this incident occurred before the Medo-Persian conquest. The events covered here are virtually the same as those in Nebuchadnezzar's dream, but they are represented with quite a different set of images (Dan. 7 and all fn.; cf. Dan. 2 and commentary). The curious beasts seem to signify some characteristics of the kingdoms of this world. The coming of the

kingdom of the Messiah, given only two verses in Daniel 2, occupies more than two-thirds of this chapter (Dan. 2:44-45; 7:9-28 and cross-references). The Aramaic portion of the book of Daniel ends here; the Hebrew is resumed in chapter 8. (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament, p.623)

1-28. What Was Represented by the Beasts Daniel Saw? Like chapter 2, chapter 7 gives a pictorial representation of history: There are four successive empires, and then the kingdom of God is established. Chapter 7, however, seems to relate more to the establishment of the ecclesiastical aspects of the kingdom, whereas chapter 2 deals more with the political aspects of the kingdom of God. The Prophet Joseph Smith taught the following about Daniel's vision of the beasts: "You there see that the beasts are spoken of to represent the kingdoms of the world, the inhabitants whereof were beastly and abominable characters; they were murderers, corrupt, carnivorous, and brutal in their dispositions. The lion, the bear, the leopard, and the ten-horned beast represented the kingdoms of the world, says Daniel. … "… The prophets do not declare that they saw a beast or beasts, but that they saw the image or figure of a beast. Daniel did not see an actual bear or a lion, but the images or figures of those beasts. The translation should have been rendered 'image' instead of 'beast,' in every instance where beasts are mentioned by the prophets. … When the prophets speak of seeing beasts in their visions, they mean that they saw the images, they being types to represent certain things. At the same time they received the interpretation as to what those images or types were designed to represent." (Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith, pp. 289, 291.) (Old Testament Student Manual: 1 Kings-Malachi, p. 304)

INTRODUCTION Modern Biblical scholars interpret the Bible according to their own predisposition, although objectivity is often claimed. These scholars can be placed on a continuum ranging from a completely allegorical approach to a literal view of the fundamentalist. The approaches are numerous including textural, historical, grammatical, literary, form, tradition, and redaction criticism. Theologians often forget that their own personal religious backgrounds often color their exegesis of a particular text. The movement today is toward a more historical-critical approach to biblical interpretation. President Joseph Fielding Smith declared that many of this so called, "higher criticism is in reality destructive criticism" (Man, His Origin and Destiny p.490). Nephi saw that the Bible would go through the hands of the great and abominable church in which they would pervert the right ways of the Lord, that they might blind the eyes and harden the hearts of the children of men (see I Nephi 13:27). The historicity of many biblical events and people, including the creation, Adam and Eve, the universal flood and the baptism of Christ have all come under question concerning their actual validity during this century. The great blessing that Latter-day Saints enjoy is in the belief of divine revelation. In November of 1831, the Lord revealed that those ordained to the priesthood "shall speak as they are moved upon by the Holy Ghost ... and whatsoever they shall speak when moved upon by the Holy Ghost shall be scripture" (D&C 68:3-4). This process of producing scripture in 1831 has been the same since Adam spoke as he was moved upon by the Holy Ghost which produced scripture and genealogy records (see Moses 6:8). Peter declared the same principle when he said, "Prophecy came not in old time by the will of man but holy men of God spake to they were moved by the Holy Ghost." (2 Peter 1:21) Therefore, when scripture is viewed from this perspective of divine revelation it takes on an entirely new meaning. For example, many scholars holding to the historical critical method of interpretation cannot accept the fact that Isaiah could have known of a man named Cyrus before he was born. They declare that this is proof that a later author, second-Isaiah must have written those sections in Isaiah where Cyrus is mentioned. A person who understands revelation can accept the fact that Isaiah knew of Cyrus. However, it must also be stated that L.D.S. scholars who believe in divine revelation can still gain much by the use of the above approaches to biblical criticism. For example, it is important to understand the historical context of a particular passage of scripture in order to gain the full meaning of the scripture in

question. A literary criticism of Alma 36 reveals the beauty of the chiastic pattern found therein. A study of Judaism will help in understanding certain Old and New Testament scriptures. The doctrine of divine revelation however, should overwhelmingly takes precedent in understanding biblical passages. An example of the differences between the historicalcritical approach and those who accept divine revelation for interpretation can be found within the Book of Daniel. Roman Catholics, as well as other denominations since the days of the Vatican II council have accepted the historical-critical method for biblical criticism. Many of that tradition doubt whether Daniel was even a historical person since the only mention of him is in the Bible. Latter-day saints believing in divine revelation have other scriptures that confirm Daniel as being a historical person. (see D&C 116:1, and D&C 118:44) The Savior, himself, mentioned Daniel in his Olivet discourse (Matt. 24) Even so modern scholars would question whether Matthew was reporting the actual discourse of the Savior or simply relying on the earlier tradition of Daniel. The Historical-critical method also maintains that the author who wrote Daniel lived during the intertestemental period (approx. 167 B.C.), and certainly not during the exile period (600 B.C.) as others have stated. These Bible critics feel that all the prophecies and events mentioned in the Book of Daniel can be seen within the context of the Maccabean revolt. An example of the interpretation can be seen with the little horn mentioned in Daniel 7:8. The little horn would be interpreted as the power exhibited by Antonius Enpiphines IV. This approach to interpretation is sometimes called the preterist view which understands fulfillment of all events and prophecies to be during the life time of the individual authors. On the other hand, a person who believes in prophecy can see the little horn as a future earthly kingdom growing up after the Roman period continuing on to the present. Another example can be seen with the prophecy of the Abomination of Desolation (see Daniel 11:31). Preterists see this as having fulfillment only during the Maccabean revolt where the temple was desecrated. The Savior in the Olivet discourse used this prophecy of Daniel to describe the future Roman persecution during 70 A.D. under Titus, and also of another Abomination of Desolation before his second coming. AN APPROACH TO DANIEL 7 Chapter seven is the beginning of the vision that Daniel had while in captivity. He had these visions and dreams while upon his bed (verse 1). Nephi when telling of Lehi's visions also used the rare bed motif (see I Nephi 1:7), which is another confirmation that Daniel was a contemporary of Lehi around 600 B.C. Daniel describes his vision (vs. 2-14) followed by a commentary (vs. 15-28). He was permitted to see a vision of earthly kingdoms coming out of the sea (vs. 3). It should be mentioned that for the people of the Old Testament the sea was a symbol of chaos, foreboding, or evil. John also used the symbol of the sea to describe the beast which he depicted in Revelations 13:1. Daniel described these earthly kingdoms like a lion (Babylon), a bear (Medes-Persia), a leopard (Greece), and a beast (Rome) (vs. 4-8). Joseph Smith interpreted these passages by saying, "There is a grand distinction between the actual meaning of the prophets and the present translation. The prophets do not declare that they saw a beast or beasts, but that they saw the image or figure of a beast. Daniel did not see an actual bear or a lion, but the images or figures of those beasts ... but John saw the actual beast in heaven, showing to John that beasts did actually exist there, and not to represent figures of things on the earth " (History of the Church, vol. 5, pp.349-354) Joseph Smith was not allowed to give us everything he wanted to about the relationship between Daniel's and John's visions. He even admitted that he only had taught more about

these visions once at Ramos where Charles Thompson stuffed him like a cock-turkey with the prophecies of Daniel, and crammed it down Joseph's throat with his finger. Joseph qualified this statement by saying later in the discourse that the beast coming out of the sea in Revelation 13 should be translated the image of a beast as he had referred to it in Daniel's vision. (H.C. pp. 349-354) Daniel continues speaking of a little horn appearing among the other ten horns he had just previously described speaking great things (vs. S).
