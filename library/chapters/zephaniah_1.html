Donaldson●●○○○

This is the major theme of the book; and is the reason why Zephaniah has been called the "Prophet of the Lord's Day of Wrath, " Zephaniah sees that because of sin, there will be punishment if there is no repentance--and it seems that only a few (a remnant) are in such a condition of heart that they could repent (see 2:3; 3:12-13), The punishment he speaks of is to come to his people in his day. It would do_little to and for his people, in their sins, to pronounce a day of wrath which would occur two or three thousand years away! When the prophet says the words recorded in Zephaniah 1:7, 8, we can be quite certain that he is referring to the Day of the Lord, or day of wrath which is for that immediate time. This is made even more positive in 2:4-15 when all the other contemporary nations around Judah are to be punished and destroyed in the same destruction. However, this immediate Day of the Lord in the prophet's mind may have only been a small segment of the ultimate Day of the Lord which he may have seen. There are several passages which seem more universal and ultimate in scope than for just his day. Zephaniah 3:9-13 certainly refers to events which follow the great Day of the Lord, and which are of the Millennial Era in nature. The pure universal language, the gathering of all the dispersed from the south and the north (ten tribes), and the remnant which "shall feed and lie down and none shall make them afraid, " all point to times even subsequent to our own day. Donaldson●●●●●

The "fish gate" was on the north end of the city, and those there would be the first to see an enemy invading from the north. The fish gate opened into the part of the city known as the "second quarter" (v. 10), probably because it was an expansion of the original city of David. This quarter would be the first reached from the north. (Keil and Delitzsch, Commentary, 10:2:134).

To "search with candles" suggests an exhaustive search, since in the poorly lighted houses of those times one would have to use a candle to look for a lost object at night. (Keil and Delitzsch, Commentary, 10:2:134).

"Settled upon their lees" (v. 12) is a figure drawn from wine making. The lees are the thick residue of the pulp of the grapes. "Good wine, when it remains for a long time upon its lees, becomes stronger; but bad wine becomes harsher and thicker" (Keil and Delitzsch, Commentary, 10:2:134).

the day of the Lord is darkness, and not light. This phrase has at least two meanings. Donaldson●○○○○

The word translated here as nation is the Hebrew goi; it is a generic word meaning "any or all people." Thus this chapter, which contains condemnations of various peoples other than Israel, begins with an invitation to people not before chosen ("desired") to respond to the call of Abraham and "seek . . . the Lord . . . seek righteousness, seek meekness" (Zeph. 2:3). It was an invitation little heeded before Babylon's invasion but one that will be heard and implemented in the days before the fall of spiritual Babylon. (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament, 673.)
