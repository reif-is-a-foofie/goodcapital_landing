Donaldson●●●●●

7-10 Much has been written about Paul's "thorn in the flesh." Numerous hypotheses have been advanced by way of identifying the "thorn," including stuttering, epilepsy, a shrewish wife, an eye affliction, malaria, some mortal antagonist, or some spiritual weakness. Whatever it was, he knew why it was there, "lest I should be exalted above measure." He had pleaded with the Lord to take it away, but he learned, as we all do, that the Lord intentionally gives each of us weaknesses to keep us humble. Weaknesses are meant not to humiliate but to humble. If we let them accomplish their appointed purpose, weaknesses will not weaken us but strengthen us.

It is curious language about the thorn in the flesh - Paul was pricked at his conversion. He is using the same language. I personally believe that Paul was bothered by his persecuting the Christians. I don't think he ever got over it. The language of the next few verses seems to reinforce that idea.

a thorn in the flesh The word (Gr. skolops) Paul uses here literally means 'a Pale' (as in impaled) or 'a stake.' It was used to refer to sharpened stakes, to surgical instruments, or to fishhooks. The very term suggests something that was extremely painful and troublesome to Paul. There have been endless debates on what such an infirmity might be, and the suggestions have included a bitter and shrewish wife who turned against Paul at his conversion, epilepsy, a serious eye affliction, malaria, and some spiritual weakness with which he was constantly troubled. There is no way of knowing from the present records what Paul meant.

Into every life there come the painful, despairing days of adversity and buffeting. There seems to be a full measure of anguish, sorrow, and often heartbreak for everyone, including those who earnestly seek to do right and be faithful. The apostle Paul referred to his own challenge: "And lest I should be exalted above measure . . . there was given to me a thorn in the flesh, the messenger of Satan to buffet me, lest I should be exalted above measure." (2 Corinthians 12:7.) (James E. Faust, To Reach Even unto You [Salt Lake City: Deseret Book Co., 1980], 98.)

Some undergo searing developments that cut suddenly into mortality’s status quo. Some have trials to pass through, while still others have allotments they are to live with. Paul lived with his “thorn in the flesh” (2 Corinthians 12:7). (Neal A. Maxwell, in Conference Report, Apr. 2000, 90; or Ensign, May 2000, 72)

And if we want to give the members of the Church some understanding of the anguish the prophets feel, how about this tender insight from Moses, even after he has delegated some responsibilities: "I am not able to bear all this people alone, because it is too heavy for me" (Numbers 11:14). In preparing your students, as I think you must, to understand that life is not a bowl of cherries, that trials and tribulations will come to them, please distinguish between the kind of tribulations and trials which come to us because of our own stupidity and sin, those which come to us because of the structure of life in which the Lord maketh the rain to fall on the just and the unjust alike, and, third, those that I call the highest kind of tribulation, in which we know, as Paul did, the fellowship of Christ's sufferings. As people suffer that latter kind of tribulation, they are reassured that it is of the Lord, when the scriptures say, as did Paul, "There was given to me a thorn in the flesh" (2 Corinthians 12:7). "Given" is an action verb. Paul didn't say, "I happened to develop such a problem." Likewise, Mosiah 3:19 is the only place where sainthood is defined, and in addition to the attributes of love and patience, a saint is one who is "willing to submit to all things which the Lord seeth fit to inflict upon him." (Neal A. Maxwell, The Old Testament: Relevancy Within Antiquity, CES Old Testament Symposium, 16 August 1979)

Donaldson●●○○○

After teaching 20 years, I was forced to retire. My chronic back pain had driven me to an early retirement. About the only place that was comfortable for me was on the carpeted floor. I had already had two back surgeries without much relief. My prayer to Heavenly Father was for a complete recovery. I forgot "thy will be done." In my painful anguish all I could envision was complete relief from my excruciating pain. I had been given blessings but I felt no respite to my satisfaction. In my desperation, I sat down and wrote a letter to each of my children and tried my best to explain why I had taken my life. I quietly tucked the letters in a box in the closet where surely they would be found after my demise. Enduring to the end is fine, I thought, if you do not experience daily chronic pain. Now all I had to do was think of a "clean" way to end my life. As I entered the shower, I began to sing. All of a sudden I realized I had not sung in weeks. I felt an exhilarating joy as the water hit my face. Later as I studied, I came upon this scripture. Never had a scripture meant so much to me at this time. My mode of praying changed from, "Please, Heavenly Father, heal me," to "Heavenly Father, grant me strength to bear the pain and not murmur." Am I pain-free? No, but I am able to sleep and endure the pain much better than before. My faith has increased greatly. Through my trials, I have learned that faith does indeed precede the miracle. Our prophets always encourage us to read, study and ponder the scriptures. I am eternally grateful for following that counsel. Failure to do so may have jeopardized my quest for eternal glory. (Dora E. Paulson, LDS Church News, March 15, 2003)
