Nabi is generally defined as a "speaker" or "spokesman" for God, and carries the idea of "one who is called." A prophet, then, is one commissioned by God to make known his will. The spirit of prophecy is the spirit by which the knowledge of God must be communicated and is not confined to the foretelling of future events (see Moses 6:13, 23; Moses 8:16).

RECORD OF ZENIFF (CHAPS. 9-22).

Mormon inserts this history of the people of Zeniff. It comprises about 70 years and three kings, Zeniff, Noah, and Limhi. It is important to remember that chronologically this is a flashback—to tell us what happened to these people and give background for the state of affairs with Limhi’s people. This record includes chapters 9-22 and is taken from the plates spoken of in Mosiah 8:5. (Bryan Richards)

The transition between the material in Mosiah 7-8 and 9 is certainly Mormon’s least literary shift to date in the Book of Mormon. Mormon will begin this insertion with direct quotations from Zeniff, but will end with abridgements of that information. It would appear that Mormon’s logic for the transition was something on the order of the following: a. Mormon intended to at least begin with a direct translation from the plates of Zeniff (which are valuable to Mormon both for the words of Abinidi and their effect upon Alma the Elder). b. In order to have any transition into the story on the plates of Zeniff, Mormon needed the historical context of the reuniting of the Limhites with Ammon. c. The story of Limhi necessarily hinges upon the events surrounding the end of Noah’s reign. d.

Mosiah 9-10: The reign of Zeniff. (This portion appears to be unabridged.) Mosiah 9:1-3: Zeniff is part of an expedition to reclaim the land of Lehi-Nephi. He assumes command of the expedition by coup d'état. Mosiah 9:4-10: Zeniff's people take possession of the land of Lehi-Nephi, which they occupy for twelve years in peace. Mosiah 9:11-19: Zeniff's people, remembering to call on the Lord for help, repel an invasion by the Lamanites, with heavy losses. Mosiah 10:1-5: Zeniff puts his people on a war footing, but has peace for twenty-two more years. Mosiah 10:6-21: A second Lamanite invasion is repelled. Zeniff encourages his people with a speech (vv. 11-18) explaining the origins of Lamanite hatred of Nephites. Mosiah 10:22: Zeniff confers the kingdom on Noah3.

Mosiah 14: Abinadi quotes Isa. 53, containing many references to the "suffering servant." (See a previous lesson.) Mosiah 15:1-9: Abinadi tells of the life and suffering of the coming Christ. Mosiah 15:10-31: Abinadi interprets Isa. 52:7-10, the question he was asked in Mosiah 12:20-24: emphasizing the word "seed" in those verses, he says that they refer to the resurrection. Mosiah 16: Abinadi tells the consequences of what he has taught: we must repent if we want to achieve a good resurrection. Mosiah 17: Abinadi is burned to death, declaring doom upon the king and his priests for their deed. Alma1 believes Abinadi, and is cast out. Mosiah 18:1-16: Alma1 preaches and baptizes at the Waters of Mormon. Mosiah 18:17-29: Alma1 establishes the order of the church.

1. Where does this record come from?

In the preamble to chapter 9, Mormon introduces the record of Limhi's people. This is a good place to insert their records, so Mormon leaves off his abridgement of the large plates to put this in. It is worthwhile to note that part I of this record is in the first person. Mormon tends to put everything in the third person in his abridgement, so this may well be unabridged. If so, it is interesting that Zeniff begins his record by talking about his education as a youth, most particularly his language. Thus he carries on the tradition of starting one's life record this way, which began with Nephi (1 Ne. 1:1-3) and continued with Enos (Enos 1:1) (see a previous lesson).
