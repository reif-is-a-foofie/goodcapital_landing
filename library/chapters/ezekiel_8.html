Donaldson●●○○○

Sun worship was practised by the Canaanites, but lately had been reintroduced from Assyria (2 Ki. 23:5, 11; Je. 8:2). Between the porch and the altar was the place where the priests offered prayer (Joel 2:17), with their faces, of course, towards the Temple; in this spot, with their backs to the temple, the adoration of the sun took place, as complete a renunciation of Yahweh [an alternate spelling of Jehovah] as possible. (Guthrie and Motyer, New Bible Commentary, p. 670; see also 2 Chronicles 29:6.)

While in exile in Babylon, Ezekiel was told that Jerusalem was to be destroyed. He saw in vision the wickedness of Jerusalem so that he could explain to the people why the Lord was going to allow the Holy City to be conquered. As part of that vision, Ezekiel saw "five and twenty men" who had their backs toward the temple and were worshiping the sun. A student might say that it is a serious thing when a couple of dozen people reject the true God, but still, twenty- five people out of an entire city seems like a small minority. And besides, Israel had been guilty of idolatry numerous times before this, so why was this incident so critical to the fate of the people? But the key concept is the number twenty-five. In those days twenty-four courses (or orders) of the priesthood were assigned to perform the temple ordinances. The number twenty-five thus includes the leaders of all the courses and the high priest. For Israel to participate in idolatrous worship was serious enough, but for the entire priesthood to worship a false god in the very precincts of the temple was even more serious, for it shows how deep into iniquity Israel had sunk. (CES Growing Edge, Vol. 16 No. 3, Nov 1983)

Although it is not clear what the expression "put the branch to their nose" means, and there are differences of opinion among the scholars, a comment from Dummelow may be helpful. He wrote that the expression was "usually explained as a ceremony connected with sun-worship. Persian sun-worshippers held bunches of the twigs of certain trees before their mouths, that they might not contaminate the sun with their breath." (Commentary, p. 498.)
