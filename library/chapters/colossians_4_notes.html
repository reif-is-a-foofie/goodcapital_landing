Though we do not have all of the letters Paul composed, scholars have dated 1 Thessalonians as Paul's first epistle. The letter was likely written near the end of A.D. 52. Paul's usual practice was to dictate to a scribe and then add a few words in his own handwriting. Curiously, the subscriptions at the end of each book were written much later by copyists-probably between the fourth and sixth centuries after Christ. Hence, they are sometimes questionable (see Anderson, Understanding Paul, 72). For instance, although the subscription appearing after 1 Thessalonians 5:28 says the epistle was written from Athens, 1 Thessalonians was probably written from Corinth, a city about 250 miles from Thessalonica (see Bible Dictionary, "Epistles to the Thessalonians").

Thessalonica was originally named Therma because of the many hot springs in the surrounding area, but in 315 B.C. it was renamed Thessalonica after the half sister of Alexander the Great. It later became known as Salonika and today it is called Thessaloniki. It is one of the few cities that still exists today from New Testament times and has a booming population of 300,000. The city was conquered by Rome in 168 B.C., and was made the capitol of the entire providence of Macedonia. When Paul made his journey to the city, it boasted a population of 200,000 consisting mostly of Greeks though there was a large Roman

population with a strong Jewish minority. Its location also contributed to its importance. It was probably the greatest of the cities along the entire Egnatian Road, a great military highway which connected Rome with the East and which ran through Macedonia and parallel to the Aegean Sea. It had a sheltered harbor which was made into a naval station and equipped with docks by the Romans. Its midway position between the Adriatic and the Hellespont makes it even today a natural outlet for traffic from all points. This commercial activity had two important results. First, it made Thessalonica a wealthy city. Well-to-do Romans settled there and Jewish merchants were attracted by the commercial advantages of the city (see Acts 17:4).

I AND II THESSALONIANS These letters constitute what is probably the earliest writing of the Apostle Paul. There were written in A.D. 51-52, soon after the founding of the Thessalonian church, and give Paul’s answer, to some basic problems disturbing the Christians of Thessalonica. The major contributions are eschatological, investigating especially the events preceding and accompanying the return of Christ. The concern of Paul for his followers is apparent throughout.

Who wrote this letter? 1st Thessalonians is generally considered to be a genuine letter of Paul. When was it written? Probably about 50 A.D.; it was written while Paul was in Athens (1 Thes. 3:1). This is the oldest piece of Christian writing to survive. Who were the Thessalonians? They lived in Thessalonica, on the northern boundary of Greece. Paul visited them on his second missionary journey. While Acts claims that the saints there were primarily Jews and "devout" (i.e., Jewish-believing) Gentiles (Acts 17:4), Paul himself never mentions Jews among them in the letter, but rather refers to his converts as former pagans ("...ye turned to God from idols..." 1 Thes. 1:9).
