Who is the branch? Christ is the Branch, a name applied in ancient Israel to point attention to the great truth that the promised Messiah would come in the lineage of Israel and of David, that he would be a branch or part of that illustrious line. Through Jeremiah the Lord said: "Behold, the days come, saith the Lord, that I will raise unto David a righteous Branch, and a King shall reign and prosper, and shall execute judgment and justice in the earth. In his days Judah shall be saved, and Israel shall dwell safely: and this is his name whereby he shall be called, THE LORD OUR RIGHTEOUSNESS (Jer. 23:5-6; 33:15-17; Isa. 11:1-5; Zech. 3:8-10; 6:12-15.)" (Bruce R. Mc Conkie, Mormon Doctrine, p.102)

We shall now see that he is also called David, that he is a new David, and Eternal David, who shall reign forever on the throne of his ancient ancestor. Isaiah 11: The Rod, the branch, and the Branch Copyright © Jeff Newton 2005 Introduction The King James' Version (hereinafter "KJV") of Isaiah 11:1 reads as follows, "And there shall come forth a rod out of the stem of Jesse, and a Branch shall grow out of his roots."

This paper analyzes, in turn, the etymological roots of, the meaning of, and interpretations of the key nouns in said scripture, namely rod, stem of Jesse, Branch (or branch), and roots. In contravention to various ecclesiastical and biblical commentaries, this paper proposes evidence and reasoning for a different interpretation of the word branch (as opposed to the Branch) as used in the context of Isaiah 11:1. Hebraic and Doctrinal Denotations Rod: Pursuant to The New Strong's Exhaustive Concordance of the Bible (hereinafter "Strong's"), the rod of Isaiah 11:1 is the Hebrew word chôter (reference number 2415) and means "from an unused root of uncertain significance; a twig - rod". Doctrine and Covenants (D&C) 113:3-4 provides additional insight as to the meaning of the rod. "What is the rod spoken of in the first verse of the 11th chapter of Isaiah, that should come out of the Stem of Jesse? Behold, thus saith the Lord: It is a servant in the hands of Christ, who is partly a descendant of Jesse as well as of Ephraim, or of the house of Joseph, on whom there is laid much power." Stem: Pursuant to Strong's, the stem of Isaiah 11:1 is the Hebrew word geza' (reference number 1503) and means "from an unused root meaning to cut down (trees), the trunk or stump of a tree (as felled or planted) - stem, stock." Stem of Jesse: D&C 113:1-2 gives the express, direct, and indisputable interpretation as to what the Stem of Jesse signifies. "Who is the Stem of Jesse spoken of in the 1st, 2d, 3d, 4th, and 5th verses of the 11th chapter of Isaiah? Verily thus saith the Lord: It is Christ." (As the Stem of Jesse is unquestionably Christ, it is noteworthy that verses 2 through 5 of Isaiah 11 have direct reference to Christ's power, glory, judgment, equity, righteousness and faithfulness.) Branch (or more specifically branch): Pursuant to Strong's, the branch of Isaiah 11:1 is the Hebrew word nêtser (reference number 5342) and means "in the sense of greenness as a striking color; a shoot; figuratively a descendant - branch." The KJV capitalizes this use of branch in Isaiah 11:1 which issue is discussed more at length below. Roots: Pursuant to Strong's, the roots of Isaiah 11:1 is the Hebrew word sheresh (reference number 8328) and means "a root (literal or figurative): - bottom, deep, heel, root." Different Word Choices and Interpreting Ellis Rasmussen's Literal Translation Substituting the four key Hebrew words above into KJV of Isaiah 11:1 reads as follows, "And there shall come forth a chôter out of the geza' of Jesse, and a nêtser shall grow out of his sheresh." Similarly, one may effectively substitute different words from those chosen by the KJV translators to generate a different and potentially superior translation of such text. While indicating that Isaiah 11:1 is a Hebrew direct poetic couplet, Ellis Rasmussen has done so and suggests the literal translation into common English is "There shall come forth a branch from the trunk of Jesse: Indeed, a shoot from his roots shall bear fruit". (Introduction to the Old Testament 2:45 as quoted in D&C Student Manual) If one then inserts the replacement for stem of Jesse (pursuant to D&C 113) into the above literal translation, the result is as follows: "There shall come forth a branch from Christ: Indeed, a shoot from his roots shall bear fruit". (italics added) Accepting Rasmussen's prima facie assertion that Isaiah 11:1 is a Hebrew direct poetic couplet, chôter and nêtser are parallel ideas while geza' of Jesse and sheresh are parallel ideas. BRANCH, Branch, or branch: nêtser Is Christ the branch (or nêtser) of Isaiah 11:1? There are only four references in the Hebrew Bible that use nêtser for branch. Of course, one is Isaiah 11:1, as shown above. The other three are as follows: Isaiah 14:19: In reference to Lucifer, its reads "But thou art cast out of thy grave like an abominable branch, and as the raiment of those that are slain, thrust through with a sword that go down to the stones of the pit; as a carcase (sic) (carcass) trodden under foot." Isaiah 60:21: Speaking about righteous Israel, the Lord states, "Thy people also shall be all righteous: they shall inherit the land for ever, the branch of my planting, the work of my hands, that I may be glorified." Daniel 11:7: In reference to a "king's daughter of the south" it reads, "But out of a branch of her roots shall one stand up in his estate which shall come with an army, and shall enter into the fortress of the king of the north, and shall deal against them, and shall prevail." It is noteworthy that not one of these other three uses of nêtser for branch is used in allusion to Christ. BRANCH, Branch or Tsemach as an Honorific Title for Christ: By contrast, many references to Christ as the Branch, a capitalized honorific title, are used in the Hebrew Bible. Pursuant to Strong's, such Branch is the Hebrew word tsemach (reference number 6780) and means "a sprout (usually concrete), literally or figuratively - branch, bud, that which (where) grew (upon), spring(-ing)". All the references in the Hebrew Bible that employ this use of tsemach are as follows: Isaiah 4:2: "In that day shall the branch of the Lord be beautiful and glorious and the fruit of the earth shall be excellent and comely for them that are escaped of Israel." Given that this version of the word tsemach is not capitalized by the KJV translators, biblical scholars have found dual denotations for it. According to Parry et al, "This word [tsemach] may have a double meaning. First, the Lord is called Branch messianically." (See below.) "Second, an offshoot of the house of Israel is often called "a righteous branch" (Jacob 2:25) or a "branch of the house of Israel" (1 Ne. 15:12; 2 Ne. 3:5) The context of this section suggests that the branch represents a specific remnant, or branch, of Israel that remains in Israel after God's judgments, identified in Isaiah 3." (Parry, Parry, and Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, pp. 47-48) (See also, Nyman, pp. 37 - 38) Jeremiah 23:5: "Behold, the days come, saith the Lord, that I will raise unto David a righteous Branch, and a King shall reign and prosper, and shall execute judgment and justice in the earth." All biblical scholars and authorities appear to agree that this instance of tsemach refers to Christ. Jeremiah 33:15: "In those days, and at that time, will I cause the Branch of righteousness to grow up unto David; and he shall execute judgment and righteousness in the land." All biblical scholars and authorities appear to agree that this instance of tsemach refers to Christ. Zechariah 3:8: "Hear now, O Joshua, the high priest, thou, and thy fellows that sit before thee: for they are men wondered at: for, behold, I will bring forth my servant the BRANCH." All Biblical scholars and authorities appear to agree that this instance of tsemach refers to Christ. Zechariah 6:12-13: "And speak unto him, saying, Thus speaketh the Lord of hosts, saying, Behold he man whose name is The BRANCH; and he shall grow up out of his lace, and he shall build the temple of the Lord. Even he shall build the temple of the Lord; and he shall bear the glory, and shall sit and rule upon his throne; and he shall be a priest upon his throne: and the counsel of peace shall be between them both." All biblical scholars and authorities appear to agree that this instance of tsemach refers to Christ. Equating Tsemach with Geza' With the background of the above scriptures for tsemach as indubitably referring to Christ, various LDS commentators and authorities have effectively equated tsemach and nêtser with what appears to be an innocent, unquestioned reliance on the capitalization of nêtser as Branch in Isaiah 11:1 by the KJV translators. Bruce R. Mc Conkie states, "Isaiah speaks of the Stem of Jesse, whom he also designates as a branch growing out of the root of that ancient worthy. He recites how the Spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him;

how he shall be mighty in judgment; how he shall smite the earth and slay the wicked; and how the lamb and the lion shall lie down together in that day - all of which has reference to the Second Coming and the millennial era thereby ushered in. (Isa. 11.) As to the identity of the Stem of Jesse, the revealed words says: "Verily thus saith the Lord: It is Christ." (D&C 113:1-2) This also means that the Branch is Christ, as we shall not see from other related scriptures." (Promised Messiah, Bruce R. Mc Conkie, p. 192) Then Elder Mc Conkie proceeds to cite and comment on three of the four abovementioned scriptures from Jeremiah and Zechariah that employ the word tsemach for branch and refer to Christ as the Branch without addressing the notion that Isaiah 11:1 actually employs the word nêtser instead. Ellis Rasmussen states, "Because Jesse was the father of King David and his descendants were the kings of Judah, of whose lineage Jesus would be born, it is clear who this 'Branch' is. Jesus the Lord, Jehovah; hence the wisdom, understanding, counsel, might, knowledge, and reverence of the Lord reside in him. Jeremiah and other prophets also knew this 'Branch' (cf. His righteous reign on earth, bringing justice and peace to man and beast, will be established in his second coming (Isaiah 11:4-9)." (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Commentary on the Old Testament, p. 512) Rasmussen makes the similar assumption as did Elder Mc Conkie above. Doctrine and Covenants Student Manual states, in reference to Isaiah 11, "The branch and stem are both Christ (see Jeremiah 23:5-6; 33:15-17); these terms refer to Christ's being of the lineage of King David, the son of Jesse." (p. 283) Monte S. Nyman states, "The branch which is to grow out of the roots is Christ." ("Great Are the Words of Isaiah, Monte S. Nyman, p.71) KJV editors that capitalized Branch or BRANCH did not have the benefit of D&C 113:1-2. It appears LDS pundits followed the KJV translators' who incorrectly associated Isaiah's 11:1 nêtser with the other direct references to Christ as the tsemach. Save one narrow exception due to potential dual meaning (Isaiah 4:2), no other biblical use of nêtser ostensibly refers to Christ while all uses of tsemach do refer to Christ. Evidence Deduced from the Direct Parallelism of Isaiah Further, accepting at face value Rasmussen's proposition (above) that Isaiah 11:1 is a Hebrew direct parallel poetic couplet, one effectively equates or pairs chôter with nêtser (that is, rod and twig are paired with branch, shoot and descendant) and geza' with sheresh (that is, trunk, stump, stem, and stock are paired with roots). (See the table immediately below.) Such a direct comparison or parallelism is very reasonable, given the similarity of the definitions and synonyms. One is figuratively a relatively small outgrowth from a larger, fundamental part, tree, or entity. chôter geza' nêtser sheresh KJV rod stem of Jesse Branch roots Possible Strong alternatives rod twig trunk stump stem stock shoot descendant roots Conversely, a chiastic or inverted parallelism does not appear proper in this verse for the same reasoning as above and would be correspondingly much more difficult to infer. Further, accepting arguendo that Isaiah 11:1 is a direct parallelism and then inserting what is taught from D&C 113:1-2 that Christ is the stem of Jesse (or geza'), then one reaches the following comparison: We effectively equate chôter with nêtser (that is, rod and twig are paired with branch, shoot and descendant) and geza' and Christ are paired with sheresh (that is, trunk, stump, stem, and stock are paired with roots). The result with the replacements would read like as follows: "And there shall come forth a rod out of Christ, and a Branch shall grow out of Christ." Continuing the analysis, and assuming arguendo that Christ is also nêtser (the branch) (as suggested above by various scholarly and ecclesiastical authorities) (note, the author is not questioning that Christ

is the Branch or tsemach), attempting to insert Christ as well for nêtser creates an inconsistency within the direct parallelism, as Christ cannot be the chôter and nêtser and geza' and sheresh simultaneously. The result with the replacements would oddly read like as follows: "And there shall come forth Christ out of Christ, and Christ shall grow out of Christ." The insertions of Christ for the various figurative paired Hebrew words are mutually exclusive. If one believes Isaiah 11:1 is a direct parallelism, then one cannot consistently rationalize that Christ is both geza' (as indubitably taught by the D&C 113:1-2) and nêtser (the branch; not the Branch (tsemach)). Again assuming a direct parallelism, believing such an inconsistent analysis where Christ is both geza' and nêtser would create a silly, meaningless, and even a result more difficult to rationalize. ("And there shall come forth Christ out of Christ, and Christ shall grow out of Christ.") Thus, Isaiah 11:1 cannot be a direct parallelism, have Christ signify geza' (the stem of Jesse) and be nêtser (the branch). Furthermore, the chôter (rod) out of the stem of Jesse is clarified by D&C 113:3-4 as "a servant in the hands of Christ, who is partly a descendant of Jesse as well as of Ephraim, or of the house of much power." The Old Testament Student Manual: 1 Kings - Malachi states, in reference to chôter, "This scripture seems to be a reference to the Prophet Joseph Smith and to Ephraim's leadership in the restoration of the last days." Other LDS scholars agree stating, "Sidney Sperry suggested that the rod or 'servant in the hands of Christ' referred to the Prophet Joseph Smith. It may also refer to another latter-day prophet yet to come forth." (Parry, Parry, and Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, pp. 47-48, emphasis in original) (See also Nyman, p. 71) Thus, if Isaiah 11:1 is a direct parallelism, such evidence also supplies a fatal blow to the erroneous conclusion that Christ is nêtser (the branch), because nêtser is paired with chôter, and chôter is not Christ (according to scripture, supra). Conclusion As the 8th Article of Faith teaches, we must accept scripture translated correctly as questioned truth. The Doctrine and Covenants clearly teaches that Christ is the Stem of Jesse (geza') of Isaiah 11:1. The Doctrine and Covenants clearly teaches that a servant of Christ is the rod (chôter) out of the Stem of Jesse (geza') of Isaiah 11:1. We can reasonably accept biblical scholars' teachings that Isaiah 11:1 is a direct parallelism or poetic couplet. A serious inconsistency is invited, however, by blindly accepting the KJV translators' capitalization of nêtser as Branch. Generally, the use of nêtser in the Bible does not allude to Christ. In contrast, many if not all other KJV uses of another Hebrew word tsemach indeed mean branch in English and represent an honorific title of Christ, therefore meriting capitalization. The author asserts that the capitalization of nêtser as Branch in the KJV Bible is a translation error and a capitalization error on which many scholarly and ecclesiastical authorities have reasonably but erroneously relied due to the confusion of the similar English translations (branch) of nêtser and tsemach. This paper simply submits that we put the 8th Article of Faith in action as it relates to nêtser of Isaiah 11:1.

When he arrived here he said, "This is the right place." This very statement indicates that he would know the place when he saw it. He had seen Ensign Peak in vision. Two days after his arrival, with some of his brethren, he climbed the peak and there, in the words of Isaiah, unfurled "an ensign for the nations" (Isaiah 11:12). It may have only been a pocket handkerchief that was used, but it was indicative of a great millennial vision on the part of those leaders. I am convinced they saw not only 1847 but the planting and growth of this work through the years to come. Brigham Young dug his cane into the sun-baked soil and he noted where a temple should be built. That building was in fulfillment of the words of Isaiah: "And it shall come to pass in the last days, that the mountain of the Lord's house shall be established in the top of the mountains, and shall be exalted above the hills; and all nations shall flow unto it. For a moment, may I take you back 142 years when there was, of course, no tabernacle here, nor temple, nor Temple Square. On July 24, 1847, the pioneer company of our people came into this valley. An advance group had arrived a day or two earlier. Brigham Young arrived on Saturday. The next day, Sabbath services were held both in the morning and in the afternoon. There was no hall of any kind in

which to meet. I suppose that in the blistering heat of that July Sunday they sat on the tongues of their wagons and leaned against the wheels while the Brethren spoke. The season was late, and they were faced with a gargantuan and immediate task if they were to grow seed for the next season. But President Young pleaded with them not to violate the Sabbath then or in the future. The next morning they divided into groups to explore their surroundings. Brigham Young, Wilford Woodruff, and a handful of their associates hiked from their campground a little to the south of us, on past the ground where we are, and up the hill to the north of us. They climbed a dome-shaped peak, President Young having difficulty because of his recent illness. When the Brethren stood on the summit, they looked over this valley to the south of them. It was largely barren, except for the willows and rushes that grew along the streams that carried water from the mountains to the lake. There was no building of any kind, but Brigham Young had said the previous Saturday, "This is the place." The summit where they stood was named Ensign Peak out of reference to these great prophetic words of Isaiah: "And he [speaking of God] will lift up an ensign to the nations from far, and will hiss unto them from the end of the earth: and, behold, they shall come with speed swiftly" (Isaiah 5:26). "And he shall set up an ensign for the nations, and shall assemble the outcasts of Israel, and gather together the dispersed of Judah from the four corners of the earth" (Isaiah 11:12). There is some evidence to indicate that Wilford Woodruff took from his pocket a bandanna handkerchief and waved it as an ensign or a standard to the nations, that from this place should go the word of the Lord, and to this place should come the people of the earth. I think they may also on that occasion have spoken of the building of the temple, which today stands a few feet east of here, in fulfillment of the words of Isaiah: "And it shall come to pass in the last days, that the mountain of the Lord's house shall be established in the top of the mountains, and shall be exalted above the hills; and all nations shall flow unto it. "And many people shall go and say, Come ye, and let us go up to the mountain of the Lord, to the house of the God of Jacob; and he will teach us of his ways, and we will walk in his paths: for out of Zion shall go forth the law, and the word of the Lord from Jerusalem" (Isaiah 2:2-3). (Gordon B. Hinckley, in Conference Report, Oct. 1989, 68; or Ensign, Nov. 1989, 51-52)
