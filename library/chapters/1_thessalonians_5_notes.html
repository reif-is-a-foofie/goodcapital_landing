1-11 Paul continues his discussion on the Second Coming, exhorting the people to be ready for it and emphasizing that salvation is in Christ. "Whether we wake or sleep" (v. 10) is a play on words harking back to the idea of being asleep in Christ, that is, awaiting the resurrection while in the spirit world. Paul reminds the Saints that they already know that "the day of the Lord so cometh as a thief in the night" (v. 2), meaning that it will happen unexpectedly for some people, when they are unprepared spiritually. The phrase "day of the Lord" goes back to Amos 5:18. In Old Testament times the day of the Lord was regarded as a time when God would come and intervene with judgment, instituting blessing or condemnation. The Joseph Smith Translation of Luke 12:44 confirms that Jesus himself was author of the expression "as a thief in the night." This translation also indicates that Jesus told much more about his own second coming than various versions of the New Testament disclose. Paul probably quoted several of the lost teachings of Jesus as part of his own exhortation, just as we saw in 1 Thessalonians 4:15. (D. Kelly Ogden and Andrew C. Skinner, New Testament Apostles Testify of Christ [Salt Lake City; Deseret Book, 1998], 117)

The first word, "times" (chronos) is concerned more with the idea of elapsed time or duration of time. The latter word, "seasons" (kairos), stresses the quality or the characteristics of time, hence, the events, the nature of the time with its accompanied signs and characteristics like those expressed in Matthew 24, "the sun darkened," or as expressed here, "as a thief in the night."

"Sleep" is the Greek katheudo", a different word from the one used in chapter 4 for death. Katheudo" refers to a state of spiritual insensitivity. In Ephesians 5:14, members are enjoined to wake out of such a sleep that they might walk in the light of Christ in wisdom as wise and not foolish. Here in 1 Thessalonians 5, they are enjoined not to enter into it. In chapter 4, the word for sleep, koimao", refers to a gentle, peaceful sleep and refers to physical death. It is a synonym for death and is in contrast with "those who are alive." In chapter 5, katheudo" looks at the opposite, a state of sleep which is rest-less and wild. It refers to spiritual indifference and to carnality. It implies immorality and a life that seeks to live devoid of a relationship with God. Katheudo" is a synonym for spiritual lethargy or carnality and is in contrast with not physically being alive, but with being spiritually awake and in tune with the Lord.

The Greek word for “whole being” is holocleron, meaning “having all parts.” The English words “whole” and “holistic” are based on the first root of this word. All the parts of a person are given.

1. Spirit • Greek: pneuma • High Self 2. Soul • Greek: psyche • Middle Self 3. Body • Greek: soma • Basic Self

The Spirit is the “spiritual” or divine part of a person and is connected with breath. The word “spirit” comes from the Latin word, spiritus, meaning breath and the Greek word, pneuma, also means breath or air (as in pneumatic, pneumonia). Spirit relates to the High Self, which is the part of our being that is connected to divinity.

Soul is identified with the psychic nature, i.e., mind, rather than with the spirit. This is the Middle Self, or reasoning, thinking, conscious part of the person. Soul and spirit are not the same. Theology often confuses the two and uses them without distinction.

Body is the physical part and relates to the Basic Self, which has charge of the autonomic functions of the body. Soma (root, somat- ) is found in such English words as “somatic” and “chromosome.” Some of the other words are badly translated. “Blameless” (an adverb in Greek) means “in a manner not meriting blame” and refers to the verb, not to the “whole being.” The verb “keep” means “to care for” or “keep watch over.” The phrase should not be “Keep Blameless,” but “Care for in a manner that merits no blame.”
