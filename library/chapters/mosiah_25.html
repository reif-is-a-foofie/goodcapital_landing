People of Zarahemla] See Omni 1:12-19. When Mosiah I led the more righteous portion of the Nephites northward from the land of Lehi-Nephi (about 200 B.C.) he found on the west bank of the river Sidon a city inhabited by a people whose language he could not understand. They were ruled by a king named Zarahemla. When the two races developed the ability to communicate, it was discovered that the people of Zarahemla were the descendants of a colony which had been led by the Lord out of Jerusalem in the year when that city was destroyed by the king of Babylon (587 B.C.). After wandering in the wilderness they were brought across the ocean and landed in the Americas. In the years that followed, they migrated southward to the place where they were found by Mosiah. Because they had brought no scriptural or written records with them their language, society, and spiritual nature had all devolved or become corrupted. (Joseph Fielding Mc Conkie and Robert L. Millet, Doctrinal Commentary on the Book of Mormon, 4 vols., 2:, p.288)

Mulek] Mulek was the son of Zedekiah, king of Judea, who was preserved when the rest of his brothers were slain by the king of Babylon (see 2 Kings 25:7). It is quite probable that because of his lineage Mulek was appointed the king of the wandering colony of which he was a part. Through this colony the blood of Judah was destined to be mixed with the blood of Joseph in the Americas. (Joseph Fielding Mc Conkie and Robert L. Millet, Doctrinal Commentary on the Book of Mormon, 4 vols., 2:, p.288)

The merger of the Nephites and Mulekites is recounted with very little explanation in the Book of Mormon and presents some anomalies. Since Zarahemla, leader of the Mulekites, was descended from Zedekiah, king of Judah (see Omni 1:14-15; Mosiah 25:2; Helaman 6:10; 8:21), we are perplexed by the fact that he acknowledged Mosiah as king over the new confederation. It may be that we are dealing with the age-old leadership conflict between the biblical tribes of Judah (represented by Zarahemla) and Joseph (represented by Mosiah). If the Book of Mormon story is truly analogous to the biblical, we should expect that some of the Mulekites objected to Nephite rule and occasionally rebelled against those in power. There is, in fact, evidence that the rebellious king-men and Nehors may have been Mulekites. (John A. Tvedtnes in Stephen D. Ricks and William J. Hamblin, eds., Warfare in the Book of Mormon, p.298)

The name of King Zedekiah's son (first mentioned in Mosiah 25:2) was Muloch, not Mulek. The name Muloch has very interesting implications regarding pagan worship in the land of Jerusalem at the time Muloch was born. (Royal Skousen's Analysis of Textual Variants of the Book of Mormon)

The earliest manuscript spelling for the surviving son of king Zedekiah reads Muloch (in Mosiah 25:2 of the printer's manuscript). On the other hand, this name is spelled Mulek in Helaman 6 - 8 of the printer's manuscript. This alternative spelling is probably due to the nearby influence of 13 occurrences of the name of the city Mulek (consistently spelled as such in both manuscripts, from Alma 51 through Helaman 5). Note that the spelling Muloch suggests an ominous connection with the god Molech /Moloch (to which children in Israel were sacrificed prior to the Babylonian captivity?see 1 Kings 11:7 - 8, 2 Kings 23:10, and Acts 7:43). (Royal Skousen's Analysis of Textual Variants of the Book of Mormon)

Mosiah's intent was not to entertain his listeners. His purpose in reading the histories of Zeniff and Alma was to humble his own followers and to help them profit from the mistakes of others. Thus, by use of the recorded materials, he took his people from the depths of sorrow to the peak of concern for the welfare of their enemies. And, whether he intended to do so or not, he surely must have reinforced in the minds of his listeners many of the reasons for record-keeping, including those already cited, which are: o The preservation of language. o The preservation of the words of the prophets. o The preservation of lineage and priesthood rights. o The preservation of faith-promoting experiences. o The retention of Church members. o The preservation of personal memories. o The welding of the readers (children) to the writers (parents). These reasons and others that could be listed should convince us of the need to plant the "pecan tree" practice of personal and family histories. It should be our desire to "keep a proper and faithful record" of our lives (see D&C 128:9). Children Who Disinherit Their Parents by S. Kent Brown Religious Studies Center Newsletter~May 1999 A Book of Mormon passage that speaks of children effectively disinheriting their fathers invites comment. In From Jerusalem to Zarahemla, I have mentioned this matter only incidentally, in the chapter "Marriage and Treaty in the Book of Mormon: The Case of the Abducted Lamanite Daughters" (108 n. 8). The fathers were former priests of King Noah who had abandoned their families during a Lamanite invasion (Mosiah 19:10-12,18-21). Their leader was a man named Amulon. Two years after abandoning their families, these men kidnapped Lamanite women to be their wives, thus precipitating another Lamanite invasion (Mosiah 20:1-7). The passage in question reads: "The children of Amulon and his brethren ... were displeased with the conduct of their fathers, and they would no longer be called by the names of their fathers, therefore they took upon themselves the name of Nephi, that they might be called the children of Nephi" (Mosiah 25: 12). Two points draw our attention. First, I am not aware of any passage in the annals of ancient law that allowed an abandoned child effectively to divorce one parent or the other. Second, I am not aware of any ancient society that permitted a child to choose the person to whom he or she would be linked. Yet the passage evidently portrays both circumstances. It is possible, of course, that there was no legal provision in Nephite law that would allow these children to act as they did. If this observation is true, then authorities may have allowed their 3 actions as symbolic gestures. Such gestures, however, would not have solved certain questions tied to inheriting property from the children's biological fathers nor to potential responsibility for inherited debts. But Mormon did not raise these matters in his record since they were apparently not germane to his purposes. What had caught his attention instead was the children's unusual and strong response to the actions of their fathers. The Hammurapi Code, compiled in the eighteenth century B.C., legislated important standards that may beam light on the children's actions. According to laws 168-69, if a father wanted to disinherit a son (the reverse of the Book of Mormon case), there had to be evidence of a "grave offense." Additionally, for the first offense, the judges of a case between a father and a son were to "pardon" the son. It is only "if he should commit a grave offense a second time" that the father was allowed to "disinherit his son." The case of the Nephite children fits this requirement because their fathers had first abandoned their wives and children and then, in a separate, second offense, kidnapped the Lamanite women. Other legislation in the Hammurapi Code may connect to the Book of Mormon situation. According to

laws 192-93, which deal specifically with adopted children, a child's attempt to reject adoptive parents was not allowed and, in fact, carried severe penalties. Because the statute stood in Hammurapi's Code, it clearly hints that adopted children had tried such actions. In this light, to override such a standard would presumably require that a parent, adoptive or natural, commit extraordinary crimes. While it cannot be shown that this kind of standard operated among Nephites, the extreme situation that led to the children's actions would possibly, under the code of Hammurapi, have allowed their extraordinary procedure. (Citations are from Martha 1. Roth, Law Collections from Mesopotamia and Asia Minor, Writings from the Ancient World, Society of Biblical Literature, vol. 6 [Atlanta: Scholars Press, 1995], 113, 120.) However, although both Nephite and later Jewish law descended from Old Testament law, one must not step into the trap of seeing Nephite law through the provisions of Jewish law. For example, statutes in Jewish law address the legal status of women who lose husbands, determining the woman's inheritance rights and her right to remarry (e.g., Mishnah, Yebamoth 10.1-5; 15.1-16.7; Shebuoth 7.7). But we cannot assume that such statutes would mirror those that developed among the Nephites and would therefore illuminate the situation of the children's abandoned mothers. The social, religious, and military situations that deprived women of husbands in the two societies were very different through the centuries and would have given rise to different pressures as laws developed.
