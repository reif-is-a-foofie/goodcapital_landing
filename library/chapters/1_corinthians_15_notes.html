But Hebrews spells out the plan of forgiveness-exactly what Latter-day Saints know from modern revelation as the first principles of faith, repentance, baptism, and the laying on of hands for the gift of the Holy Ghost. Acts shows the same plan of salvation, not lying inert in pieces on the dissecting table but in action as a living whole. Adam brought mortality, Christ immortality; Adam brought death, Christ life. Death consists in the separation of body and spirit and is possible only because mortality came into the world through the fall of Adam. The Bible supports the doctrine [of baptism for the dead]. If there is one word of the Lord that supports the doctrine, it is enough to make it a true doctrine. Again, if we can baptize a man in the name of the Father, [and] of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost for the remission of sins, it is just as much our privilege to act as an agent and be baptized for the remission of sins for and in behalf of our dead kindred who have not heard the gospel or fulness of it. (Joseph Smith, Discourse of 27 March 1842, recorded by Wilford Woodruff; WJS, 109-10.)

The Lord has said that His work is "to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man." It follows then that all mankind, living and dead, should have the opportunity of hearing the gospel either in this life or in the spirit world. As Paul said to the Corinthians, "Else what shall they do which are baptized for the dead, if the dead rise not at all? why are they then baptized for the dead?" This is the reason we do ordinance work in temples for our deceased ancestors. No person's choice or agency is taken away. Those for whom the work is done may accept it or not, as they choose. (James E. Faust, in Conference Report, Apr. 2006, 68; or Ensign, May 2006, 67)

See the epistle written by Joseph Smith in 1842 containing instructions about baptism for the dead: D&C 128:1-18 (especially vv. 16-18). It is essential to discuss the ordinance of baptism itself when talking about the bold doctrine of baptism for the dead. If baptism by immersion is mandatory for the living for entrance into the kingdom of God, as Jesus and his apostles plainly taught, it should also be a requirement for those who have passed on, who had no opportunity to receive baptism during mortality. There is evidence outside the Bible that the ordinance of baptism for the dead was taught and practiced by early Christians. Even the Roman Catholic Jerome Biblical Commentary admits that is seems as though Christians at Corinth "would undergo baptism in the name of their deceased non-Christian relatives and friends, hoping that this vicarious baptism might assure them a share in the redemption of Christ." Nevertheless, the Commentary regards the passage as obscure and the practice strange (Brown, Jerome Biblical Commentary) 2:273). Many scholars find no satisfactory explanation for such a practice (see "Baptism for the Dead" in Ludlow, Encyclopedia of Mormonism) 1:95-97). (D. Kelly Ogden and Andrew C. Skinner, New Testament Apostles Testify of Christ [Salt Lake City; Deseret Book, 1998], 145-146)

There is in the ruins of Ephesus now a place called St. Paul’s Prison.

But Paul was a Roman citizen and it was unlawful to make such a one be a yhriomacov. If he were cast to the lions unlawfully, he could have prevented it by claiming his citizenship. That may seem so obvious that it is not pertinent. But the Greek actually says: “There is a PSYCHIC body and there is a spiritual body.” The two phrases are identical to the nouns in the preceding passage: psychikon and pneumatikon. If “physical” was intended, the word would have been physikon.

These terms, "natural" and "spiritual" are ones we need to look at more closely. Let's start with "spiritual." This word is pneumatikos, which means "having to do with the pneuma," which, in turn, is the word for spirit, breath, and wind: here, clearly "spirit." Now, we assume that the other word, here translated "natural," is supposed to be some kind of opposition to this; something more like the existence we have now. It is because of this assumption that the word was translated "natural." I want to emphasize, it was translated that way, not because that's what it means, but because no other term seemed to fit. In fact, this is a pretty rotten translation, but, because nothing else seems to fit, modern translations tend to perpetuate the error. The word is psykhikos, meaning "having to do with the psykhê," a word usually translated "soul." For us, I think this fact is one of the most interesting things about this chapter. While statements like those in verse 44 are often used to show that we will only be spirits, not whole beings, when we are resurrected, the truth is that the words psykhê and pneuma both can mean "spirit": our new existence is no more "spiritual," really, than our old one. The only real difference between these words is that the psykhê was generally considered to survive only in a diminished state after death, if at all, while the pneuma is characteristic of beings that dwell with God. Paul coordinates the word psykhikos with psykhê in the next verse. Unfortunately, we can't see the relationship in the KJV, because it's translated as "soul." (Mike Tueller)

Thanks be to God, which giveth us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ" (1 Cor. 15:54-57; see also Hel. 14:14-18). (Donald W. Parry, Jay A. Parry, and Tina M. Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, p.228)
