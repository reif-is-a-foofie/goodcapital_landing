Donaldson Commentary

Lament for a Broken and Corrupt Nation (7:1-6)

7:1-6. The prophet Micah employed three figures to portray the gross state of Israel's wickedness: (1) the picture of a solitary grape upon the vine (see Micah 7:1); (2) a battle between a man with a net and a man without a net (see v. 2); and (3) the comparison of a wicked man to a briar or a thorn hedge (see v. 4). Here the prophet points out the small number of the upright to be found in the land. He himself seemed to be the only person who was on God's side; and he considers himself as a solitary grape, which had escaped the general gathering. … He desired to see the first-ripe fruit-distinguished and eminent piety; but he found nothing but a very imperfect or spurious kind of godliness. … They hunt every man his brother with a net. This appears to be an allusion to the ancient mode of duel between the retiarius and secutor. The former had a casting net, which he endeavoured to throw over the head of his antagonist, that he might then despatch him with his short sword. The other parried the cast; and when the retiarius missed, he was obliged to run about the field to get time to set his net in right order for another throw. While he ran, the other followed, that he might despatch him before he should be able to recover the proper position of his net; and hence the latter was called secutor, the pursuer, as the other was called retiarius, or the net man. … … The best of them is as a brier. They are useless in themselves, and cannot be touched without wounding him that comes in contact with them. He alludes to the thick thorn hedges, still frequent in Palestine. (Clarke, Commentary, 4:726.)

Donaldson Commentary

NAHUM This book is a vivid prediction of the approaching downfall of Nineveh, the capital city of Assyria, one of the most warlike of the ancient heathen nations. Of the Prophet Nahum, whose name means "consolation" or "comfort", little is known. His purpose was to comfort his people, long harassed by Assyria, with the promise that this cruel and oppressing people would soon meet destruction at God’s hand.

Nahum was one of the greatest poets of all the prophets. The King James Version does not show this as clearly as does the Hebrew text. His little book of only forty-seven verses is the announcement and proclamation of the fall of Ninevah, the Assyrian Capital. Nahum predicts the destruction, event by event as if he were sitting on the walls of the city at the very moment of the siege. There is an element of rejoicing over such a terrible destruction. This has made some critics evaluate Nahum as an inferior prophet. But when one realizes the decades that Assyrian cruelty had remained unchecked, the righteous indignation felt by Nahum is more readily appreciated. Nahum has been called the "Prophet of Ninevah's Doom," That was his message. Of Nahum himself we only know what can be obtained from the short book he wrote. He was a poet of great brilliance and dramatic power, even comparable to the prophet Isaiah. He was born in the village of Elkosh, but there is no certainty as to where this was located. It may have been a place in the vicinity of ancient Ninevah, and if so, Nahum may have been a descendant of those Israelites taken in the Assyrian captivity of Israel. There are other possibilities for the location of Elkosh, and no positive evidence for any of them has been found (The Voice..., p. 352). The word Nahum means "comforter" or "consoler" but his message in the book of Nahum is anything else but comforting, at least to the Ninevites. Nahum is the "Prophet of Moral Indignation" or the"Prophet of Ninevah's Coming Doom." By the vision and insight given him by the Lord, Nahum announced and proclaimed the horrible fall and destruction of the capitol of that great nation--Assyria. Because his central message is of God's avenging wrath upon that cruel nation, many scholars have said that Nahum is more poetry than prophecy, and have considered it to be an inferior book. It must be emphasized that part of a prophet's work is to rebuke, condemn, denounce sin and pronounce judgment and punishment upon the sinners unless they repent. Nahum knew that God controls the destiny of nations and that by the punishment of the brutal tyrant, so full of cruelty, rapacity, deceitful diplomacy, and commercial trickery, his (God's) morality and justice are manifested. Nahum could therefore rejoice over Ninevah's fall. Nahum describes in vivid language (2:1-10) the siege, capture, and plunder of Ninevah. He describes the

feelings of fear and confusion when the city is being destroyed. He pictures blood and slaughter; he sees the women ravished and the city spoiled. "The heart melteth, and the knees smite together, and much pain is in all loins, and all the faces of them all gather blackness (appear troubled)" 2:10, Nahum asks a question in sarcasm: "Where is the dwelling of the lions ."[Assyria's Capital] (2:11), He also says that everyone who hears of the fall of the city will applaud by clapping hands (3:19). One of the most vivid and remarkable descriptions of the siege of a city to be found in any literature in existence is in Nahum 3:1-3. The English translation lost much of Nahum's power of expression and poetry, but even so, it is still a remarkable piece of literature. The instructor should let the student read thispas`sage and imagine the siege of Ninevah. It should be read from various translations for comparison. What lessons may we learn from Nahum? (1) That God hates sin. Compare: "For I the Lord cannot look upon sin with the least degree of allowance." (D & C 1:31) A rebuke of militarism. Compare: "All they that take the sword shall perish with the sword. " Matthew 26:52. That war and wickedness will never succeed in the long run. That God governs the destiny of the nations. Compare D & C 60:4; 117:6. God's judgment will ultimately triumph over all the forces of evil. (CES Manual)

Nahum gave his book the title "the burden of Nineveh," and its source of information, "the vision of Nahum the Elkoshite." Burden designates it as a message "lifted up" against Nineveh. Vision is used here to translate chazon, which denotes divine vision, not ordinary sight. Nahum means "full of comfort" and could relate to the comfort he brought to Judah in the knowledge of the end of the threat of Assyria. The meaning or location of Elkosh is not known, but tradition holds that ancient Capernaum was the home of this prophet; its Hebrew name, Kephar-Nahum, means "village of Nahum." Nineveh was the capital city of Assyria, which had been a threat to Israel since at least 850 B.C. Assyria finally conquered and exiled northern Israel in 722 B.C. and threatened Judah until Sennacherib's forces were divinely decimated at the end of that century (2 Kgs. 19:20-37). Nahum may have lived about 642 B.C. (BD, "Chronology," p. 639). Fulfillment of Nahum's prophecy came a short time after he gave it, about 614 to 606 B.C., when the mother city of Assyria, Asshur, was destroyed; then Nineveh, the capital city, fell; and finally the rest of the empire capitulated to Nabopolassar, king of the rising empire of Babylon (BD, "Chronology," p. 639). Nahum was another poetic prophet; his language is picturesque and full of symbolism. In his first chapter, he wrote a partially acrostic poem to describe both the destructive might and the abundant goodness of the Lord. At the end of it, he foretold the end of wickedness and heralded the era of peace on earth in words similar to Isaiah's (cf. Isa 52:7). In his second chapter, Nahum graphically and dramatically described the invasion and overthrow of Nineveh; and in the third, he told why that destruction was deserved and decreed (BD, "Nahum"). (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament, 665-666.)
