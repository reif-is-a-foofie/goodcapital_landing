Olive oil is referred to in the Hebrew Bible as "the oil of gladness" (Psalms 45:7; Isaiah 61:3; cf. Proverbs 27:9). Well-kept Hebrews, says the book of Ecclesiastes, should always have it on their heads (Ecclesiastes 9:8; cf. Psalm 23:5), for oil, say the Psalms, makes the face "shine" (Psalms 104:15). Indeed, anointing oneself is a good practice even during a fast, according to the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 6:17). It is especially appropriate after a bath and is thus often associated with acts of washing and clothing (2 Samuel 12:20; Ruth 3:3). So, too, a guest is highly honored when his host anoints him before a feast (Psalms 23:5; Luke 7:36-50; esp. 7:46). Of course, only olive oil may be used for anointing; other liquids, such as wine or vinegar, are inappropriate for such use. In Homeric Greece, as well, newly arrived guests at the house of a great king or lord are washed, anointed with olive oil, and clothed before they sit down to the banquet table. In pharaonic Egypt and among the biblical Hebrews, this olive oil was often perfumed (Ruth 3:3). The Romans thought olives warmed the body and protected it against the cold. (John Gee and Daniel C. Peterson in Stephen D. Ricks and John W. Welch, eds., The Allegory of the Olive Tree: The Olive, the Bible, and Jacob 5, p.219)
