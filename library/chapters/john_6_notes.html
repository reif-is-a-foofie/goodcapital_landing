Sea of Galilee.-This, the largest body of fresh water in Palestine, is somewhat pear-shape in outline and measures approximately thirteen miles in extreme length on a northerly-southerly line and between six and seven miles in greatest width. The river Jordan enters it at the northeast extremity and flows out at the south-west; the lake may be regarded, therefore, as a great expansion of the river, though the water-filled depression is about two hundred feet in depth. The outflowing Jordan connects the sea of Galilee with the Dead Sea, the latter a body of intensely saline water, which in its abundance of dissolved salts and in the consequent density of its brine is comparable to the Great Salt Lake in Utah, though the chemical composition of the waters is materially different.

"Jesus Went over the Sea of Galilee Which Is the Sea of Tiberias." (John 6:1.) There are four seas inside or bordering the land of Jesus. Three of them are mentioned in the New Testament. Though well known, the Dead Sea, or the Salt Sea as it was called, is not specifically referred to, nor does it figure in any story. The Red Sea is mentioned in connection with the miracle the Lord performed for the ancient Israelites departing from Egypt. (See Acts 7:36.) The Mediterranean is described simply as "the sea" in the accounts of Jesus visiting Tyre and Sidon (see Luke 6:17) and Peter visiting Simon the tanner in Joppa (see Acts 10:6, 32).

Technically the small body of water, which is less than eight miles wide and about twelve miles long, is a lake, but the Hebrew term yam may be translated as either "lake" or "sea." The Sea of Galilee lies in the Rift Valley nearly seven hundred feet below sea level, making it the lowest freshwater lake in the world. Around its shores, especially to the north and west, most of Jesus' ministry occurred. The rivers included in the narratives of Jesus' life are the Jordan, which runs into and out of the Sea of Galilee, and the Kidron, which is only a seasonal stream where it begins at Jerusalem. No springs are specifically cited. (D. Kelly Ogden, Where Jesus Walked: The Land and Culture of New Testament Times, p.55)

John 7 and 8 Overall Structure a) Jesus comes out of hiding from the Jews seeking to kill him (7:1-36) b) Living water and the Spirit (7:37-44) c) The Jewish leaders fail to arrest Jesus (7:45-52) b) The light of the world and the Father (8:12-20) a) Jesus again hides himself from the Jews seeking to kill him (8:21-59) This structure is taken from Peter Ellis' The Genius of John (Collegeville:Liturgical Press, 1984)

7:1-53. CHRIST AT THE FEAST OF TABERNACLES.

[John 7-8] These chapters contain events about the feast of Tabernacles (Sukkoth, Ingathering: Exodus 23:16; Tents, Booths: Deut 16:13-16), with its symbols of booths (originally built to shelter harvesters), rain (water from Siloam poured on the temple altar), and lights (illumination of the four torches in the Court of the Women). They continue the theme of the replacement of feasts (Passover, John 2:13; 6:4; Hanukkah, John 10:22; Pentecost, John 5:1), here accomplished by Jesus as the Living Water. These chapters comprise seven miscellaneous controversies and dialogues. There is a literary inclusion with Jesus in hiding in John 7:4, 10; 8:59. There are frequent references to attempts on his life: John 7:1, 13, 19, 25, 30, 32, 44; 8:37, 40, 59. (New American Bible)
