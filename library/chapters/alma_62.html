It rebuilt the confidence he had had in the Governor of the Land, which in Moroni had been shattered, and replaced the parts thereof, like those of a cross-word puzzle, into a picture of joy and thanksgiving. Great was Moroni's rejoicing and unlimited the gladness of his heart when he knew that Pahoran had remained faithful, and had not, like many others, "become a traitor to the freedom and cause of his country." The Sacred Record says of Moroni, "His heart did take courage." Some how, someway, he seemed to think, "All is not lost, the Lord God will, as in times past, come to our aid, and will deliver us from our enemies." Moroni's rejoicing was marred only by his sorrow for those who had driven Pahoran from the Judgment-Seat, also for those who for personal reasons and in pursuit of their own happiness had raised the standard of rebellion against "their country and their God." Knowing through Pahoran's epistle that he, the Governor of the Land and its Chief Judge, had been forced to flee Zarahemla, the Nephite Capital, because of threats made against him by the King-Men who sought to depose him, Moroni rushed to his assistance with all possible speed. For him not to have done so, would have imperiled the very existence of the Republic, its freedom and worship. Taking but a few men with him, as Pahoran had suggested, Moroni, leaving the Nephite army in the northeast under the command of Lehi and Teancum, marched to the defense of the Governor who had taken refuge in Gideon. As he advanced towards Gideon he rallied the people along his line of march to the defense of the liberties of the Republic, and in it he was so successful that thousands flocked to his call "and did take up their swords in the defense of their freedom, that they might not come into bondage." In this manner Moroni raised an army of considerable size, continued his march to where the Chief Judge had taken sanctuary, and there joined together his new recruits with Pahoran's veterans, the combined strength of them being greater even than "the men of Pachus, who was the king of those dissenters who had driven the Free-Men out of the Land of Zarahemla and had taken possession of the land." Both Moroni and Pahoran, at the head of their troops, and in the strength of the Lord, moved forward against the captured City, undaunted along the path of duty and loyalty. The King-Men, who supported Pachus, came out to meet them. Man for man we may assume they were equal, but add to the strength of Moroni's men the strength of the Lord, and we immediately see that with the Lord victory does not depend on few or many. In the battle that followed, the King-Men were overthrown, their leader, Pachus, was slain, and the rebellion was completely crushed. Pahoran was restored to his Judgment-Seat, and all those who took part in the uprising were brought to trial according to the laws of the Republic. If any among them, together with those who before time had entered into the same kind of conspiracy, were found guilty, they too were put to death as provided by law. Not only that, but the law took precautionary measures to meet a possible need in the event "whosoever would not take up arms in the defense of their country, but would fight against it, were put to death." The chronicler of these times noted that this was the latter part of the Thirtieth year of the Judges, and that Moroni and Pahoran had brought peace to the Land of Zarahemla. The very idea of a monarchy superseding the Republic which might be had in any Nephite head, was quickly overcome by the law inflicting without delay the death penalty upon anyone who clamored for just such a form of government. In that way, perfidy, treachery, any violation of allegiance, and treason, were stamped out, and a mutual trust took their place. The people of Zarahemla had restored to them a constitutional government, and peace and good-will presaged a season of prosperity and happiness for them. (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.180)

14-17 Moroni and Pahoran with a large body of men took their march towards the Land of Nephihah. Notwithstanding the sudden call for help from Pahoran, to which Moroni was compelled to contribute. He continued to plan the deliverance of Nephihah from the Lamanitish hands, which was the thing he wanted most to do. But to his honor and perspicacity, he put that aside, and brought peace once more to Zarahemla. To do so was to him a diversionary movement, for it confronted his foes with a new problem, the manner of his approach. That diversion proved the strength of his leadership, and now he took up again the consideration of ways and means to liberate the City of Nephihah. In furtherance of the plans Moroni made, together with Pahoran, he left a large company of men to defend Zarahemla, and took with him another corps of troops and marched towards Nephihah "being determined to overthrow the Lamanites in that city." Providence lent a helping hand in their undertaking. As they followed a course that would lead them to

the stricken city, they came across a large company of Lamanitish warriors who, evidently, were out looking for a likely place to plunder and from which they might steal. The army of Moroni's immediately attacked them, slew many, and took their provisions and weapons of war. To the number of 4000, the Nephites also took prisoners; indoctrinated them according to Nephite beliefs, and at their own request, sent them to join their brethren, the People of Ammon in the Land of Jershon. This incident in Moroni's campaign was not an accident. It proves that the Lord is willing to, and does help His people in their difficulties if they keep His commandments. To the Nephite armies He provided new and much needed supplies. We, ourselves, may be strengthened and aided, too, when we remember that in all things, "The accidents of man are the inspirations of God, and the incidents of this life are the leadings and the guidings of Him Who made it." Here we are reminded of the words of the poet concerning the purposes of the Lord; they are applicable in this case: "Out of evil, still educing good." Moroni's march continued, and at length his army reached the plains that lay before the City whose liberation they sought. Moroni and Pahoran encamped here for the night. At no time before had Moroni been better prepared for battle than for the one that was brewing. His soldiers were numerous, and almost impatiently awaited his command. They were well fed, and appeared much rested after the long journey from Zarahemla. They presented an imposing array to the Lamanites as they lined up for the oncoming struggle. But the Lamanites, knowing of the Nephites' valor and their exceeding great courage, together with the vastness of their number, refused to make contact with them. Fearful of the fighting qualities of the Nephites, they remained ensconced behind their fortifications. For them to do this, to say the least, irritated Moroni, who wanted to have a decisive battle fought between them on the plains confronting the City. The day passed, and night enshrouded both the Nephites and the Lamanites with a thick gloom. Now appeared the moment for Moroni to act. He lost no time in doing so, and under the canopy of night's darkness, which obscured all and everything, he climbed to the top of the City's walls to watch secretly the enemies' comings and goings. He wanted, especially, to know where the army of the Lamanites was encamped. Moroni observed everything within his vision. On the east side of the City, near the entrance its walls provided, Moroni found the warriors of Laman sound asleep. He hastened back to his own camp and caused that strong cords and ladders be quickly made by which his soldiers could let themselves down on the inner side of the City's walls. When enough of these devices had been made available for his men, making ingress to the City an artifice which won the battle before it had been fought, Moroni's troops pierced the defenses of the City without even the appearance of a struggle at all. Few at a time, his men helped each other to slide down the barricades until every soldier was in battle position, in line for the fray. The Lamanites happened to be on the east side of the City where they had been encamped; and the army of Moroni on the west where they had scaled the City's walls. When morning came, and the light of day disclosed the Nephites within the City's walls, the Lamanites were astonished and were sore afraid. Quickly hysteria took possession of their vaunted might, and in confusion they fled from the City through the pass close by which they had encamped. Now, when Moroni saw the success with which his stratagem was crowned, immediately ordered his men to follow the fleeing Lamanites. They, without any hesitancy, obeyed his command, and the affrighted soldiers of Ammoron became an easy prey to the inspirited men of the Nephite army who were fighting for the freedom which God had granted to them. The record of those slain of the Lamanites shows that there were many, and the number of prisoners taken by the Nephites was also great. The remainder of Ammoron's troops who were not killed or taken prisoners, rushed precipitantly towards the City of Moroni where they sought asylum and refuge from the Nephites' deadly blows. With the blessings of the Lord which follow righteous leadership, Moroni and Pahoran overcame the Lamanites in the City of Nephihah, and took possession of it without the loss of a single man, notwithstanding the fact that many Lamanites, at the same time, were slain. (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.180)

27-29 Many of the prisoners were desirous to join the people of Ammon. Among the prisoners taken by the Nephites were many Lamanites who desired to unite themselves with the Ammonites of whom they had heard, and with them become a free people. This was a like request as was made by a large number of Lamanites who also were captured by Moroni's army as they, that is, Moroni's army, were marching from Zarahemla to the relief of the City of Nephihah. The prisoners that were captured at both these times were granted like permission to join their former countrymen, and in doing so relieved the Nephites of a "great burthen" (same as burden), "yea," the Sacred Record says, "insomuch that they were relieved from all the prisoners of the Lamanites." Not only were the Nephites freed from the burden of caring for them, or, as we might say, "the burdensome sense of their responsibility," but the permission granted to the Lamanite prisoners by Moroni for them to join the People of Ammon made of them an industrious and prosperous segment of that people, instead of being a small portion of a loathsome and wicked race who delighted in murder and pillage. Again, we repeat the Book of Mormon: "Therefore, all the prisoners of the Lamanites did join the People of Ammon, and did begin to labor exceedingly tilling the ground, raising all manner of grain, and flocks and herds of every kind." After their defeat at Nephihah, the Lamanites began what may be termed a disorderly retreat southward. The battle for Nephihah ended in a decisive victory for Moroni's army. The great number of prisoners that was taken by the Nephites diminished exceedingly the strength of the Lamanites, while at the same time many Nephites who were held as prisoners by the Lamanites were released and joined the forces of Moroni, thus adding many veterans to his rapidly growing army. Moroni, sensing victory, gave the Lamanites no time to repair what proved to be disasterous for them. He followed their debacle, which was rendered more so because the Lamanites again fled from before his approaching army. It was not long before Lehi and Teancum with their army of tried veterans came into action, but as in the case of Moroni's troops, the Lamanites fled before them. From city to city the Lamanites raced to keep ahead of Moroni's men, until at last they all were gathered together near the City of Moroni which was close by the sea. Here was the scene of the final battle of the war. The Lamanites had concentrated into one body of do-or-die soldiers. The Nephites under the able leadership of Moroni, Lehi, and Teancum, pitched their tents in a circle around the bewildered Lamanites, save it was the wilderness side to the south and east. At their head, commanding the soldiers of Ammoron, was Ammoron, himself. Both the

Nephites and their foes were ready for a final and desperate stand, except for the fatigue caused by the previous days marches. The battle loomed up for the morrow. (George Reynolds and Janne M. 35-38 Teancum was exceedingly angry with Ammoron. It was the night before the expected decisive battle, but the Nephite officers and men were too worn by their imposed duties to devise stratagems or to execute them. Teancum, alone did not seek rest. He remembered with intense bitterness all the bloodshed, woes, hardships, famine, etc., that had been brought about in this great and lasting war between the two races, which he rightly attributed to the infamous ambition of Amalickiah and Ammoron. He reflected how he had slain the former, and determined that as he had slain Amalickiah, so should Ammoron fall. In his anger he stole forth into the enemy's camp, let himself over the walls of the city, sought out the King's tent, and when he had found the object of his search, he cast a javelin at him, which pierced the King near the heart. But, unlike Amalickiah, Ammoron's death was not instantaneous. He had time to awaken his servants before he passed away. The alarm was sounded, the guards started in pursuit; Teancum was overtaken in his effort to make a getaway. He was caught and killed. On the morrow, Moroni attacked the Lamanites, defeated them with great slaughter, captured the City of Moroni, and drove the Lamanites entirely out of the Nephite territory. The writer of the Book of Mormon records: "When Lehi and Moroni knew that Teancum was dead they were exceeding sorrowful; for behold, he had been a man who had fought valiantly for his country, yea, a true friend to liberty; and he had suffered very many exceedingly sore afflictions. But behold, he was dead, and had gone the way of all the earth." In that glorious galaxy of patriot-priests, or warrior-prophets, call them which we may, to whose stern integrity, inspired valor, and unflinching virtue the Nephite Republic, in the days of which we write, owed so much of its stability and was so greatly indebted for its perpetuity, Teancum shines among the brightest. View him from whatever point we please, there is no mistaking the man. His ardent disposition, his fiery impetuosity, his zealous patriotism, his undaunted courage, his love of liberty, his entire disinterestedness, shine forth in every action. Indeed, we might almost call him rash, so little did he consider his personal safety when he thought the good of his country required the sacrifice. In picturing the heroes of those days, Teancum looms up before us almost as a Hotspur or Murat. In our mind's eye we can see him charging the solid phalanxes of the Lamanites, rushing at full speed towards the enemy several lengths ahead of his line of battle; his commanding presence inspiring confidence, his unwavering voice ringing out the word of command, his bright armor shining in the sun, and his hair streaming from beneath his helmet, as, regardless of all save the liberties of his country, he falls upon the thickest of the foe, seeking out their chief captains, that by their death an end may possibly be put to the horrors of war. Thus we find him slaying with his own hand, at different times, Morianton, Amalickiah and Ammoron. In fact, it is quite noticeable that in nearly all the great battles of this age, the Nephites appear to have made it a conspicuous part of their policy to slay the commander of the opposing hosts. So fell Amlici, Morianton, Jacob, Coriantumr and others. (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.180)

39-52 The People of Nephi began to prosper again in the land. "Thus ended the Thirty and first year of the Reign of the Judges over the People of Nephi," records the sacred historian who gives us the events of Nephite history in their chronological order. In the next year after the capture of the City of Moroni, peace was established in all the land; not a single Lamanite warrior remained on Nephite soil. Then Pahoran returned to his Judgment-Seat, and Helaman re-commenced his labors in the ministry. The long-continued and savage war just closed had brought various evils to the Church. In many parts of the land it may be said to have been disorganized. The occupancy of so many of the Nephite cities by the unbelieving Lamanites had produced numerous demoralizing effects. Murders, contentions, dissensions, and all manner of iniquity had become rife, and the hearts of many of the people had become hardened. Yet not altogether so, for there were some who acknowledged the hand of the Lord in their afflictions. These humbled themselves in the depths of humility; and because of the prayers of the righteous ones the people were spared. Such was the state of affairs when Helaman went forth to call the people to repentance, and set the Church in order. In this blessed work he had much success, and with the help of his brethren he again established the Church of God throughout all the land. These labors he continued until the time of his death and his joy therein was greatly increased by the faithfulness of the people. They, not withstanding their abundant prosperity, which as ever followed their repentance, remained humble, fervent in prayer, and diligent in well-doing. Such was the happy condition of the Nephite people when Helaman died (57 B.C.), he having survived his illustrious father sixteen years. Mormon makes what is a side comment on what was near the closing scenes in Moroni's life; "And Moroni yielded up the command of his armies into the hands of his son, whose name was Moronihah; and he retired to his own house that he might spend the remainder of his days in peace." (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.180)
