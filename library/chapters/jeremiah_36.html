6Therefore go thou, and read in the roll, which thou hast written from my mouth, the words of the LORD in the ears of the people in the LORD'S house upon the fasting day: and also thou shalt read them in the ears of all Judah that come out of their cities.
Donaldson Commentary

fasting day--(See Jer 36:9). An extraordinary fast, in the ninth month (whereas the fast on the great day of atonement was on the tenth day of the seventh month, Le 16:29; 23:27-32), appointed to avert the impending calamity, when it was feared Nebuchadnezzar, having in the year before (that is, the fourth of Jehoiakim), smitten Pharaoh-necho at Carchemish, would attack Judea, as the ally of Egypt (2Ki 23:34, 35). The fast was likely to be an occasion on which Jeremiah would find the Jews more softened, as well as a larger number of them met together. (Jeremiah Commentary by A. R. FAUSSET)

One morning, after having thought about such things at a choir practice the night before, I was reading the scriptures aloud to myself-aloud, because I didn't want to miss anything. Scripture, I believe, is best appreciated when heard, just as music is. Some few people, not I, can appreciate music by reading it to themselves. But even they must appreciate it more when they hear it. According to one Bible scholar, ancient writers, including biblical writers, designed their material "to be heard," "to be read in public," "to reach the audience through hearing," "to appeal through the ear." He cites as an example Jeremiah, who, unable to go to the house of the Lord himself, commands his scribe Baruch, "Therefore go thou, and read in the roll, which thou hast written from my mouth, the words of the Lord in the ears of the people in the Lord's house upon the fasting day: and also thou shalt read them in the ears of all Judah that come out of their cities." (Jeremiah 36:6.) When Baruch does so, some of the princes get word of it and summon Baruch. "And they said unto him, Sit down now, and read it in our ears. So Baruch read it in their ears. Now it came to pass, when they had heard all the words, they were afraid both one and other, and said unto Baruch, We will surely tell the king of all these words." (Verses 15-16.) They do, and "the king sent Jehudi to fetch the roll. . . . And Jehudi read it in the ears of the king, and in the ears of all the princes which stood beside the king." (Verse 21.) It seems crucial to everyone involved that they hear Jeremiah's words, in their ears. (Dennis and Sandra Packard, Feasting upon the Word, p.199-200)

8And Baruch the son of Neriah did according to all that Jeremiah the prophet commanded him, reading in the book the words of the LORD in the LORD'S house.
Donaldson Commentary

Although the chapter outline shows Baruch's reading of Jeremiah's words in this verse to be distinct from the one in verse 10, it may well be the same reading. The question of two readings hinges on the length of time it took Baruch to record the prophecies of Jeremiah. Verse 9 gives the date of the second (?) reading as the ninth month of the fifth year. The month of the fourth year when Jeremiah was commanded to write is not given, but the recording process could easily have taken them several months. In addition, they were commanded to read it on the "fasting day," so they may have waited until a fast was proclaimed by the king. (Monte S. Nyman, The Words of Jeremiah, p.13)

30Therefore thus saith the LORD of Jehoiakim king of Judah; He shall have none to sit upon the throne of David: and his dead body shall be cast out in the day to the heat, and in the night to the frost.
Donaldson Commentary

Although in verse 29 the Lord mentions an earlier prophecy which the king had questioned, this is the only prophecy that is actually recorded in this chapter. It really consists of three prophecies. First, Jehoiakim is told that no son of his will sit upon the throne of David. Jehoiachin, the son of Jehoiakim, did succeed his father, but he was only eighteen years of age (see 2 Kings 24:8; 2 Chronicles 36:9 says he was only eight, but this is obviously an error for he had children), and he reigned only three months and ten days. Nebuchadnezzar, king of Babylon, had captured his father Jehoiakim and carried him bound into Babylon, later sending for Jehoiachin and appointing Zedekiah as the puppet king in Judah (see 2 Chronicles 36:5-10). Jehoiachin may not have been officially recognized as a king by the Lord or by Jeremiah, and he certainly was not by Nebuchadnezzar. (Monte S. Nyman, The Words of Jeremiah, p.13)
