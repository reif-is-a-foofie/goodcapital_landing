In biblical Hebrew, the repetition of a word, for example, "bare, bare" makes the word comparative ("more bare") or superlative ("most bare"). By repeating "bare" three times in verses 1-3, Isaiah stresses his point-the earth will be extremely bare! Isaiah also uses assonance in verse 1 (boqeq "empty" and boleqah "waste") and alliteration in verse 3 (hiboq-tiboq "empty-empty" and hiboz-tiboz "spoiled-spoled") to further tighten his poetic form. Thus, through structure, sound, and meaning, Isaiah combines many forms of parallelism in just three verses. (Victor L. Ludlow, Isaiah: Prophet, Seer, and Poet, p.241)

Donaldson Commentary

defiled (chanap) "to pollute, profane or make godless

earth also is defiled under the inhabitants. To defile (Hebrew chanap) means "to pollute, profane or make godless." Earth's inhabitants have polluted her by transgressing laws, changing ordinances, and breaking the everlasting covenant. (Donald W. Parry, Jay A. Parry, and Tina M. Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, p.215)

transgressed the laws. People have disregarded the revealed word of God by transgressing the laws given to them by the prophets. Law (Hebrew torah), used here in a rare plural form (Gen. 26:5; Ex. 16:28; Ps. 105:45), indicates that all God's laws have been transgressed; this by itself is enough to bring a curse upon the people (Dan. 9:10-11). (Donald W. Parry, Jay A. Parry, and Tina M. Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, p.215)

changed the ordinance. Ordinance (Hebrew choq) comes from the root word chaqaq, which means "to carve or engrave." God's ordinances were essentially carved in stone and were not meant to be changed; rather, they were to be engraved on the hearts of the children of God. When the ordinances such as baptism (JST Gen. 17:3-6; Moro. 8:14, 16) are changed, their power to save is lost. (Donald W. Parry, Jay A. Parry, and Tina M. Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, p.215)

broken the everlasting covenant. The everlasting covenant is the gospel with its covenants and ordinances. Because the ordinances had been perverted or lost, the Lord revealed for the last time through the Prophet Joseph Smith the everlasting covenant for the earth, "lest all of the earth be smitten with a curse" (D&C 1:12-16, 22; D&C 29:8-10). (Donald W. Parry, Jay A. Parry, and Tina M. Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, p.215-216)

glory begins to break forth through the dark atmosphere of sectarian wickedness, and their iniquity rolls up into view. And they hastily are preparing to act the part allotted them when the Lord rebukes the nations, when he shall rule them with a rod of iron and break them in pieces like a potters vessel. The Lord has declared to his servants some eighteen months since that he was then withdrawing his Spirit from the earth, and we can see that such is the fact. For not only the churches are dwindling away, but there are no conversions, or but very few. And this is not all. And now what remains to be done under circumstances like these? A Hymn of Praise: Triumph over the Wicked (25:1-5)

Those who survive the judgments and the destructions upon the earth, as set forth in Isa. 24, will praise the Lord, perhaps in prayer or song. Isaiah preserves the words of a hymn of praise in these verses. The hymn includes words that bring us great comfort: Jehovah is our "strength" and our "refuge from the storm"; he is our "shadow from the heat." He is always there when we need him, whether for spiritual protection or physical safety. (Donald W. Parry, Jay A. Parry, and Tina M. Peterson, Understanding Isaiah, p.223-224)
