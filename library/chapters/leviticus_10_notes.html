The violation committed by two sons of Aaron and its tragic consequences are best understood in light of other scriptures regarding the perpetual fire to be kept for ritual purposes ( Ex. 27:20-21; Lev. 24:1-4) and the incense to be burned every morning (Ex. 30:7-9). As to just what the sons of Aaron did wrong and why they were so summarily smitten, nothing is known beyond what is stated in the scriptures (consider Lev. 10:8-11). (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament) But Nadab and Abihu were not allowed to accomplish their purpose. The same fire, which a little ago had consumed the accepted sacrifice (Lev. 9:24), now struck them, "and they died before Jehovah," that is, in front of His dwelling-place-most probably in the court (comp. Leviticus 1:5), just as they were about to enter the Holy Place. Thus, on the very day of their consecration to the priesthood, did the oldest sons of Aaron perish, because they had not sanctified the Lord in their hearts, but had offered Him a worship of their own devising, instead of that holy incense consumed by fire from off the altar, which symbolized prayer, offered up on the ground of accepted sacrifice. And this twofold lesson did the Lord Himself teach in explanation of this judgment (10:3). So far as the priesthood was concerned-"I will sanctify Myself in those who stand near to Me, and" (so far as all the people were concerned) "before all the people I will glorify Myself." In other words, if those who had been consecrated to Him would not sanctify Him in heart and life, He would sanctify Himself in them by judgments (comp. also Ezekiel 38:16), and thus

glorify His Name before all, as the Holy One, Who cannot with impunity be provoked to anger. (Edersheim, Alfred, Old Testament Bible History).

Chapter ten of Leviticus records an episode of improper stewardship by two of Aaron's four sons. They offered "strange incense" upon the golden altar in the tabernacle and were struck down by the Lord. Apparently they had been drinking too much wine because the Lord then commanded Aaron, his sons, and the later generations of Levitical priests to drink no wine whatsoever while they were officiating and teaching (see Lev. 10:8-11). At the death of his two sons, Aaron and his remaining sons were commanded to show no outward remorse nor could they participate in the funeral services (see Lev. 10:6, 7). They were also commanded to eat the remainder of the meat sacrifices which were then upon the altar (see Lev. 10:12-15). Aaron and his sons followed these strict commands. (Victor L. Ludlow, "Aaron," Ensign, Feb. 1981, 39)

Q: Why were the children of Israel so immature? A: They had been slaves for 400 years, living without education, at the lowest levels of society. Low esteem. The religion of the Egyptians had a great impact on them. Recall Lev. 10, when Nadab and Abihu were burning apostate incense on the altar of the tabernacle. God sent his fire and destroyed them. Their father, Aaron, was told not to grieve. God didn't want them mourning for wickedness. They couldn't stop ministering in the temple. Again, God is trying to get them to see the difference between what is holy and unholy. This is a law that is designed to get their attention and keep. (Robert Norman, 1989 Old Testament Class Notes)

Leviticus 11, the lord begins to explain to them how to discern between the clean and unclean beasts. What is all of that about? This law is given because they are "immature" and have not proved themselves. God gave them the "schoolmaster" to bring them to Christ, the law of Moses. It is that same as a mother teaching little children how to bake a cake. You give a different set instructions to an 18 year old, than an 8 year old. The younger will need more detailed instruction. That is similar to the Law of Moses. More detail, more steps, more business. All the steps. With Israel at the bottom of the cycle, their punishments have to be harsh, to get their attention, instructions detailed, and they need to be kept busy so they don't sin. Look at it from another standpoint. Celestial Kingdom, how many laws are there? Probably only one. Love God. The spiritually mature don't need a lot of laws. Recall the Council of 50. The Lord told them, "you are my constitution." That was it. We have taken upon ourselves his name, his righteousness, his image. The more mature, the less rules. Ye are my constitution. Who is the WORD? Christ. We model our life after him, we follow him. (Robert Norman, 1989 Old Testament Class Notes)
