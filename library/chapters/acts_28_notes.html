By 1914 the practice had spread throughout the Church of God, however, the actual act of snake handling was only practiced by a small portion of the members (Melton 1996, 636).

1. Who wrote the Pauline epistles?

This may seem like a trivial question at first (like "who is buried in Grant's tomb?" which also isn't trivial), but it really is worth asking. You see, in the ancient world, there was no guaranteed way of ascertaining the authenticity of any written work. (For that matter, we can't always be certain today either.) For any given work that claimed to be the product of someone--let's say Paul--there were several possibilities:

The work was actually written by the famous Paul that everyone has heard of. The work was written by some other Paul, who has since been forgotten, and now everyone just assumes it was by the Paul they've heard of. The work was written by someone who used Paul's name to gain acceptance for his ideas. (Why would a person do this? Some reasons will be discussed below.) The work was written by someone who had no intention of passing off his work as Paul's, but since then someone else thought that the work looked like Paul's, so they attached Paul's name to it. The work was really written by Paul, but has since then been changed in some way--by the addition or subtraction of material, by rearrangement, or both.
