20-30. The second evil spoken of in verse 13, that of following after the false gods of other nations, is further described in these verses. Jehovah reminds Israel of times past when he delivered her out of bondage and she then covenanted to keep his commandments; yet on many subsequent occasions, Israel wandered astray and played the harlot by having an affair with Baal or Baalim, the false gods of the surrounding nations. Baal was worshiped in high places among groves of trees-and this included temple prostitution. Therefore, the description of Israel as a harlot carries a double meaning: a literal act of immorality and a figurative representation of Israel's forsaking her husband (Jehovah) to have an affair with another god. The Lord next declares that Israel had been planted by him as a noble vine, wholly a good seed, and asks why she has become a degenerate and strange vine to him. He further describes her apostasy as an internal or spiritual one. and her uncleanliness as that which cannot be washed away with soap or cleansing agents. Israel's or Judah's pretended impotence is refuted by the Lord. and he compares his wayward people to two familiar animals. The first is a swift dromedary which has gone against her training. A dromedary is a one-humped camel especially bred and trained for riding. Israel is therefore accused of knowingly forsaking the commandments of God. Secondly, the Lord compares her to a wild ass who has learned the way of the wilderness and is free to follow the desires of her heart. He then asks, "In her occasion who can turn her away?" The question seems to be, "Who can take away her freedom?" The King James Version of the Bible marginal note reads "or, reverse it?" The Joseph Smith Translation of the Bible (often referred to as the "Inspired Version") renders the answer, "All they that seek her will weary themselves; in her month they shall not find her" (verse 24; italics added). If she follows her natural instincts for freedom, she will not be caught. Other commentators, following the corrupted text, suggest that "on her occasion" and "in her month" refer to the male animals seeking her out in the time of her periodic breeding seasons, during which time she will be caught. The Joseph Smith Translation of the Bible (often referred to as the "Inspired Version") fits the context of the following verses better. The Lord admonishes the wild ass (Judah) to "withhold thy foot from being unshod, and

thy throat from thirst." In other words, she is invited to maintain her freedom by avoiding those who are seeking to capture her as she satisfies her thirst at the waterhole. However, Israel, or Judah, does not choose to maintain her freedom; rather she has loved strangers (other gods) in the past and now chooses to go after them again. She succumbs to the will of the flesh, the natural man or ways of the world. rather than follow the enticings of the Spirit (see Mosiah 3:19). The Lord declares that as a thief who is caught is ashamed, so will Judah be ashamed for worshiping gods of wood and stone who cannot save her in times of trouble. He further predicts that she will plead with him for deliverance. The Lord concludes with a lament over his attempts in the past to correct Israel, but she has always reiected his prophets and killed them with their own swords. (Monte S. Israel Symbolized by Vineyard and Vines.-The aptness of our Lord's representation of Israel as a vineyard could not have escaped the perception of the Jews, to whom Old Testament similes of analogous form were familiar figures. Notable among others is the striking picture presented by Isaiah (5:1-7), in which the well provided vineyard is shown as producing wild grapes only, for which grievous disappointment of his expectations the owner determined to break down the wall, remove the hedge, and leave the vineyard to its fate of abandonment. The explication of the parable voiced by Isaiah is thus given: "For the vineyard of the Lord of hosts is the house of Israel, and the men of Judah his pleasant plant: and he looked for judgment, but behold oppression; for righteousness, but behold a cry." The worthlessness of a vine save only for its fruit was set forth by the Lord through His prophet Ezekiel (15:2-5); and truly it is so, that the wood of the grape plant is fit for nothing but burning; the whole vine as wood is inferior to a branch from a forest tree (verse 3). And Israel is represented as such a vine, precious if but fruitful, otherwise nothing but fuel and that of poor quality. The psalmist sang of the vine that Jehovah had brought out of Egypt and which, planted with care and hedged about, had flourished even with goodly boughs; but the favor of the Lord had been turned from the vine, and it had been left desolate (Ps. 80:8-16). For further allusions see Isa. 27:2-6; Jer. 2:21; Ezek. 19:10-14; Hosea 10:1. (James E. Talmage, Jesus the Christ: A Study of the Messiah and His Mission According to Holy Scriptures Both Ancient and Modern, p.502)
