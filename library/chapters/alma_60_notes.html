PAHORAN, THE ELDER The third chief judge of the Nephite republic. He succeeded his father, Nephihah, on the judgment seat, B. C. 68, and held that exalted position until his death in B. C. 53, when he was succeeded by his son, Pahoran. The days that Pahoran judged the people were full of tumult and war. No sooner was he placed on the judgment seat than great contentions arose between the royalists and republicans. There was quite a number of the people who desired to restore the monarchy, but they were not so numerous as those who desired to maintain the existing form of government. Being outvoted, the kingmen refused to take up arms against the hosts of the Lamanites under Amalickiah. who were then invading the lands of the Nephites. Full power being given to Moroni, the Nephite general, to deal with these rebels, he marched against them with his forces, and in the conflict that followed 4,000 of the royalists were slain, while many of their leaders were captured and cast into prison. The remainder submitted, joined their countrymen in opposing the Lamanite hosts, and gave full allegiance to the republican government. In the mean-time, the Nephites having been weakened by their internal dissensions, Amalickiah marched triumphantly northward, capturing city after city, along the Atlantic seaboard, while his brother Ammoron conducted a victorious campaign in the southwest. The calamities brought on by this royalist defection lasted many years, for the war was not ended until B. C. 61, and for many years afterwards its evil effects, in the condition of the people, were widely manifest. Even while the war continued, a second royalist uprising occurred. Pachus, the leader, drove Pahoran from the judgment seat and obtained control in Zarahemla. The chief judge retired to Gideon. Shortly afterwards (B. C. 62), Moroni came to the assistance of Pahoran, and their united forces gave battle to the rebels, in which encounter Pachus was slain and his followers defeated. This ended the second attempt to re-establish the kingly form of government during Pahoran's judgeship. After the termination of the war with the Lamanites, every effort was put forth to counteract the demoralization which the long-continued struggle had induced. Helaman, and other leaders in the priesthood, went forth among the people preaching the word and organizing the churches; while Pahoran and his associates gave their attenton to restoring civil order. A few years later (B. C. 55), the colonization of the northern continent began, which was continued with increasing numbers of emigrants during the succeeding years. In B. C. 53, dissension again had place among the Nephites, numbers went over to the Lamanites and stirred up strife. An invasion followed, which was speedily repulsed by the forces of Moronihah, the son of Moroni. The character of Pahoran is best shown in his epistle to Moroni (Alma 61.) He was evidently a wise judge, a faithful servant of God, and a loyal citizen of the republic. He also seems to have possessed the characteristics of patience, mildness and forgiveness to a marked degree, but was not lacking in courage, zeal or determination. Although, not in these words, but Moroni's meaning is clear, he told Pahoran that failure on his part and also that of his adjutants to perform their allotted tasks, was answerable as the primary cause of the miserable showing his armies had made in the campaign both in the east and in the west. In spite of all manner of afflictions, they suffering hunger, thirst, and fatigue, he said that his men had carried on with little or no complaint. On the field of battle, Pahoran knew that affliction was expected and endured, but when suffering was multiplied by negligence at home, the expression of resentment cannot be too forcefully presented. Because of your neglect of our needs, Moroni said, "Great has been the slaughter among our people; yea, thousands have fallen by the sword, while it might otherwise have been if ye had rendered unto our armies sufficient strength and succor for them. Yea, great has been your neglect towards us." The promised men and provisions had not arrived as Moroni expected them to; the reason, Why?, was an enigma which no one in Moroni's camp could explain. The answer to it was inscrutable. Because of their lack many Nephite soldiers were dying by the sword, and others were laid low by fever and famine. Certainly, there must be a reason for all this neglect by the Government. Moroni wanted to know that reason. So did his men. "Yea, we desire to know the cause of your thoughtless state." The rebuke that Moroni administered to Pahoran, as Governor, was almost cruel. It scorned his position and derided his person. Not the position of Governor, for that office was created by the laws, but the way he was filling it; not the body of Pahoran, the Chief Judge, but the manner of his being, suggesting indifference. Moroni queried, "Can you think to sit upon your thrones in a state of thoughtless stupor, while your enemies are spreading the work of death around you? Yea, while they are murdering thousands of your brethren. Yea, even they who have looked up to you for protection, yea, have placed you in a situation that ye might have succored them, yea, ye might have sent armies unto them, to have strengthened them, and have saved thousands of them from falling by the sword." Not only was this neglect confined to manpower, but needed provisions were withheld from the troops

Moroni already had when in fact an abundance filled the storehouses in Zarahemla. But, notwithstanding their scarcity of food, and their need for other supplies, Moroni sought to impress upon Pahoran the loyalty of his soldiers by telling him that sooner than betray the welfare of their country and its freedom, and abandon it to the whims of the enemy, they suffered their life's blood to be shed, and fought the battle of the Lord which was the conflict between good and evil. "And this have they done," said Moroni, "when they were about to perish with hunger, because of your exceeding great neglect towards them." (George Reynolds and Janne M. Although Moroni was a military leader possessing all the qualifications of a good soldier, he was, nevertheless, a man of peace, dedicated to the cause of his Commander-in-Chief, the Prince of Peace. Like Him, Moroni was long-suffering and devoid of hatred. While his understanding was great, he, however, could not fathom the neglect of the Governor of the Land to send him the needed supplies for the army of the Republic; yet, in spite of the tribulation it caused, he gratefully acknowledged Pahoran and his helpers as My beloved Brethren, and concluded deep in his heart that Pahoran was not trifling with the duties of his high office. However, the fact of the matter was that owing to the lack of food and other supplies, many of Moroni's men had fought and bled out their lives in defense of God and their country; and not only that, but the battle-weary troops under his command, despite hunger and fatigue were steadfast and true, and were ready at all times to do battle with the enemy. They, Moroni declared, seeking to lay bare his misgivings about those at home, are united in all things even unto death, that they might preserve the liberty in the which God hath made them free. 22-24 Will ye sit in idleness? Moroni wondered at what he believed was self-complacency as he contemplated the inaction of Pahoran and of his lieutenants. The very thought that they were satisfied with things as they were, enraged him. While many of their brethren were "falling by the sword, yea, wounded and bleeding," tens of thousands of able-bodied men in and about Zarahemla were idling away their time, seeking for pleasure and for that which would satisfy their greed, or in other words, for their own happiness. To Moroni, the very idea was preposterous; they sought for happiness in doing iniquity (Hel. 13:38), but, said the Lord's servant, "Wickedness never was happiness." (Alma 41:10) And to seek happiness in idleness is wickedness. "Thou shalt not idle away thy time," is a commandment of the Lord, and it follows that idleness is a sin. Moroni could no longer bear the restraint of moderation. He became vehement. "Do ye suppose that God will look upon you as guiltless while ye sit still and behold these things?" Moroni again lashed out at Pahoran. There is no question in the minds of the authors hereof that Moroni remembered the words of Isaiah: "Be ye clean that bear the vessels of the Lord,"-meaning, no doubt, uncontaminated by the world; in scriptural language vessels are persons into whom something has been poured; infused; servants of the Lord. The heads of the Government at Zarahemla were supposed to be His servants; His vessels. But sin had unquestionably entered their souls, and filthiness had become their portion. To cleanse from sin, the vessel, itself, must be scoured and scrubbed, not with the unclean waters of iniquity, but by purging everything within them of evil import. Moroni was unrelenting in applying his criticism of Pahoran. He did not mean to censure an innocent man, but his patience was now exhausted, and the evils that had accumulated along with the growing strength of the Lamanites, so frustrated his well-thought-out plans that, at last, nothing save unity at home could bar his determination to weed out the unfaithful and the untrue. "Except ye do repent of that which ye have done, and begin to be up and doing, and send forth food and men unto us, and also unto Helaman... behold it will be expedient that we contend no more with the Lamanites until we have first cleansed our inward vessel, yea, even the great head of our government." The Book of Mormon is replete with parallelisms. The poetic patterns serve, as they do in the Bible, to emphasize messages, define and expand them, make them more memorable, and structure them. One form of parallelism, chiasmus, has been extensively studied, but surprisingly, almost nothing has been written on the abundance of other parallelisms in the Book of Mormon. Here is a review of some other types of parallelisms found in the Book of Mormon. Parallelism of numbers. In Hebrew and other Semitic languages, numbers have no synonyms, with the exception of twenty/score. Equivalents in English like twelve/dozen and fractions like half a hundred/fifty do not exist. Numbers are therefore parallel only when they are repeated or given in multiples-the a fortiori, or "how much more so," effect. The Book of Mormon contains both of these types of parallelisms. Only six examples of a fortiori parallelism exist in the Book of Mormon, all using the number tens of thousands in an exaggerated comparison that heightens the sense of multitude, as in Alma 60:22: Yea, will ye sit in idleness while ye are surrounded with thousands of those, yea, and tens of thousands, who do also sit in idleness? 25-29 It is my God Whom I fear. Aroused to keen or bitter anger because his soldiers in the field, fighting the Lamanites, were continually being neglected by those who had been appointed to send them food and other supplies to maintain an army. Moroni resolved to take into his own hands the matter of needed provisions. That decision was not easy to make, but he saw no other way out of the dilemma. He thought that to warn the Governor of the Land what he intended to do was the best for all, and he in turn could warn those concerned. No more delay was his aim, and his method to achieve it was both bold and brooked no interference. This was his plan: That unless the Government at Zarahemla granted, without hesitation, the terms outlined in his epistle, thereby strengthening and fortifying the armies under his command, Moroni, himself, would lead the greater part of his soldiers against those in authority in Zarahemla and take by force that which was needed for the support of the armies in the field. The remainder of his troops which he called Freemen he said that he would leave, with the blessings of God upon them, to maintain this part of the land. In the strength of the Lord and sustained by His blessings, Moroni testified "that none other power can operate against them." And this, he said, is "because of their exceeding faith, and their patience in their tribulations." Moroni's threat to come in vengeance among the dissident in Zarahemla was bolstered by his conviction that all the inhabitants of that great city were not traitors, and that there still remained "a spark of

freedom" which he could fan into a conflagration which would consume all the rebellious "until those who have desires to usurp power and authority shall become extinct." Now, Moroni insisted, is the time to bestir yourselves in the defence of your country and your little ones, meaning the time is at hand to rouse yourselves to vigorous action in their protection, or await your destruction which will surely come. 30-31 I come unto you and smite you with the sword. Notwithstanding the appearance of evil in their midst, and the idleness of which Moroni accused Pahoran and his aides, Moroni's epistle was, in the opinion of the authors hereof, rather severe. It would have been more concordant to the manner of justice if Moroni had tempered his allegations with sorrow or pity aroused by the distress or misfortune of the Governor. Moroni knew Pahoran; they had labored together for many years, yet, in spite of the integrity Pahoran had exhibited in the past, Moroni was ready, and herewith announced his determination to force, even with the sword, his demands which were based upon his ignorance. We say, ignorance, because Moroni was without firsthand knowledge concerning the real conditions in Zarahemla. On the other hand, if Moroni had used the language of equivocation, he might have deceived Pahoran, and then the very purpose for which he was struggling would have been defeated by his own ambiguity. 32-36 Behold, I am Moroni, your Chief Captain. It is not according to the justice of God to save you from the troubles you bring upon yourselves, or to deliver you in your iniquity, while at the same time deny unto the Lamanites His mercy, when their hatred towards us is caused by their traditions, and too, when "it has been redoubled by those who have dissented from us." The Lamanites, Moroni observed, hated all righteousness because that was the manner of their teachings, while you observe to do wickedly because "of your love for glory and the vain things of the world." Moroni, who was always strict in himself keeping the Lord's commandments, could not bear but that his brethren should do likewise. When they did not, he was quick to rebuke. He could not understand why the Lord continued His lovingkindness to those who knew Him, yet who let pride and worldly things crowd all thoughts of Him from their hearts. Nor could he comprehend why God should surround with His righteousness those who were not steadfast in spirit, or upright in heart. "Ye know that ye do transgress the laws of God," he said to Pahoran, so why should He be your protector? Why should He be an ever present Helper in time of trouble? The Lord will not deliver His people in their sins, but, "Behold the Lord saith unto me: If those whom ye have appointed your governors do not repent of their sins and iniquities, ye shall go up to battle against them." Moroni felt impelled to obey the voice of the Lord, and keep unwavering His commandments, otherwise the covenant he had made when he was a child and thereafter renewed many times, would be as an empty vessel, meaningless, insofar as he was, himself, concerned. Only as a final gesture which the long-suffering of the Lord would favor, Moroni called Pahoran to repentance and thereby cause that bloodshed should no longer be the only recourse of the faithful to the cause of freedom. "Therefore I would that ye should adhere to the word of God, and send speedily unto me of your provisions and of your men, and also to Helaman." Moroni now put the decision of what to do, squarely up to the Governor of the Land. He told Pahoran: "If ye will not do this I come unto you speedily." He gave Pahoran to understand that his (Moroni's) soldiers would not be alone in fighting for their country, for their God and the liberty in which He had made them free, but that with the help of the Lord, Moroni said, their united strength would be as the strength of men who are drilled and marshalled into an army whose leader is the Mighty Jehovah, the Prince of Peace, and no other. We, with Him at our head, he further said, will march against you for He will not suffer "that we should perish with hunger," but "will give us of your food, even if it must be by the sword." Moroni now closed his epistle with a firm avowal of faith in Him Whose glory he alone sought, and the welfare of my country, which together will bring happiness to all the inhabitants of the land. God and Country, united in the service of both is what I seek, and in them find not the honor and power of the

world which honor and power I despise. (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.164-165)
