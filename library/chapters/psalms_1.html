3And he shall be like a tree planted by the rivers of water, that bringeth forth his fruit in his season; his leaf also shall not wither; and whatsoever he doeth shall prosper.
Donaldson Commentary

The blessing to those who trust in the Lord is compared to "a tree planted by the waters" which will always be green and yield fruit. The same promise is in Psalm 1:3 and is promised to latter-day Israel in the land of Zion (see D&C 97:9). (Monte S. Nyman, The Words of Jeremiah, p.58)

We approach the meaning of the atonement in the allegory by consulting scripture for additional references to trees. Scripture abounds with symbolic trees. A tree planted by a river is an Old Testament symbol of a righteous man (Psalm 1:3, Jeremiah 17:8). Isaiah writes, "The Lord hath anointed me to preach good tidings unto the meek; . . . that they might be called trees of righteousness" (Isaiah 61:1, 3). In Daniel's dream the great tree represents a man (Daniel 4:10, 22). Another tree in Isaiah produces a stem (of Jesse), which is Christ (D&C 113:1-2). Two famous trees grow in Eden: the tree of knowledge of good and evil and the tree of life (Genesis 2:9, 17). A millennial tree of life in Revelation 22:2 has leaves to heal the nations, an obvious reference to the Savior. Jesus is hanged on a tree of life (Acts 5:30). In vision Lehi and Nephi see a divine tree that is connected with Jesus' saving ministry (1 Nephi 8:10; 11:8). Lehi's dream tree receives at least three meanings: the Son of God and his divine activity (1 Nephi 11:7); the love of God (1 Nephi 11:22, 25); the tree of life (1 Nephi 11:25; 15:22). Since these meanings all overlap, we would understand that Lehi's dream tree represents multiple facets of Christ. (M. Catherine Thomas in Stephen D. Ricks and John W. Welch, eds., The Allegory of the Olive Tree: The Olive, the Bible, and Jacob 5, p.12)

Third, Jesus equates individual people with the Tree, for by partaking of the fruit of the Tree of Life, or by planting the seed of life in oneself, each disciple grows up into a tree of life, as the Prophet Alma describes (see Alma 32:41-42). Each good tree of life has a place in God's paradise, growing up unto eternal life and yielding much fruit-powerful imagery also present in the Old Testament Psalms (see Psalm 1:1-3) and in the earliest Christian hymns. "Blessed, O Lord, are they who are planted in Thy

land, and who have a place in Thy Paradise; and who grow in the growth of Thy trees" (Odes of Solomon 11:18-24). In other allegories, there is only one tree, Jesus being the root and righteous people becoming the branches (see John 15:1-5; Jacob 5). (John W. Welch, The Sermon at the Temple and the Sermon on the Mount: A Latter-day Saint Approach, p.76)

References to "the wicked" abound in the Hebrew Bible…. Acutely conscious of the darker side of human nature and the variety of human corruption. The Hebrew Bible features several terms to designate those who practice it. The first one used in this psalm is the most severe. "The wicked," [ungodly] in Hebrew resha'im, are time and again the focus of attention, especially in the books of Palms and Proverbs, and in Isaiah and Ezekiel. …While the wicked are motivated by base desires, the actuating cause of these evil deeds is a false theology. A fallacious understanding of the nature of God convinces these reprobates that evil can be perpetrated with impunity. They believe God to be wholly removed from the life of the world and altogether indifferent to human behavior. Hence, the wicked neither put their trust in God nor fear Him… Less villainous than "the wicked" are "the sinners," who, for that reason, might posses greater drawing power for the ordinary person. In Hebrew, they are the hatta'im. The primitive meaning of the stem of this word is "to miss, err, stray." The hote', "the sinner," is, therefore, one who strays from the path or misses the goal; a wayward person… In fact the hote' never attracts the interest of the psalmists… Forming the third category in this cast of dishonorable characters are the "scoffers", in Hebrew letsim. This term is also variously rendered in the English translations by "the scornful," "the mockers," "the cynics," all designations are deserved by these people. Very frequently, the letsim are associated with the foolish, the arrogant, and the wicked. This should not be surprising because in the Bible, folly is less a condition of intellectual inadequacy than a lack of discrimination and judgment in the moral sphere - the inability or unwillingness to foresee or consider the consequences of one's actions. [You will notice in the Hebrew that frequently the verb walking is associated with the wicked, standing is associated with the sinner, and sitting with the scoffer] (Nahum Sarna, Songs of the Heart, 32-35)
