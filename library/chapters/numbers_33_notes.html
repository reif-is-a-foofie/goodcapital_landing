The day 15 Nisan was the day of the exodus of the children of Israel from Egypt. (See Num. 33:3.) The Lord declared it to be an annual holy day in order to remind Israel of its liberation from the bondage of Egypt. (See Ex. 12:17, 42; Ex. 13:3.) It was a day of rejoicing, and even today Passover is practically synonymous with "liberation." (John P. Pratt, "Passover-Was It Symbolic of His Coming?" Ensign, Jan. 1994, 40)

The Division of Canaan (w. 33:50-35:44)

THE COMMAND (vv. 50-56)

Having completed its wilderness trek (chaps. 1-21, 33:1-49), secured its base at the Jordan against all enemies (chaps. 22-25,31), allowed two and a half tribes to settle in Transjordan (chap. 32), and resolved the problem of who will be its leader (27:12-23), Israel can turn its undivided attention to the conquest and apportionment of Canaan. Each pericope begins in an identical manner: God commands Moses to transmit instructions to Israel (33:50; 341,16; 35:1,9). The instructions begin, nearly always, the same way: "When you cross the Jordan to the land of Canaan" (33:51; 34:2; 35:10). The topics of the divine command are sequentially logical: the conquest and apportionment of the land (33:50-56), defined by precise boundaries (34:1-15), under the supervision of designated chieftains (34:16-29), who will also appropriate forty-eight towns for the Levites (35:1-8) and six Levitical towns as asylums for the involuntary homicide (35:9-34). (Jacob Milgrom, The JPS Torah Commentary: Numbers, Philadelphia, 1989, 282)
