Donaldson●●●○○

I conclude with the words of Ezekiel, "And thou, son of man, … though briers and thorns be with thee, and thou dost dwell among scorpions: be not afraid" (Ezekiel 2:6). In our constantly changing world, may we continually cling to those things that do not change: to prayer, to faith, to saving covenants, to love of families, and to brotherhood. By removing the slivers of sin and the thorns of worldly temptation in our lives, and by denying ourselves and taking up our own cross and following the Savior, we can change a crown of thorns to a crown of glory. (James E. Faust, Ensign, May 1991, 70)

Donaldson Commentary●●●○○Cross-ref · Revelation 5:1●●●○○
written within and on the backside. The technical term for this book is an opistographi, which is a scroll that has writing on both sides. Ezekiel's scroll is similarly described: "I looked, . . . and [the scroll] was written within and without: and there was written therein lamentations, and mourning, and woe" (Ezek. 2:9-10). The book in God's right hand overflows with his "revealed will, mysteries, and . . . works" (D&C 77:6). (Donald W. Parry and Jay A. Parry, Understanding the Book of Revelation [Salt Lake City: Deseret Book Co., 1998], 67.)
