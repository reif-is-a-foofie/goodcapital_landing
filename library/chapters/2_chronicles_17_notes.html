Jehoshaphat . . . strengthened himself against Israel --The temper and proceedings of the kings of Israel rendered it necessary for him to prepare vigorous measures of defense on the northern frontier of his kingdom. Donaldson●●●○○

2 Ch 17:7-11. HE SENDS LEVITES TO TEACH IN JUDAH.

Who were "his own" who "received him not"? Were they the Jews in general? Or were they the Qumran community? Jesus fulfilled all of the prophecies that were to be fulfilled by the Messiah except those which would be accomplished at his second coming. It is clear, however, that a great number of people rejected him for not having immediately fulfilled the others also. The main stumbling block to the Essenes would have been their unique reliance on the Aaronic Priesthood, knowing not that the keys of the Melchizedek Priesthood would be restored through the Messiah. And yet, as we have already seen, in the ancient times, especially under kings David and Solomon and the prophet Moses before them, the Melchizedek Priesthood did exist upon the earth and governed the affairs of the Church. The Essenes would have had a tendency to forget that the Aaronic Priesthood is only a division or an incomplete part of the Holy Priesthood after the Order of the Son of God. Had they understood the full meaning of the scriptures, they would have seen how in ancient days the holders of the two types of priesthood worked hand-in-hand. One possible example of this is in 2 Chronicles 17:7-9: Also in the third year of (Jehoshaphat's) reign he sent his princes, even to Ben-hail, and to Obadiah, and to Zechariah, and to Nethaneel, and to Michaiah, to teach in the cities of Judah. And with them he sent Levites, even Shemaiah, and Nethaniah, and Zebadiah, and Asahel, and Shemiramoth, and Jehonathan, and Adonijah, and Tobijah, and Tobadonijah, Levites; and with them Elishama and Jehoram, priests. And they taught in Judah, and had the book of the law of the Lord with them, and went about throughout all the cities of Judah, and taught the people. (John A. Tvedtnes, The Church of the Old Testament, p.72)

Also in the third year of his reign he sent to his princes, . . . to teach in the cities of Judah--The ordinary work of teaching devolved on the priests. But extraordinary commissioners were appointed, probably to ascertain whether the work had been done or neglected. This deputation of five princes, assisted by two priests and nine Levites, was to make a circuit of the towns in Judah. It is the first practical measure we read of as being adopted by any of the kings for the religious instruction of the people. Time and unbroken opportunities were afforded for carrying out fully this excellent plan of home education, for the kingdom enjoyed internal tranquillity as well as freedom for foreign wars. It is conformable to the pious

style of the sacred historian to trace this profound peace to the "fear of the Lord having fallen on all kingdoms of the lands that were round about Judah." (Commentary by ROBERT JAMIESON)
