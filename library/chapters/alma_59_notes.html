1-4 Moroni was exceedingly rejoiced because of the exceeding success which Helaman had had. Moroni received the epistle from Helaman in the early part of the Thirtieth year of the Judges, and upon him reading it he was filled with joy and thanksgiving for he knew that no man without the help of the Lord could accomplish the mighty things that Helaman had brought to an issue of full success. Nor was Moroni alone in his rejoicing, but that all the Nephites in every part of the region round about could also be glad and give thanks to the Lord, he broadcast Helaman's words of triumph everywhere as seed in the hand in sowing. Helaman's success in reconquering the Nephite lands from the Lamanites fired the spirits of Moroni and also of all his military officers. They wanted to do in the eastern theatre of war as Helaman had done in the west. Moroni immediately wrote to Pahoran, the Governor of the land, requesting that men and supplies be forthwith, or as soon as possible, sent to strengthen Helaman's army in order that he might maintain the "land which he had been so miraculously prospered in regaining." (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.155)

5-8 The people of Nephihah were attacked by the Lamanites. Inspired and inspirited by Helaman's success in regaining from the Lamanites the lands and cities of the Nephites situated in the southwestern sector of greater Zarahemla, Moroni, after sending his epistle to Pahoran, began to map out the strategy in which he saw the fulfillment of his desires for the conquered cities along the Atlantic seaboard. Moroni's plan to recover out of the hands of the Lamanites all Nephite territory in the east was rapidly being perfected, when, to his utter astonishment, the Lamanites suddenly attacked the City of Nephihah. That city had become the gathering place for refugees who had fled from the barbarities of the Lamanites in former campaigns, and who now were huddled together for self-defense. There were many "from the City of Moroni and the City of Lehi and the City of Morianton," all met together to protect the liberty which God had given them, and each one seeking to maintain the safety of their wives and children. (See Alma 51:24 ) While this huge throng was assembled in Nephihah, the Lamanites, at Ammoron's command, came forth against them. Utterly disregarding whosoever were women and children, they sought the extermination of all. Relieved of the task of maintaining an army in the west, many of the Lamanites who formerly were attached to a fighting unit there, aligned themselves with their compatriots in the east. Thus, day to day, although numerous in the beginning, they added strength upon strength, and resource upon resource, until at length they overpowered the city's defenses. By sheer weight of numbers the Lamanites soon overwhelmed every obstacle placed in their way, and the City of Nephihah fell to brutal strength and savage glee. (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.155)

9-13 When Moroni saw that the City of Nephihah was lost he was exceeding sorrowful. Amid all his cares and preparations for the struggle before him, Moroni was forced to rely on those in governmental positions to lessen his task by fulfilling their allotted requirements. He had been assured, or at least led to think, that troops to the proper number would be sent to bolster the defenses of Nephihah. But when he learned that none had arrived there, and that because of inadequate manpower the city was lost before help could come, he was "exceeding sorrowful, and began to doubt, because of the wickedness of the people" gathered there. He wondered in his heart whether or not they were reaping the just reward for their transgressions, and if the Lord was allowing them to be destroyed because of them. This sudden failure on their part to maintain possession of their city also troubled the minds of Moroni's commanders, who saw in it the surrender of good to evil. For, they reasoned, undoubtedly, that if the inhabitants of the city had kept the Lord's commandments, He would not have allowed their destruction, but would have saved them as He had many others in the past. Moroni, not knowing the actual conditions of the Government in Zarahemla, could see no reason why it failed to protect the freedom of the Commonwealth. He saw only indifference to its welfare, and the cause thereof he was left to surmise. (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.155)

The loss of the city of Nephihah illustrates the strong correlation between the wickedness of the Nephites and their inability to defeat their enemies in the "strength of the Lord" (see Mosiah 9-10). The leaders of the Nephite armies were often men who "had the spirit of revelation and also prophecy" (3 Nephi 3:19). These righteous military leaders attributed Nephite defeats not to the Lamanites, but to Nephite wickedness. By contrast, faithful Nephites were usually able to defend themselves and recover lost cities, often with relatively minimal loss of life (see Alma 48-50; 52:19; 56:30-57; 57:3-12; 58:1-28; 62:22-26). The Lord has repeatedly taught that while we may face difficulties and serious problems, if we are righteous and rely on the Lord, we can always have confidence that the Lord will be with us and his work will ultimately prevail (see D&C 6:34; 10:69; 33:13).

Cleanse the Inner Vessel Moroni complains to Pahoran of the government's neglect of the armies-The Lord suffers the righteous to be slain-The Nephites must use all of their power and means to deliver themselves from their enemies-Moroni threatens to fight against the government unless help is supplied to his armies (Alma 60 headnote). Helaman had retaken many cities from the Lamanites, but his forces were spread too thin. Moroni had recently lost the city of Nephihah and had seen many Nephites slain. With these things weighing heavily on him, Moroni wrote a letter to Pahoran. Moroni's description of the situation is pretty bleak: And now behold, I say unto you that myself, and also my men, and also Helaman and his men, have suffered exceedingly great sufferings; yea, even hunger, thirst, and fatigue, and all manner of afflictions of every kind. But behold, were this all we had suffered we would not murmur nor complain (Alma 60:3-4). Moroni could handle the hunger, thirst, fatigue, and afflictions. His complaint was about the government's neglect. I Can't Stand All This Sitting Apparently, Moroni pictured Pahoran and the others at the head of the government sitting around in ignorance of all the fighting and dying going on in other parts of the land. Moroni makes it clear in his letter that he didn't like sitting around, and he didn't like thrones. Can you think to sit upon your thrones in a state of thoughtless stupor, while your enemies are spreading the work of death around you? . . .
