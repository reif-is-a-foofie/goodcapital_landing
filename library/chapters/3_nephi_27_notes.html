What's in a name?' isn't just a question of Jewish concern. Shakespeare joins a long list of philosophers, theologians and scholars who been intrigued by the possibility of a link between name and person. It goes without saying that a name is the most distinctive thing about any human being. Our possessions leave us at death, but our names remain attached to us beyond the grave. It is, of course, possible that our unique 'calling card' is just a coincidence. Judaism, however, has decided otherwise. Obviously, the reason God intervenes to this day in the naming process is to give each of us a sense of mission. What We Learn About Translated Beings from the Three Nephites Lee Donaldson, LDS World Columnist

Many years ago I was attending a philosophy of religion class at a secular university. We were discussing the dating of New Testament books. The professor concluded the writings of John were really authored by another person because John could not have lived long enough to write them. A student sitting across the aisle quickly responded that John and Nephite disciples had been translated so time was not an issue. The professor responded by laughing that anyone would believe such ridiculous folklore.

I have had a number of years to reflect on that experience and to ask myself why would the Lord permit John and the three Nephites to tarry on the earth until His Second Coming? The more that I have pondered the Savior's blessing to these four disciples, the more I am convinced that the doctrine of translated beings is another witness for the Lord's hand in this great work.

At their baptism, the Prophet Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdrey were filled with the Holy Ghost and their "minds being now enlightened, we began to have the scriptures laid open to our understandings, and the true meaning and intention of their more mysterious passages revealed unto us in a manner which we never could attain to previously, nor ever before had thought of" (JSH 1:74). This gift of the Spirit led

them to understand "those things which never have been revealed from the foundation of the world, but have been kept hid from the wise and prudent, shall be revealed unto babes and sucklings in this, the dispensation of the fulness of times" (D&C 128:18). The doctrine of translated beings is one of those important doctrines hidden from the world.

It is the Book of Mormon that sheds more light on this doctrine than any other volume of scripture. For instance, the Bible tells us that "Enoch walked with God: and he was not; for God took him" (Genesis 5:24) and Elijah was taken up into heaven (2 Kings 2:11). Yet, outside of Paul's comment that "Enoch was translated that he should not see death; and was not found, because God had translated him" (Hebrews 11:5), the Biblical record is silent on this important doctrine. 3 Nephi, however, clarifies the characteristics and purpose of translated beings.

Attributes of Translated Beings From 3 Nephi 1) They "never taste death" (3 Nephi 28:7) or "endure the pains of death" (3 Nephi 28:8). However, a translated being, like all will still pass through death, The Prophet Joseph Smith explained "Translated bodies cannot enter into rest until they have undergone a change equivalent to death" (Teachings 191). 2) They "live to behold all the doings of the Father unto the children of men" (3 Nephi 28:7). They serve as witnesses to the Lord's dealings with His children. 3) At a future date they will "be changed in the twinkling of an eye from mortality to immortality" (3 Nephi 28:8). Elder Bruce R. Mc Conkie taught that this "change from mortality to immortality, though almost instantaneous, is both a death and a resurrection" (Mc Conkie, Mortal Messiah 4:390). 4) They do not suffer physical pain, but can feel spiritual sorrow (3 Nephi 28:9) Eventually, the Three Nephites "began to sorrow for the sins of the world (4 Nephi 1:44). This sorrow echoes of the sorrow of the Lord with Enoch (Moses 7:28) and His sorrow with the Nephites (3 Nephi 17:14). 5) Those who are translated have their calling and election made sure (3 Nephi 28:10). The three specific promises given to the Nephite Twelve in this verse are perhaps the most salient summary of the blessings of eternal life that is found anywhere in the scriptures. First, they will receive the promise of resurrection, known as a fulness of joy (D&C 93:33-34). Second, by receiving the promising "to sit down in the presence of God", they are promised an inheritance in the Celestial kingdom.
