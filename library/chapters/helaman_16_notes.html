Types of the Second Coming in Third Nephi by Lee Donaldson Sacred scripture both describes actual events and foreshadows future realities. The Savior clearly taught this dual nature of scripture to the Nephites when he commanded them to search the writings of Isaiah, because this ancient prophet "spake as touching all things concerning my people which are of the house of Israel . . . And all things that he spake have been and shall be , even according to the words which he spake " (3 Nephi 23:1-3; emphasis added). This manner of prophesying is called typology. Typology is a divine means to prepare the minds of God's people for the future. It testifies that God's hand is in history and he directs the affairs of his children. Sacred history, therefore, contains specific events which foreshadow the future. This paper will examine the types in the coming of the Savior to the Nephites and their fulfillment, the antitypes, in the millennial advent of the Lord. We can better understand typology if we comprehend the difference between a symbol and a type. The word symbol "was originally a token, in the form of an object broken in twain, so that the identity could be proved by having the two parts match . . . . This story hides within the word, from the Greek symbolos [which is derived from symballein]" (Shipley, 346). Sym means 'together' and ballein 'to put'. Symbol literally means to put together. A symbol is only valuable when it is put together with its meaning. Therefore, symbolism is, in the broadest sense, the putting together of a physical object with its corresponding concept or idea. Milton Terry pointed out that a symbol has three distinct characteristics. "(1) The names of symbols are to be understood literally; (2) the symbols always denote something essentially different from themselves; and (3) some resemblance, more or less minute, is traceable between the symbol and the thing symbolized" (Terry 356). For instance, the bread of the sacrament is a symbol of the Lord's body (3 Nephi 18:1-7). A symbol is designed "to represent some character, office, or quality" (Terry 336). A symbol is not fixed to a historical time. A type, on the other hand, is tied to a specific time in history and it prefigures a future reality. A type is, as the Savior said, something that "has been and shall be" (3 Nephi 23:3). G.R. Osborne points out that "a symbol deals with the spiritual meaning of terms and a type deals with the deeper concept behind a larger event or institution. Thus types cannot be linked with a symbolic or general expression of spiritual concepts" (Osborne 931). Terry has identified five categories of types; people, ordinances, offices, events, and actions (Terry 338,339). For instance, Adam was a type for Christ (Romans 5:14) and Joseph of Egypt was a type for Joseph Smith (2 Nephi 3:15). The Passover foreshadowed the sacrament. The high priest of ancient Israel entered the holy of holies on the day of atonement for the redemption of Israel to typify when Christ would enter into the heavens having atoned for our sins. In the scriptures, the word "type" and related words such as "typify" only appear in the Book of Mormon (2 Nephi 11:4; Mosiah 13:10; Alma 13:16; 25:10, 15; 33:19; 37:45; Ether 13:6) and the Doctrine and Covenants (D&C 76:70). Although, the Greek word for type, tupos, occurs sixteen times in the New Testament (John 14:26; 20:25; Acts 7:43,44; 23:25; Romans 5:14; 6:17; 1 Cor 10:6, 11; Phillipians 3:17; 1 Thes 1:7; 2 Thes 3:9; 1 Tim 4:12; Titus 2:7; Heb 8:5 1 Peter 5:3) it is never translated as type in the King James Version . It is translated as ensample (5 times), print (2), figure (2), example (2), pattern (2), fashion (1),…
