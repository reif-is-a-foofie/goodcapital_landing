After the gathering but before the kingdom of peace shall be established, one last mighty attack will be mounted against the Lord's potential kingdom. The attackers will come from all sides. Gog, "chief prince of Meshech and Tubal," is evidently of the Japhetic peoples of the Caucasus and northward, as are Gomer and the house of Togarmah (Gen. 10:2; BD, "Gog"). Persia comes from the east, Ethiopia the south, and Libya the west; thus, they may represent all nations (Ezek. 38:2-7; Zech. 14:1-3). (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament, 609.)

The battle described here of Gog and Magog against Israel will be "in the latter days" (vs. 8, 16) after the people of Israel have gathered back to their land and "dwelleth safely" (v. 14). In the Doctrine and Covenants the Lord reaffirms that the destructions "spoken by the mouth of Ezekiel the prophet" (29:21) will occur "before this great day [the second coming of Christ in power and great glory] shall come." (D&C 29:14.) A second battle of Gog and Magog against the covenant people of the Lord will occur after the Millennium, according to the Lord as revealed to John the Apostle. (Rev. 20:8.) Daniel H. Ludlow, A Companion to Your Study of the Old Testament, 344.)

Donaldson●●○○○

Gog "mountain" the prince of the land of Magog. The title was probably a common one of the kings of the country, as "Pharaoh" in Egypt. Ezekiel may have used Gog and Magog from the historical fact familiar to men at the time, as ideal titles for the great last anti-Christian confederacy.

Magog Maha, in Sanskrit, means "land."

The Gog and Magog prophecy of Ezek. 38-39 is paralleled by John's vision in Rev. 17-20, part of which speaks about the great "whore," the "mother of harlots" (Rev. 17:1-6, 15-18) which falls (Rev. 18:1-3) and whose "smoke rose up for ever and ever" (Rev. 19:1-3). John wrote of the war of Gog and Magog, wherein the forces of evil are devoured by fire from heaven and the devil who deceived them is "cast into the lake of fire and brimstone" with his followers (Rev. 19:20; 20:7, 14; 21:8). The fire from heaven that destroys the army of Gog and Magog is also mentioned in Ezek. 39:6, while fire and brimstone are in Ezek. 38:22.

Ancient Name Modern Equivalent Notes Magog (v. 2)

The areas north and east of the Black Sea and the Crimea, territories now occupied by Russia or her former satellites

Magog was inhabited anciently by the Scythians, a fierce barbaric people who were masters of swift and brutal warfare. Thus Magog becomes a symbol, or type, suggesting the warlike nature of these people. Meshech (v. 2)

The people of Meshech were anciently called the Mushki. Some linguists think they gave their name to Moscow (Russian Moskva). This area was also in the territory now under the control of the Russia.

Both Meshech and Tubal, like Magog, were descendants of Japheth (see Genesis 10:2), and the three are usually associated. They seem to have had close geographical and other ties.

By association this area is assumed to have been close to Meshech, very likely in the area around the Black Sea in Georgia and Turkey.

Considering that Gog is to be from Magog, Meshech and Tubal lends credence to the idea that Gog is not necessarily one person but may be a nation or group of leaders who act as one. Persia (v. 5)

Iran In Ezekiel's day Persian had not yet become a great world empire, so its territories were not as extensive at this time as they would be later. Ethiopia (v. 5)

The southern part of the continent of Africa In Hebrew Cush usually represented the whole of Africa lying south of Egypt and the north African coastal countries. Libya (v. 5) The northern African countries Libya (Phut in Hebrew) generally meant North Africa except Egypt.

It included the areas now occupied by Libya, Tunisia, Morocco, and Algeria. Gomer (v. 6)

Most of modern Europe and parts of Asia Minor Gomer, another descendant of Japheth (see Genesis 10:2), is thought by most scholars to be the ancestor of the people known by classical historians as the Cimmerians. The Cimmerians were the source of such later peoples as the Theric, Gaelic, Celtic, British, and Teutonic tribes. Togarmah (v. 6)

Asia Minor Togarmah was a son of Gomer (see Genesis 10:3), and the Armenians claim to be his

Nephi writes of the "great and abominable church," while Ezek. 38:7 speaks of Gog's assembly ("they that are assembled unto thee"), which is the meaning of the word church. The tie is somewhat lost in the King James translation, which speaks of "thy company that are assembled unto thee." In the Hebrew text, both the verb and the noun derive from the root qhl, which means "to assemble." Significantly, Nephi notes that the "great church" would be formed "among the nations of the Gentiles" (1 Ne. 13:4).
