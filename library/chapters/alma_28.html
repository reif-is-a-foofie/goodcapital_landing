A time of much fasting and prayer] Fasting was commonly associated with times of grief or sorrow among the ancients. The Psalmist indicated sympathy with his adversaries' sickness by fasting (see Psalms 35:13). Old Testament texts suggest that there was a mourning fast in behalf of the dead (see 1 Samuel 31:13; 2 Samuel 1:12). David, for instance, demonstrated his grief at Abner's death by fasting (see 2 Samuel 3:35). It appears that such a fast was preserved as a funeral custom among the Nephites (see Alma 30:2). (Joseph Fielding Mc Conkie and Robert L. Millet, Doctrinal Commentary on the Book of Mormon, 4 vols., 3:, p.192)

Thus commenced the reign of the judges] After Lehi left Jerusalem, time was reckoned according to the date of his departure-e.g., "fifty years after Lehi left Jerusalem," or "four hundred years had passed away from the time that Lehi left Jerusalem," and so forth. With the establishment of the system of the judges we encounter a new reckoning of time among the Nephites-the "first year of the reign of the judges," the "fifteenth year of the reign of the judges," and so on.

“[Alma 29] is a soliloquy which finds Alma talking to Alma when none but God was near. It is a song of praise in which he exults in God’s goodness and mercy. It is an unselfish prayer for the welfare of each and all. In it Alma expresses the innermost desire of his heart.” (Reynolds and Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, vol. 4, p. 52)

Who is talking in Alma 29?

Alma 29 is a very strange chapter. It is clearly some sort of a personal expression in the first person, but it is not introduced or concluded by any indication of who speaks it. Our first thought, then, might be that it is by Mormon, wishing that he could have the same missionary success as Ammon2 and his brothers had. However, if Mormon is the speaker, then Alma 29:10 explicitly contradicts most of Mormon's writings about himself (see especially Morm. 3:2-3, 12; 5:15-18; 6:22), which indicate that Mormon had no success in his missionary endeavors.

Often the person next thought of is Alma2. This seems likely enough--after all, he wrote the original record that is in the background of Mormon's abridgement. And Alma2 did find success in his own missionary work, which might well be compared to that of the sons of Mosiah2. The only problem with this might be that he calls the sons of Mosiah2 "brethren" (Alma 29:14). If Alma2 is the speaker, this cannot be taken literally; but, then again, a metaphorical use seems permissible. However, we should keep in mind that "brethren" is nearly always literal in the Book of Mormon.

Because of this fact, we should consider one more possibility: what if these are the words of Ammon2? The chief objection to this is that the speaker clearly sees his own efforts as separate from those who were in the land of Nephi (Alma 29:14), while Ammon2 is generally seen as a partner with his brothers in that land. However, this misperceives the facts: Ammon2 never went to the land of Nephi. He had intended to go there with his brothers (Alma 17:8), but instead parted company with them and went to the land of Ishmael (Alma 17:18-19). After going to Middoni to free his brothers, he returns to the land of Ishmael (Alma 21:18), while Aaron3 and his other brothers go to Nephi (Alma 22:1-2). Thus, it makes sense that Ammon2 could have spoken these words, rejoicing in his own success and that of his brothers.

The only difficulty with this conclusion is that Ammon2 has already had his turn at rejoicing, and it was

recorded in Alma 26. Does this cause a problem? Well, first of all, I think we should notice that there are a number of similarities between the two chapters. Besides the general tone and theme, which are very similar, we see expressions of concern lest rejoicing lead to boasting (Alma 26:10-13, 35-36; 29:9) and a strange image of fear that strong emotion could overwhelm the body and lead to death (Alma 26:17-20; 29:16). It seems quite likely, then, that what we have here is an alternate (and more poetic) version of the rejoicing of Ammon2 in Alma 26. Mormon could have put it in as an appendix to his account of the sons of Mosiah2, perhaps thinking that this alternate version he had found may very well be the correct one--it is certainly worth reading. There is plenty of precedent for presenting alternate versions to the reader in this way. (Mike Tueller)
