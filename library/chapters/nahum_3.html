Donaldson Commentary

1-19 According to their own records, many of which have been unearthed and deciphered, as well as the biblical account, the Assyrian armies were violent, guilty of many atrocities. This chapter is a kind of taunt-song, reciting their vices and telling of equivalent violence to be wrought on Nineveh. No-amon and her allies had been overthrown by Assyrians half a century earlier (Nahum 3:8-10, 8), but the time had come that Nineveh's defenders would be as dazed as the drunken. The strongholds would be like fig trees ready for harvest and the people in the midst of Nineveh as vulnerable as women. The gates would be opened and the bars burned. The prophet, in satirical phrases, told them they could prepare for siege but it would be in vain. Like the insects of a plague, her merchants, her crowned heads, and their armies had done their damage and would fly away. Then, in a ten-stich (Hebrew) poetic epitaph, the prophet laid Nineveh's shepherds and nobles to rest, predicting applause for their downfall. Thus ends the burden of Nineveh. Although it is a taunt-song, rejoicing over the vanquishing of an inveterate and violent enemy, this prophetic book teaches some religious truths: The Lord is patient, great, and just; he is good, dependable, and helpful; and the time is coming when all wickedness shall cease and He shall reign in peace (Nahum 1:3, 7, 15). (Ellis T. Rasmussen, A Latter-day Saint Commentary on the Old Testament, 666.)

Donaldson Commentary

HABAKKUK While this book is true prophecy, its method is quite different from other writings of the prophets. Dramatically constructed in the form of dialogue, this book contains the prophet’s complaints (questions) and God’s reply to them. In god’s answers Habakkuk discovers the doorway leading from questioning to affirmation, through which he enters into a faith that enables him to affirm, "I will rejoice in the Lord… God, the Lord, is my strength."

Habakkuk's greatest contribution is in giving the concept that the "just shall live by faith." In some different emphases, Paul, Martin Luther, and the whole of the Protestant Reformation used this same principle as their watchword.

For Habakkuk, it was the answer to his questions. It was the solution to apparent evils and misfortunes and sufferings that the righteous endure. No matter what happens in this world which we cannot understand--things in this world which seem inconsistent with the power and goodness of God--by having faith we provide a keel on our ship which will not let the vessel overturn, even in the most terrible tempest.

The idea above will not solve all the problems one may face in life, but it promotes an attitude of assurance,' strength, and courage to continue and abide in this life and the life to come. The just shall live by faith; that is by faithfulness.

In a strict sense, any attempt to solve the seeming injustices and evils that the righteous suffer is called a "theodicy." The study of Job and Habakkuk are theodicies. They attempt to solve questions such as: Why does God allow the wicked to prosper ? Why do the righteous suffer? Why does God create a world and allow evil and suffering in it? The prophet Habakkuk voiced the anguish of many souls who suffered oppression, injustices, and lawlessness which prevailed during the reign of Jehoiakim (609-598 B. C.). This king brought back conditions as they had been in the days of the wicked King Manasseh.

Habakkuk asked the question of the people of Judah as to why God would use the wicked and proud Chaldean nation as His agent for Judah's punishment. Habakkuk was surely aware of King Josiah's death (609 B. C. ), Egypt's domination of Judah in 609-605 B.C., and the victory of the Chaldean army over Egypt at the Battle of Charchemish (605 B. C. ). After this last mentioned battle, the Chaldeans controlled the lion's share of the earth Messages or Central Themes The Voice..., pp. 368-375. 1. Habakkuk's first question is one of universal and timeless application. The sense of that question might be stated: Why does evil and wickedness continue and seem to prevail over the righteous? Why doesn't God do something about it? This is the question of theodicy. Habakkuk's plea was full of anguish: O Lord, how long shall I cry, and thou wilt not hear ! even cry out unto thee of violence, and thou wilt not save! Why dost thou shew me iniquity, and cause me to behold grievance? for spoiling and violence are before me: and there are that raise up strife and contention. Therefore the law is slacked, and judgment cloth never go forth: for the wicked doth compass about the righteous; therefore wrong judgment proceedeth. (1:2-4) The Lord's answer to Habakkuk's questions about violence and iniquity and injustice was that he was going to punish the wicked. Men will be punished according to their works. But Jehovah informed Habakkuk that he was going to use the great Chaldean (Babylonian) nation as his agent for this punishment (1:5-11). Babylon shall sweep upon Judah and take it captive. But the Babylonians will believe it was their own god and their own power that made them great. They will become proud and will not recognize that Jehovah is the only true God of the whole earth and so they, too, in time will be destroyed and punished. All the prophets taught that Jehovah was the universal God over the earth, as did Habakkuk. It is also almost unanimous in the teachings of the prophets that God does direct the affairs of nations, and has his hand in the direction of history.

God's answer to Habakkuk's first question posed another question in Habakkuk's mind, to which he asked an answer of God. Habakkuk differs from other of the prophets in that his book is mainly a dialogue between himself and the Lord. He inquired of God, he asked, and he had the faith to receive an answer. Habakkuk asked God the questions and then said: "I will stand upon my watch, and set me upon the tower, and will watch to see what he will say unto me . . . " (2: 1 ). Habakkuk's tower (of faith and prayer) is similar to the "enter into thy closet" phrase of Jesus or the "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask . . . " idea in James, of the New Testament. There is a source of strength available to all who feel weakness, but it can only be utilized by faith and prayer. We each must have and use our own tower or closet in order to commune with God. Habakkuk's second question was: why would God use a wicked nation (Babylon) to punish the iniquities of a nation (Judah) who was not as wicked as Babylon. This question has added significance when it is remembered that Jehovah is a moral God, He asked: Thou art of purer eyes than to behold evil, and canst not look on iniquity: Wherefore lookest thou upon them that deal treacherously, and holdest thy tongue when the wicked devoureth the man that is more righteous than he? (1:13) This is a timely question being asked today. Why do the communists increase and swallow up nations less wicked than they? Why doesn't God do something about it? Why did God let Hitler slaughter nearly six million Jews? If God is moral and omnipotent, why did he not dQ something about this? How does a college student solve this problem? In part, it will be solved for the student as it was solved for Habakkuk. 5. Following is the answer of the Lord to Habakkuk, after Habakkuk was upon his tower. It may be the answer for today also; at least it reflects an attitude that will help us: And the Lord answered me and said, "Write the vision [answer] , and make it plain upon tables, that he may run that readeth it [that one may read it easily and quickly] . For the vision [answer] is yet for an appointed time, but at the end it shall speak [it hasteth to the end], and not lie: though it tarry, wait for it; because it will surely come, it will not tarry. Behold, his soul which is lifted up is not upright [shall not abide] in him: but the just shall live by his faith [faithfulness] (2:2-4) (brackets added) The answer was given in these terms: There is an answer to these questions. The answer will not be given now. You must have patience. The answer will definitely be given someday. Meanwhile, the life of the wicked will only be temporary. f . While the life of those who are faithful will continue and be eternal. If one lives a faithful life, he will have the strength to continue--no matter the obstacles, no matter the unsolved problems of life. No problem of life will be so great that one will curse God or blame God for evil, if that person has faith and climbs that tower of faith and prayer. i . It's not the answer, but an attitude that helps one keep going, and helps one to keep loving God. 6. Do we have this kind of faith, that no matter the obstacles or adversities we still trust and joy in God? Said Habakkuk: Although the fig tree shall not blossom, neither shall fruit be in the vines; the labour of the olive shall fail, and the fields shall yield no meat; the flock shall be cut off from the fold, and there shall be no herd in the stalls: Yet I will rejoice in the Lord, I will joy in the God of my Salvation, The Lord God is my strength, and he will make my feet like hinds' feet [sure] and he will make me to walk upon mine high places . . .
