A tower was built of stones, not only to reclaim the rocky soil to more easily facilitate its cultivation, but also to guard and protect the vineyard. It offered an elevated position from which the watchman could see approaching danger (for example, thieves). The vineyard was especially guarded in the harvest season. The tower also served as a temporary dwelling and shelter from sun to avoid heat load and dehydration and was a place of storage for the fresh fruit. Remains of thousands of these towers may still be seen in the hill country of Judaea and Samaria.

“Contrary to what you may suppose, the Lord generally did not answer questions—at least not in the usual way, particularly the questions that came from those who were tempting Him. Generally He did not answer them with a direct answer or an explanation. In fact, He almost always responded by asking a question of those who raised the question in the first place. “Consider the occasion when the tempters asked Him if it was lawful to pay taxes to Caesar. This was a loaded question. If He had answered, ‘No, it is not lawful to pay taxes unto Caesar,’ He would have been guilty of treason and subject to death. If He had answered, ‘Yes, it is legal to pay taxes to Caesar,’ He would have immediately incurred the wrath of the Jews, who had been conquered by the Romans and detested the heavy taxation. Neither a yes nor a no answer was safe. “Notice how the Savior handled the question asked Him by the Pharisees and the Herodians…Notice that He is asking them a question; the initiative is with Him now…That brings us to the point: Who answered the question? Well, of course, His listeners answered their own question after some discussion and teaching. They answered their own question by answering His question.” (Boyd K. From: Doug Stott ANSWERING THE QUESTION OF MARRIAGE IN ETERNITY At one time of another we all get asked the question how we can answer the person who is taught by his or her church that there is no marriage in the resurrection and who cites the Luke 20 scripture to support that claim. Hopefully the following will be of some help in providing a satisfactory answer from the Bible. It will also show the harmony that exists between the Biblical instruction and that found in the revelations given to modern Israel in this dispensation. I. One of the first points to be made is that the Luke scripture covers an incident in which Jesus was speaking to a group of the Jews whom Jesus rightfully accused of failing to have a real testimony of the scriptures. He said, "Ye do err, not knowing the scriptures, nor the power of God." (Mat 22:29.) The Greek word translated "knowing" in this verse is oida which conveys the idea of seeing something clearly, or knowing it from an inner conviction or awareness. Hence the Sadducees neither had a testimony of the teachings of the scripture nor did they therefore have a testimony of the power of God, which on the mortal level was always manifesting through human employment of the powers of the Holy Priesthood. The concept of binding both on earth and in heaven was as ancient as the days of Moses at least, but had the Sadducees comprehended the intrinsic message or philosophical basic behind prophetic accounts, they would have had knowledge of the eternal nature of marriage as God ordained it. II. One cannot take an isolated passage of scripture and ignore all others relating to the same subject and hope to come up with a correct interpretation. That, of course, is just what is done in the sectarian world regarding the Luke 20 scripture, quoted as an argument against marriage in the hereafter. A. Jesus not only reminded the Sadducees that they lacked a testimony of the scriptures, but on another occasion, similarly chided the Pharisees concerning their view regarding marriage. His popularity with the masses had grown to the extent that the Pharisees, those haughty guardians of the Mosaic tradition, had become jealous and sent of their own number a delegation to tempt Jesus into saying something for which they could condemn him. It is important that the things said on this occasion be considered along with the matter of the question posed to the Savior by the Sadducees which we shall examine presently. The Pharisees also came unto him, tempting him, and saying unto him, Is it lawful for a man to put away

his wife for every cause? Rather than make application to the law, which would have been the same as meeting the enemy on his own territory, Jesus turned, rather, to the scriptures themselves. And he answered and said unto them, Have ye not read, that he which made them (the first man and wife----Adam and Eve] at the beginning made them male and female, [as Jesus quoted this he would have used the Old Testament wording "ish visha" which we translate into the English as "man and wife,"] and said, For this cause shall a man leave father and mother, and shall cleave to his wife: and they twain shall be one flesh? Wherefore they are no more twain, but one flesh. What therefore God hath joined together, let not man put asunder (Mat 19:3-6.) III. The next element essential to an understanding of the Savior's statements as to whether there is marriage in the resurrection is that of the Savior's answers on the matter of divorce. The Pharisees, not content to have been cut off from their goal of condemning Jesus as a traitor to the Law, followed up their previous question with one regarding divorce: They say unto him, Why did Moses then command to give a writing of divorcement, and to put her away? (Mat 19:7) Obviously Jesus could not allow the situation to become a contest between Jehovah's right to join man and wife eternally and that of his most notable prophet--Moses, to allow a dissolution of such a bond. Instead, Jesus chose a response that would, in effect, be an accusation of. Israelite infidelity to the Law and at the same time illustrate Moses inspired response to their apostasy He saith unto them, Moses because of the hardness of YOUR hearts suffered YOU to put away your wives: BUT FROM THE BEGINNING [i.e., from the inception of marriage with Adam and Eve) IT WAS NOT SO. And I [this is now the incarnate Jehovah--giver of the Law speaking] say unto you, Whosoever shall put away his wife, except it be for fornication, and shall marry another, committeth adultery: and whoso marrieth her which is put away loth commit adultery. (Mat 19:9-10.) With this response, Jesus wiped aside all of the embellishments to the Law which had grown up since the days of Moses. He restated the Law as it had originally been delivered to Israel's hero prophet. Because the males of Israel had always enjoyed a very wide range of freedom in casting off unwanted wives, these words of the Savior must have been startling even to Jesus' faithful disciples who were listening to these proceedings. No wonder they would respond thus: ...If the case of the man be so with his wife, it is not good to marry. (Mat 19:10.) The Lord's answer to them is heavy with meaning and perhaps should bring to us who are members of the Church today, an awareness of how far away we too are from the true ideal. In other words, Jesus did not retreat one iota from what he had declared to Moses. Divorce was intended to be eternal, and only infidelity on the part of a wife would be considered cause for a divorcement and where such a divorcement took place, the woman remained under the condemnation and he who would marry this woman became a partner in her guilt. If we have had to accommodate divorces in the Church today, is it because the Lord looks with more leniency on divorce today than he did anciently; or because we too are allowed these accommodations because of our spiritual ineptness. Of course the Savior did not ignore the question asked by his disciples as to whether it was good for a man to marry under such strictness as represented the Lord's view. The Lord's response that all could not receive this [the antecedent being the staying of oneself from marriage], but some would have no problem with this since they were born eunuchs, others were made eunuchs of men [by castration], while others [likely because of extended calls to the ministry under conditions of duress] made eunuchs [in mental outlook] of themselves so they could complete their labors. It was of these that Paul seems to have written some of his responses which, unfortunately, have been interpreted as being anti-marriage. He merely responded: ALL MEN CANNOT RECEIVE THIS SAYING, SAVE THEY TO WHOM IT IS GIVEN. (Matt 11.) But to continue, we have on the one side the ideal--the celestial--the Zion or consecrated attitude--the attitude of pure covenant obedience, while on the other is the accommodation allowed the Lord's people whose hearts are not malleable enough to apply the strict rules of the perfect order. Does the latter then become the rightful successor to the former---the rule which shall thenceforth and forever be the

accepted way? To respond affirmatively to that question would be to say that the Lord's people would never qualify for a celestial inheritance, for just as no unclean thing can dwell in God's presence, so too, no one whose heart is hardened against the divine will can inherit such an exalted blessing. That which was first shall finally also be that which is last, for this is a part of the restitution of all things as spoken by the mouths of all the holy prophets since the world began. IV. The next aspect of our consideration of whether marriage was intended by the Lord to be eternal is that of the influence of the levirate marriage system to which Israel had shown an adherence from very early times. The word levirate comes from the Latin levir meaning "husband's brother." In case a husband died without leaving behind a son even by one of his concubines, the ancient custom demanded that his surviving brother should marry the widow in order to preserve the name and inheritance of the dead. Then the first son born of this marriage was counted as the descendant and heir of the dead brother. We find this law in existence among the ancient Amorite peoples, of whom Abraham was one notable example. The full custom placed the responsibility upon the father-in-law to provide for the widow and children of the deceased husband. If he did not wish to give the widow to one of his sons, he must marry her himself. (Pritchard, Ancient Near Eastern Texts, Princeton University Press, New Jersey: 1955, pp182--184 ) Terah married the widow off his son Haran, then married his son Abraham to his stepdaughter Sarah, and his son Nehor to his stepdaughter Milcah. (Abraham 2:2; Gen 20:12.) Much later a descendant of Abraham, Judah, who endeavored to fulfill the levirate law in regards to his daughter-in-law Tamar, forced Tamar to take matters into her own hands when she feared Judah would not carry through completely with his responsibilities toward her. (Gen 38.) There is nothing which would indicate that this law originated with the Lord, but rather grew out of a custom held amongst very ancient oriental peoples. There are, in fact, some instances in the Old Testament where application of the custom seems not to have been strictly followed. Provision was made, for instance to allow inheritances to pass through the females, and then there is the noteable case of Boaz and Ruth, in which Boaz was not the one who according to the custom should have married Ruth. Thus it would appear that the Sadducees posed a question to the Lord regarding a situation which was not only theoretical, but did not even accord with the real beliefs of the Sadducees themselves, since they did not hold with anything which required a belief in the resurrection to make it valid. The scripture so often quoted by our sectarian friends as an argument against marriage following death is as follows: Then came to him certain of the Sadducees, which deny that there is any resurrection; and they asked him, saying, Master, Moses wrote unto us, If any man's brother die, having a wife, and he die without children, that his brother should take his wife, and raise up seed unto his brother. There were therefore seven brethren: and the first took a wife, and died without children. And the second took her to wife, and he died childless. And the third took her; and in like manner the seven also: and they left no children, and died. Last of all the woman died also. Therefore in the resurrection whose wife of them is she? for seven had her to wife. (Luke 20:27 33.) The key to understanding this scripture requires a consideration of all the scriptures examined previously above, but also lies in the response which the Savior gave immediately following this question. The question was asked by Jews who could be said to be really apostate. They were certainly classified amongst those whom the Lord had labeled as hard of heart. In other words, they were children of this world, governed by their carnal natures. They were not Christ's disciples. They did not wish to come in under the covenant and obey its strictures. Thus Jesus responded: The children of this world marry, and are given in marriage: but they [children of this world] which shall be accounted worthy to obtain that world [immortality] and the resurrection from the dead, neither marry, nor are given in marriage: neither can they die any more: for they are equal unto the angels; and are the children of God, being the children of the resurrection.(Luke 20:34--36.) Had any of the marriages referred to by these apostate Sadducees been performed by the power of God's eternal priesthood-the power to bind on earth and have it bound in heaven--there would have been no reason for any arrangement which provided an heir for the deceased, for the deceased would provide his own heirs in eternity with the eternal mate with whom he had contracted his eternal

marriage. No, the marriages of which the Sadducees spoke were temporal and adapted to the same rules as applied to divorce--accommodations for a stiff-necked and hardhearted people who could not receive the fulness by which they might have entered into the Lord's rest and partaken of the fulness of his glory. Nevertheless the questions were posed by those who should have been aware of the truth and the answer was specifically intended to apply to people living under a temporal law by their own choice. V. The last step in our consideration of this question is the manner in which the Lord has responded to this same proposition in our own dispensation, and why he said individuals who opt for marriages which are contracted for mortality only, become as the angels. A part of the great revelation given the Prophet Joseph Smith regarding eternal marriages clearly lays out the intent of the Lord that marriages shall be contracted under the authority of the Holy Priesthood by one sealed unto that authority both for time as well as for eternity. Where this law is fully understood and the opportunity available for complying with this eternal marriage contract and the parties opt to ignore this requirement for permanency of marriage beyond the grave, the Lord has said this: And everything that is in the world, whether it be ordained of men, by thrones, or principalities, or powers, or things of name, whatsoever they may be, that are not by me or by my word, saith the Lord, shall be thrown down, and shall not remain after men are dead, neither in nor after the resurrection, saith the Lord your God. For whatsoever things remain are by me; and whatsoever things are not by me shall he shaken and destroyed. Therefore, if a man marry him a wife in the world, and he marry her not by me nor by my word, and he covenant with her so long as he is in the world and she with him, their covenant and marriage are not of force when they are dead, and when they are out of the world; therefore, they are not bound by any law when they are out of the world. THEREFORE, WHEN THEY ARE OUT OF THE WORLD THEY NEITHER MARRY NOR ARE GIVEN IN MARRIAGE; BUT ARE APPOINTED ANGELS IN HEAVEN, WHICH ANGELS ARE MINISTERING SERVANTS, TO MINISTER FOR THOSE WHO ARE WORTHY OF A FAR MORE AND EXCEEDING, AND AN ETERNAL WEIGHT OF GLORY. FOR THESE ANGELS DID NOT ABIDE MY LAW; THEREFORE, THEY CANNOT BE ENLARGED, BUT REMAIN SEPARATELY AND SINGLY, WITHOUT EXALTATION, IN THEIR SAVED CONDITION, TO ALL ETERNITY; AND FROM HENCEFORTH ARE NOT GODS, BUT ARE ANGELS OF GOD FOREVER AND EVER. (Doctrine & Covenants 132:13-17,) It is significant that in the next few verses following the above text, the Lord made it clear that just because one might use all the right words, and claim he was marrying a couple Eor eternity, if he did not have the authority and priesthood form the Lord, the marriage was still invalid from an eternal standpoint, because the Lord's house is a house of order in which false claims to authority have no recognition. Thus both the apostate Israelites of old and those who practice apostasy in this dispensation lose the blessings even though they pretend to be able to lay claim to a divine inheritance. In conclusion, when a person of one of the sectarian denominations inquires regarding the question put to the Lord by the Sadducees, the short answer. (and of course we need to give more than this so they will understand), is that the question was asked regarding eight specific people who were living under the temporal conditions of the Mosaic Law so-called and did not pertain to all mankind. The Sadducees wished to know which of the seven brothers would have the wife in the resurrection, and the answer was specific: "None of them [the seven], for they [the seven] neither marry nor are given in marriage in the resurrection."
