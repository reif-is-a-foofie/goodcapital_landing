Benjamin's Speech: A Classic Ancient Farewell Address Scholars have recently taken an interest in the similarities that exist in the farewell speeches of many ancient religious and political leaders. Certain themes appear consistently in these farewell addresses, as if the speakers were following a customary pattern. William S. Kurz has published a detailed study, "Luke 22:14-38 and Greco-Roman and Biblical Farewell Addresses," Journal of Biblical Literature 104 (1985): 251-268, comparing twenty-two addresses from the classical and biblical traditions. He has found that in Greco-Roman writings, the dying speaker, usually a philosopher or statesman, was concerned with suicide, the meaning of death, and life after death.

Sixteen elements of the ideal ancient farewell address appear directly and others may be implied. Kurz signals four of his twenty elements as particularly common to addresses in the Old Testament and in the Old Testament Apocrypha: 1) the speaker proposes tasks for successors, 2) reviews theological history, 3) reveals future events, and 4) declares his innocence and fulfillment of his mission. All four of these elements appear directly in the Benjamin account. Furthermore, the emphasis in Benjamin's address, as in the Israelite tradition, is on the relationship of God to man, the speech ending with a covenant renewal.
