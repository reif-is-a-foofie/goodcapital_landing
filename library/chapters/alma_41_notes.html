Liberty and Covenants Lee Donaldson, LDS World Columnist

Anciently, owners of rare gems placed a thin layer of polished metal under their precious treasures to highlight their brilliance. This polished metal was called a foil. Later mirrors were substituted for the polished metal.

A literary foil is a technique where an author highlights the distinctive attributes of characters and situations by contrasting them one with another. Lehi said it best when he declared, "For it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things" (2 Nephi 2:11). The Prophet Mormon teaches many of the greatest lessons of the Book of Mormon by using foils. King Noah's debauchery highlights King Benjamin's righteousness. The impotence of the sword in changing men's hearts highlights the tremendous power of the word reaching men's souls in the book of Alma. The devout prophets Nephi and Lehi are encircled with fire and angels (Helaman 5) while the corrupt Nephites are encircled with devils (Helaman 13:37).

In the book of Alma the wicked Amalickiah is a foil for the Nephite Commander Moroni. In the closing moments of his life, King Mosiah II warns his people of the consequences in following an evil king. "I desire this land to be a land of liberty, and every man may enjoy his rights and privileges" (Mosiah 29:32). Nineteen years after this prophetic warning Amalickiah rises up and desires the kingship over the people (Alma 46:4). Mormon indicates that Amalickiah is a man of flattering words who desires to destroy the church of God and the foundation of liberty (Alma 46:10). Amalickiah's effect on the people, like every other enemy to true liberty, is to lead the people to "do wickedly (see Alma 30:18 and 46:10). This wickedness weakens the foundation of liberty.
