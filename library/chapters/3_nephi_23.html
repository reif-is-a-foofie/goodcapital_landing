The great prophet Isaiah spoke on a multitude of subjects, but two in particular accent his oracles: the Messiah, and the destiny of Israel. Isaiah spoke at great length about the scattering of Israel, how the people of Israel had forsaken the religion of their fathers and thus been forsaken by the Master; and about the gathering of Israel, how in a future day Israel would return to the faith of Israel's youth and once again embrace the religion of Jehovah. (Joseph Fielding Mc Conkie and Robert L. Millet, Doctrinal Commentary on the Book of Mormon, 4 vols., 4:, p.157)

It must needs be that he must speak also to the Gentiles] An angel explained to Nephi that the Bible "is a record which is like unto the engravings which are upon the plates of brass, save there are not so many; nevertheless, they contain the covenants of the Lord, which he hath made unto the house of Israel; wherefore, they are of great worth unto the Gentiles" (1 Nephi 13:23, italics added). This can be viewed from at least a couple of perspectives. If we speak of Gentiles as pure Gentiles, meaning those who are not descended from the twelve tribes of Israel, then the prophetic oracles of Isaiah are of worth to the Gentiles because many of the Gentiles will receive the gospel be adopted into the house of Israel, and thereafter enjoy fellowship with the Saints. Their destiny is thus the same as those who had been born into Israel or who had been members of the Church previously. If we speak of the Gentiles as those who are of Israel (either members of the Church or nonmembers) who line outside the land of Israel, that is, those who are Israelite by descent but Gentile by culture then again the destiny of the Gentiles is inextricably tied to that of Israel. The gospel comes first to the Gentiles in the last days, and from them it goes to the Lamanites and the Jews. Thus members of the Church ("Gentiles" by Nephi's classification-1 Nephi 13:3; 1 Nephi 22:7; see also D&C 109:60) read the holy scriptures, particularly the writings of Isaiah, find meaning and direction, and come to identify clearly with his prophetic pronouncements regarding the house of Israel. In short, Isaiah's words, though directed primarily to Israel, are of everlasting worth to all people, Jew and Gentile alike. (Joseph Fielding Mc Conkie and Robert L. Millet, Doctrinal Commentary on the Book of Mormon, 4 vols., 4:, p.158)

THE MISSING SCRIPTURE

D. Lynn Johnson February 1989 Revised October 1992

During the second day of His visit with the Nephites, the Savior quoted and discussed writings of Isaiah and Micah, and expounded to them all the scriptures they had received.[1] He then stated that there were other scriptures that they should write, which they had not. He asked Nephi to bring forth the records which had been kept. He reminded Nephi that He had commanded Samuel to testify that many would arise from the dead and appear to many at the time of His resurrection, and noted that this had

not been recorded. [2] Accordingly, the missing scripture was added to the record. Years earlier, the prophet Samuel, a Lamanite, was about to return to his own land, having been rejected by the Nephites, when the voice of the Lord directed him to return and to prophesy to the people whatsoever things came into his heart. When they denied him entry into the city, he climbed upon the wall and told them great things concerning the coming of the Savior, including the signs of His birth and death. Another prophet heard what he said, and engraved his message on the gold plates, meticulously placing the words and ideas in the inverse parallel order that pervades all scripture, and has become known as chiasmus. [3] Concerning the signs of the death of the Savior, he wrote [4] (capital letters and indenting have been added to indicate the elements of the chiasm; a more detailed rendition of the structure is shown in the Appendix)

A "But behold, as I said unto you concerning another sign, a sign of his death, behold, in that day that he shall suffer death the sun shall be darkened and refuse to give his light unto you; and also the moon and the stars; and there shall be no light upon the face of this land, even from the time that he shall suffer death, for the space of three days, to the time that he shall rise again from the dead.

B Yea, at the time that he shall yield up the ghost there shall be thunderings and lightnings for the space of many hours,

C and the earth shall shake and tremble; and the rocks which are upon the face of this earth, which are both above the earth and beneath, which ye know at this time are solid, or the more part of it is one solid mass, shall be broken up; yea, they shall be rent in twain, and shall ever after be found in seams and in cracks, and in broken fragments upon the face of the whole earth, yea, both above the earth and beneath. And behold, there shall be great tempests,

D and there shall be many mountains laid low, E like unto a valley, E' and there shall be many places which are now called valleys D' which shall become mountains, whose height is great.

C' And many highways shall be broken up, and many cities shall become desolate.

B' And behold, thus hath the angel spoken unto me; for he said unto me that there should be thunderings and lightnings for the space of many hours.

A' And he said unto me that while the thunder and the lightning lasted, and the tempest, that these things should be, and that darkness should cover the face of the whole earth for the space of three days."

Note the close comparisons between the various pairs of elements. For instance, both A and A' tell of darkness for three days, B and B' mention thunderings and lightnings that would last for many hours. The element labeled C speaks of the great upheaval of the earth and breakup of the rocks both on and beneath the surface of the earth, while C' tells of the effects of this destruction on the highways and cities. This literary form was commonly used by the authors of the Book of Mormon, although it was not discovered there until 1967. [5] The destructions predicted by Samuel came about nearly forty years later, followed by the promised visit of the Savior to the peoples who survived. Nephi faced a problem in adding the missing scripture to the record. Certainly it is not a simple thing to alter what has already been written, particularly when it was painstakingly engraved on gold plates. Probably the writing was dense in order to place as much as possible on the plates. In fact, Moroni states

that they wrote in reformed Egyptian for just that purpose. [6] Apparently Nephi fit it in as best he could, between elements C' and B' of the chiasm, where it now appears as verse 25.

"And many graves shall be opened, and shall yield up many of their dead; and many saints shall appear unto many."

The beautiful symmetry of the carefully crafted historical record had been broken, but for the higher purpose of preserving a more complete account. Moreover, the statement in verse 25 is clearly out of chronological sequence, since it mentions an event that followed the resurrection of Christ before reiterating the many hours of thunder and lightning and the three days of darkness that preceded that event. It is interesting to note that the symmetry can be restored quite easily by switching the places of verses 25 and 26.

A "But behold, as I said unto you concerning another sign, a sign of his death...sun shall be darkened...even from the time that he shall suffer death, for the space of three days,

B to the time that he shall rise again from the dead. C Yea, at the time that he shall yield up the ghost there shall be thunderings and lightnings for the space of many hours,

D and the earth shall shake and tremble... D' And many highways shall be broken up, and many cities shall become desolate.

C' And behold, thus hath the angel spoken unto me; for he said unto me that there should be thunderings and lightnings for the space of many hours.

B' And many graves shall be opened, and shall yield up many of their dead; and many saints shall appear unto many.

A' And he said unto me that while the thunder and the lightning lasted, and the tempest, that these things should be, and that darkness should cover the face of the whole earth for the space of three days."

Both the message and the symmetry are preserved, with the prophecy of the resurrection of many of the dead (B') nicely matched with the statement about the resurrection of the Lord (B). Although this arrangement satisfies the criteria for chiasmus, the following alternative arrangement, placing verse 25 after verse 27, is preferred because it not only restores the symmetry, but it simultaneously preserves the chronology of the death of the Savior and the subsequent resurrection:

Before going on, it would be well to examine certain aspects of the text more closely. The recorded words of the Savior in 23:11 might be interpreted as indicating that the fulfillment of the prophecy, rather than the prophecy itself, had not been recorded, and several have so stated, including Sperry [7] and Mc Conkie, Millett and Top [8]. On the other hand, others have said that the prophecy was missing, including Ludlow [9] and the authors of the Book of Mormon Student Manual published by the Church [10]. Each of these authors has simply stated his opinion matter-of-factly, without discussion or justification. In addition to these, the heading of Chapter 23 for the last several editions of the Book of Mormon states "The words of Samuel the Lamanite concerning the resurrection are added to their records". There is no record in the Book of Mormon of the fulfillment of the prophecy. Of course, perhaps Mormon would have found it unnecessary to mention the fulfillment, given the present dialogue. Since he selected his material to maximize its impact on future generations,[11] what did he gain by bringing the subject up in the present context? Certainly the Savior's concern for completeness and accuracy in the scriptures is taught effectively. This is reinforced immediately afterward when the Savior recites Malachi's writings. Might there be another, more subtle, reason? Could he be teaching us something about the manner of the writing, abridgment, and translation of the Book of Mormon? Consider the following. There are several indications that it was the prophecy that was missing. The Savior's question in verse 9 appears to support this interpretation. If the prophecy had been written in the records he had before him, it is doubtful that he would have reminded them of it. More than likely he would simply have reminded them that they hadn't recorded the fulfillment. Their response, "Yea, Lord, Samuel did prophesy according to thy words...", is consistent with a missing record of the prophecy. Such a statement would not have been called for if they were looking at the written prophecy. There is also the question of timing. Although some have concluded that the Savior's visit came several months after the crucifixion,[12] there are good indications that his visit was immediately after His resurrection. Verse 18 of chapter 10 says that "in the ending of the thirty and fourth year" the people had "great favors shown unto them, and great blessings poured out upon their heads". It also says that "soon after the ascension of Christ into heaven he did truly manifest himself unto them--showing his body unto them...". We read later that he visited with them three days, and than afterward came many times over what could be presumed to be several months.[13] Perhaps the great blessings spoken of as being received at the end of the year were at the culmination of his many visits, rather than at the beginning. The first two verses of chapter 11 strongly imply that the visit was within a day or so of the resurrection. People would hardly be expected to still be "marveling and wondering, and... showing one another the great and marvelous change which had taken place" nearly a year after the event. Would they have had time, a day or two after the three days of darkness and the great disruption of their homes, lives and everything else, to record anything at all? It might be argued that forty years is a long time to remember an unrecorded prophecy, particularly with a change in church leadership (Nephi turned the records over to his son Nephi, and left Zarahemla, about 1 BC[14]). But the younger Nephi would have been well acquainted with the dramatic confrontation between the rebellious Nephites and Samuel, if not actually present, it having taken place just four years before he received the records, and can be expected to have remembered this event, as well as Samuel's prophecies, for the rest of his life. Other of the disciples could well have been of similar age, and

personally acquainted with the facts. And an elderly scribe can be forgiven for forgetting a point, even an important one, in transcribing what transpired upon the city wall. Two aspects of the text in the record of Samuel's prophecy shed further convincing evidence on the question. First of all, as noted above, the statement of the resurrection of the saints is out of chronological order in the reiteration of the events signifying the death of the Savior. It is placed in the midst of the description of the natural violence of that time, rather than after the darkness lifted, which is when it would have occurred. Indeed, Mc Conkie and Millet point out the problem by noting "The rise of many Saints from the graves was not, strictly speaking, a sign of his death, but rather took place at the time of the Lord's resurrection." [15] As previously noted, placing verse 25 after verse 27 would restore the proper order and remove this inconsistency. The second and a most compelling argument lies in the literary structure of verses 2027. While not all the recorded words of Samuel from the city wall may be in chiastic form, substantial portions are. An excerpt is shown in the Appendix. Even a cursory examination reveals that most of this is strongly chiastic, with obvious parallelisms at several levels and no out-of-place elements. In fact, the chiastic forms found so profusely throughout all books of scripture rarely, if ever, show such a blatant break in symmetry and order as is in the passage in question, particularly where it could have been "fixed" so easily, as we see here (by moving verse 25 to immediately follow verse 27). The present author has discovered several hundred chiasms and lesser numbers of other parallel forms in all four of the Standard Works.[16][17][18][19][20] It is extremely rare to find an element out of order, and none other that he has observed could be corrected as easily as this one. Thus it may be concluded that the broken symmetry in these verses is not the product of the original author of the passage, but is the result of a later insertion, at the behest of the Savior, as described above. Assuming this to be so, we gain insight into the writing, abridging and translation of the Book of Mormon from these passages. Among other things, we know that Samuel did not write the record, for he jumped from the wall, fled, and was never seen again among the Nephites.[21] Moreover, he would not have omitted the prophecy. Whether the passage was spoken in chiastic form or not, whoever wrote the account took great pains to write it so, but forgetting the one prophecy. The younger Nephi did not rewrite the account, but simply wrote in the margin or between the lines. He probably was constrained by the availability of space on the plate in the placement of the missing scripture. Even if he understood the literary structure of the passage, he may not have been able to find room to preserve it in the insertion of the missing scripture. Perhaps the most important consequence of this finding is the understanding it provides of how Mormon abridged the records in writing the Book of Mormon. Here he obviously copied the corrected account verbatim into his abridgment for, if he had paraphrased or otherwise written the text himself, he could have balanced the passage quite simply as shown above. Of course, there are other evidences that this was his practice, including wordprint analysis.[22] Finally, it is clear that Joseph Smith followed the original language very closely in his translation, preserving the modified structure. It may be wondered to what extent the original authors of the Book of Mormon, and the compiler, Mormon, were conscious of the chiastic structure that occurs so commonly in that book. More significantly, it is quite likely that the young and unschooled Joseph Smith was completely unaware of the form as he was translating the record. Chiasmus had hardly been noticed by the scholars of the day,[23] and the first detailed discussion of the subject was not published until ten years after his death. [24] The presence of extensive chiasmus helps refute the claim by some that the Book of Mormon is the product of a nineteenth century author, as has already been discussed well. [25] First of all, modern writers hardly use chiasmus at all. Moreover, if a modern writer had stumbled upon chiasmus and used it in writing the book, he would not have written a passage as flawed, and as easily corrected, as the one under consideration. The case of the missing scripture, the broken symmetry caused by its insertion, and the ease with which the symmetry could have been

preserved, add to the refutation of that claim. Finally, the probability of chiastic structure in general, and this instance in particular, occurring by chance is remote in the extreme. If the author had been writing deliberately in chiastic form, he in all probability would not have given the present rendition of the verses in Helaman 14. Later, after the Messiah had quoted two chapters from the prophet Malachi (Malachi 3, Malachi 4; 3 Nephi 24, 3 Nephi 25), he said: "These scriptures which ye had not with you, the Father commanded that I should give unto you; for it was wisdom in him that they should be given unto future generations. And he did expound all things, even from the beginning until the time that he should come in his glory-yea, even all things which should come upon the face of the earth, even until the elements should melt with fervent heat, and the earth should be wrapt together as a scroll, and the heavens and the earth should pass away; and even unto the great and last day, when all people, and all kindreds, and all nations and tongues shall stand before God, to be judged of their works .... And now there cannot be written in this book even a hundredth part of the things which Jesus did truly teach unto the people." (3 Nephi 26:2-4, 6.) In one sense, to say that Jesus "expounded all scriptures in one"may mean that our Lord taught the people the entire plan of salvation out of the scriptures, perhaps even opening the heavens and providing the visions necessary to understand what had been, what was, and what was to come. Such a panoramic vision might not be unlike what was vouchsafed to Enoch (Moses 7), Moses (Moses 1), Nephi (1 Nephi 13-14), the brother of Jared (Ether 3), or Joseph Smith (D&C 76). Of his own experience in vision, Joseph Smith said: "Nothing could be more pleasing to the Saints upon the order of the Kingdom of the Lord, than the light which burst upon the world through the ... vision [D&C 76]. Every law, every commandment, every promise, every truth, and every point touching the destiny of man, from Genesis to Revelation, where the purity of the Scriptures remains unsullied by the folly of men, go to show the perfection of the theory (of different degrees of glory in the future life) and witness the fact that the document is a transcript from the records of the eternal world." (Teachings, p. 11.) On a later occasion, the Prophet explained: "I could explain a hundred fold more than I ever have of the glories of the kingdoms manifested to me in the vision, were I permitted, and were the people prepared to receive them' (Teachings, p. 305). Expounding all the scriptures in one, which truly only the Master, the author of scripture could do, would also demonstrate clearly and unquestionably that all things bear witness of Christ, things in heaven and on earth, things past, things present, and things to come (see Moses 6:63). Such an experience would verily the testimony of the prophets that "none of the prophets have written, nor prophesied, save they have spoken concerning this Christ" (Jacob 7:11; see also Mosiah 13:33; 3 Nephi 20:24). All things before Christ pointed forward to his day and to the centrality of his majestic ministry.
