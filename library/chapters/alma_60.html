Donaldson Commentary

PAHORAN, THE ELDER The third chief judge of the Nephite republic. He succeeded his father, Nephihah, on the judgment seat, B. C. 68, and held that exalted position until his death in B. C. 53, when he was succeeded by his son, Pahoran. The days that Pahoran judged the people were full of tumult and war. No sooner was he placed on the judgment seat than great contentions arose between the royalists and republicans. There was quite a number of the people who desired to restore the monarchy, but they were not so numerous as those who desired to maintain the existing form of government. Being outvoted, the kingmen refused to take up arms against the hosts of the Lamanites under Amalickiah. who were then invading the lands of the Nephites. Full power being given to Moroni, the Nephite general, to deal with these rebels, he marched against them with his forces, and in the conflict that followed 4,000 of the royalists were slain, while many of their leaders were captured and cast into prison. The remainder submitted, joined their countrymen in opposing the Lamanite hosts, and gave full allegiance to the republican government. In the mean-time, the Nephites having been weakened by their internal dissensions, Amalickiah marched triumphantly northward, capturing city after city, along the Atlantic seaboard, while his brother Ammoron conducted a victorious campaign in the southwest. The calamities brought on by this royalist defection lasted many years, for the war was not ended until B. C. 61, and for many years afterwards its evil effects, in the condition of the people, were widely manifest. Even while the war continued, a second royalist uprising occurred. Pachus, the leader, drove Pahoran from the judgment seat and obtained control in Zarahemla. The chief judge retired to Gideon. Shortly afterwards (B. C. 62), Moroni came to the assistance of Pahoran, and their united forces gave battle to the rebels, in which encounter Pachus was slain and his followers defeated. This ended the second attempt to re-establish the kingly form of government during Pahoran's judgeship. After the termination of the war with the Lamanites, every effort was put forth to counteract the demoralization which the long-continued struggle had induced. Helaman, and other leaders in the priesthood, went forth among the people preaching the word and organizing the churches; while Pahoran and his associates gave their attenton to restoring civil order. A few years later (B. C. 55), the colonization of the northern continent began, which was continued with increasing numbers of emigrants during the succeeding years. In B. C. 53, dissension again had place among the Nephites, numbers went over to the Lamanites and stirred up strife. An invasion followed, which was speedily repulsed by the forces of Moronihah, the son of Moroni. The character of Pahoran is best shown in his epistle to Moroni (Alma 61.) He was evidently a wise judge, a faithful servant of God, and a loyal citizen of the republic. He also seems to have possessed the characteristics of patience, mildness and forgiveness to a marked degree, but was not lacking in courage, zeal or determination. (George Reynolds, A Dictionary of the Book of Mormon [Salt Lake City: J. H. Parry,

Moroni wrote again to the Governor of the Land, who was Pahoran. Moroni's righteous wrath because of the Government's non-cooperation in furthering the successful ending of the war, knew no bounds. His anger, kindled almost to a blaze by what he considered unworthy or unjust treatment towards him, was undoubtedly magnified by the distance lying between them; the miles separating them together with what was perhaps an unfamiliar terrain, precluded him from having any first-hand information concerning the difficulties confronting the Governor of the Land, who was Pahoran, the Chief Judge. In consequence thereof, Moroni dispatched a second epistle to Pahoran; it was also addressed to all those "who have been chosen by this people to govern and manage the affairs of this war." Moroni was both abrupt and bold. (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.164)

2-18 I have something to say unto them by the way of condemnation. He reminded Pahoran and his aides-although later it was found by him to have been unnecessary-of the important obligations they had assumed when first they were appointed, and then when they accepted the responsibilities of recruiting and arming an adequate force of men to stem and repel the advances of the Lamanites in any part of the land which they might invade. Although, not in these words, but Moroni's meaning is clear, he told Pahoran that failure on his part and also that of his adjutants to perform their allotted tasks, was answerable as the primary cause of the miserable showing his armies had made in the campaign both in the east and in the west. In spite of all manner of afflictions, they suffering hunger, thirst, and fatigue, he said that his men had carried on with little or no complaint. On the field of battle, Pahoran knew that affliction was expected and endured, but when suffering was multiplied by negligence at home, the expression of resentment cannot be too forcefully presented. Because of your neglect of our needs, Moroni said, "Great has been the slaughter among our people; yea, thousands have fallen by the sword, while it might otherwise have been if ye had rendered unto our armies sufficient strength and succor for them. Yea, great has been your neglect towards us." The promised men and provisions had not arrived as Moroni expected them to; the reason, Why?, was an enigma which no one in Moroni's camp could explain. The answer to it was inscrutable. Because of their lack many Nephite soldiers were dying by the sword, and others were laid low by fever and famine. Certainly, there must be a reason for all this neglect by the Government. Moroni wanted to know that reason. So did his men. "Yea, we desire to know the cause of your thoughtless state." The rebuke that Moroni administered to Pahoran, as Governor, was almost cruel. It scorned his position and derided his person. Not the position of Governor, for that office was created by the laws, but the way he was filling it; not the body of Pahoran, the Chief Judge, but the manner of his being, suggesting indifference. Moroni queried, "Can you think to sit upon your thrones in a state of thoughtless stupor, while your enemies are spreading the work of death around you? Yea, while they are murdering thousands of your brethren. Yea, even they who have looked up to you for protection, yea, have placed you in a situation that ye might have succored them, yea, ye might have sent armies unto them, to have strengthened them, and have saved thousands of them from falling by the sword." Not only was this neglect confined to manpower, but needed provisions were withheld from the troops

Moroni already had when in fact an abundance filled the storehouses in Zarahemla. But, notwithstanding their scarcity of food, and their need for other supplies, Moroni sought to impress upon Pahoran the loyalty of his soldiers by telling him that sooner than betray the welfare of their country and its freedom, and abandon it to the whims of the enemy, they suffered their life's blood to be shed, and fought the battle of the Lord which was the conflict between good and evil. "And this have they done," said Moroni, "when they were about to perish with hunger, because of your exceeding great neglect towards them." (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.164)

10-12 Because of the exceeding goodness of God could ye suppose ye could do nothing, and He would deliver you? Although Moroni was a military leader possessing all the qualifications of a good soldier, he was, nevertheless, a man of peace, dedicated to the cause of his Commander-in-Chief, the Prince of Peace. Like Him, Moroni was long-suffering and devoid of hatred. While his understanding was great, he, however, could not fathom the neglect of the Governor of the Land to send him the needed supplies for the army of the Republic; yet, in spite of the tribulation it caused, he gratefully acknowledged Pahoran and his helpers as My beloved Brethren, and concluded deep in his heart that Pahoran was not trifling with the duties of his high office. However, the fact of the matter was that owing to the lack of food and other supplies, many of Moroni's men had fought and bled out their lives in defense of God and their country; and not only that, but the battle-weary troops under his command, despite hunger and fatigue were steadfast and true, and were ready at all times to do battle with the enemy. They, Moroni declared, seeking to lay bare his misgivings about those at home, are united in all things even unto death, that they might preserve the liberty in the which God hath made them free. If you are guilty of thinking that God, in His exceeding goodness, will deliver you from your troubles without you doing first for yourselves that which lay in your power to do, you have made a serious mistake in thinking so. Your actions are more powerful in prayer than are words. Therefore, "Ye have supposed in vain," and as a reward for your indolence and your neglect of them, "the blood of thousands shall come upon your heads for vengeance; for known unto God were all their cries, and all their sufferings." And too, Moroni continued, if you imagine that because they were wicked so many of your brethren have been slain, the imagination of which you foolishly indulge, I say unto you that it is not so. "There are many who have fallen by the sword," and their blood appeals to Heaven, "and behold it is to your condemnation." Do not think that the righteous who are slain are lost because their dead bodies lie strewn on the field of battle, for, "behold, they do enter into the rest of the Lord their God, Who suffereth the righteous to be slain that His justice and judgment may come upon the wicked." It is an anxious fear that almost overwhelms me, that, by the slothfulness we herewith have endured, the Judgments of God in answer to the cries of our brethren who have been slain, will be visited upon our Government because of its slothful conduct towards them. These, and many other things to dread harassed Moroni as he remembered that in the beginning when some of the Nephites first attacked their own Government, it was at the head thereof that the first signs of rebellion and apostasy from God appeared. If, Moroni boldly informed the beleagured Pahoran, if all of us had been faithful to God and our country "we could have withstood our enemies that they could have gained no power over us." (George Reynolds and Janne M. Sjodahl, Commentary on the Book of Mormon, edited and arranged by Philip C. Reynolds, 7 vols., 5:, p.164)

The Book of Mormon is replete with parallelisms. The poetic patterns serve, as they do in the Bible, to emphasize messages, define and expand them, make them more memorable, and structure them. One form of parallelism, chiasmus, has been extensively studied, but surprisingly, almost nothing has been written on the abundance of other parallelisms in the Book of Mormon. Here is a review of some other types of parallelisms found in the Book of Mormon. Parallelism of numbers. In Hebrew and other Semitic languages, numbers have no synonyms, with the exception of twenty/score. Equivalents in English like twelve/dozen and fractions like half a hundred/fifty do not exist. Numbers are therefore parallel only when they are repeated or given in multiples-the a fortiori, or "how much more so," effect. The Book of Mormon contains both of these types of parallelisms. Only six examples of a fortiori parallelism exist in the Book of Mormon, all using the number tens of thousands in an exaggerated comparison that heightens the sense of multitude, as in Alma 60:22: Yea, will ye sit in idleness while ye are surrounded with thousands of those, yea, and tens of thousands, who do also sit in idleness?
