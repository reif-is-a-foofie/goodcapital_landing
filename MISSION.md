## What We Are Building

A library where a serious reader can start at any verse, any name, any place —
and reach the best connected material in the world on that subject without losing
their thread or their place.

The library gathers:

- **Scripture** — the full LDS canon: Old Testament, New Testament, Book of Mormon,
  Doctrine and Covenants, Pearl of Great Price
- **Commentary** — General Conference talks, apostolic writings, doctrinal expositions
- **Scholarship** — biblical scholarship, ancient texts, early Christian sources,
  Near Eastern studies, Restoration-era documents
- **The noun world** — every person, place, and thing that appears across these
  sources, each with its own profile, connections, and documentary trail

---

## The Standard

Every quote stays attached to the talk or book it came from.
Every cross-reference shows which text made the connection.
Every figure links to their appearances across the whole canon.

The reader should always know:
- What kind of text they are reading
- Where it came from
- What connects to it and why

---

## The Scope

The collection grows outward from the LDS core canon and has no hard boundary.
Any text that illuminates scripture, doctrine, or the history of the Restoration belongs.

The long-term scope is what Joseph Smith described: every true principle, gathered.

## Our Methods

### I. Connection — All Things Point to Christ

> *"And behold, all things have their likeness, and all things are created and made
> to bear record of me, both things which are temporal, and things which are spiritual;
> things which are in the heavens above, and things which are on the earth, and things
> which are in the earth, and things which are under the earth, both above and below:
> all things bear record of me."*
> — Moses 6:63

> *"Behold, my soul delighteth in proving unto my people the truth of the coming of
> Christ; for, for this end hath the law of Moses been given; and all things which have
> been given of God from the beginning of the world, unto man, are the typifying of him."*
> — 2 Nephi 11:4

> *"Search the scriptures; for in them ye think ye have eternal life: and they are they
> which testify of me."*
> — John 5:39

Every link in the library is directional. A person connects to a place connects to an
event connects to a doctrine connects back to Christ. No connection is decorative.
Every edge in the graph should be traceable to a verse, a quote, or a historical record
that establishes it — and when followed far enough, every path arrives at the same center.

**In practice:** when we link Moses to the tabernacle, and the tabernacle to the
Atonement, and the Atonement to Alma 7, and Alma 7 to Christ — we are not making
interpretive leaps. We are exposing a structure that is already there. The method is
to surface it, faithfully, with provenance at every step.

---

### II. Spiritual Arcing — Navigating Between Concepts

> *"One of the tests of discipleship is the degree to which we can handle spiritual
> stitching — seeing how one principle reinforces another, how one dispensation
> echoes the next, how faith and repentance and baptism are not isolated doctrines
> but a single coherent arc."*
> — Neal A. Maxwell

> *"The scriptures are not a collection of isolated verses but a seamless body of
> revealed truth. To read them in fragments is to miss the architecture."*
> — Neal A. Maxwell

> *"All of the ordinances and all of the doctrines of the gospel are woven together
> so perfectly that if you pull on one thread, you find it is connected to every other."*
> — Boyd K. Packer

Spiritual arcing is the ability to move — quickly, faithfully — from one concept to
its neighbors. From a verse in Hebrews on faith to the faith of Nephi in 1 Nephi 3.
From the baptism of Jesus in the Jordan to the symbolism of water in Moses 6. From
Elder Holland's talk on the Atonement to Isaiah 53 to Mosiah 14.

**In practice:** the library must make this movement frictionless. A reader in
John 1 should be one click from the Jordan River profile, one click from John the
Baptist's appearances across the canon, one click from the talks that opened with
that verse. The arc should be navigable at the speed of thought — not the speed of
a search engine.

---

### III. Depth and Breadth — Every Verse on a Subject

> *"The things of God are of deep import; and time, and experience, and careful and
> ponderous and solemn thoughts can only find them out."*
> — Joseph Smith, 1839

> *"Feast upon the words of Christ; for behold, the words of Christ will tell you all
> things what ye should do."*
> — 2 Nephi 32:3

> *"If you want to know what Latter-day Saints believe about any doctrine, look at
> the totality of what the scriptures and living prophets have said — not a single
> verse, not a single talk, but the whole body of revealed truth."*
> — Bruce R. McConkie

The library must support both moves simultaneously:

**Depth** — a reader clicks a verse and can go down into every word, every figure,
every historical context, every commentary, every original language connection.

**Breadth** — a reader asks "what does the whole canon say about faith" and gets
every verse on faith, ranked by centrality, with the figures and places and events
that cluster around that doctrine across every book and dispensation.

**In practice:** topics in the library are not just indexes. Each topic — Faith,
Atonement, Covenant, Resurrection, Dispensation — has its own profile: a concept
description, the canonical verses that define it, the key figures who exemplified it,
and the sermons that taught it best. Depth and breadth are two views of the same
connected structure.

### IV. Everything Has a Story

> *"Remember the worth of souls is great in the sight of God."*
> — Doctrine and Covenants 18:10

> *"These are not myths or metaphors. These are real people. They lived, they wept,
> they struggled, they believed. When you read their words you are reading dispatches
> from real human lives."*
> — Jeffrey R. Holland

> *"And thus we see that by small and simple things are great things brought to pass."*
> — Alma 37:6

Every person in the library is a node in time and space. They were born somewhere.
They stood in particular places. They knew particular people. They acted in a
dispensation that shaped what they could know and what they were called to do.
They point forward to what came after and backward to what made them.

A complete person profile connects:

- **People** — family, contemporaries, teachers, disciples
- **Places** — where they were born, where they ministered, where they died
- **Things** — the objects they carried, built, or received
- **Time** — their dispensation, their era, the arc of history they inhabited
- **Events** — the moments that defined them and that they helped define
- **Their own history** — what they were before their central moment
- **Their own future** — what happened after, what legacy they left, how later
  prophets remembered them

**In practice:** Moses is not just a name that appears in 842 verses. Moses is a
person born in Egypt, raised in Pharaoh's house, called at the burning bush,
connected to Aaron and Miriam and Joshua, appearing on the Mount of Transfiguration
alongside Elijah, appearing again in the Kirtland Temple in 1836, quoted by Nephi
and Alma and Moroni. His story does not end at Deuteronomy. It arcs across every
dispensation. The library must surface that arc.

## The Governing Question

Before building any feature, adding any link, or writing any pipeline:

*Does this help a reader see the connection more clearly — to the source, to the
person, to the doctrine, to Christ?*

If yes: build it, faithfully, with provenance at every step.
